### 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

**Instructions:** Answer the following questions in 30-40 words.

1.  **Why did Gavin Maxwell decide to get an otter after the death of his dog?**
    *   **Model Answer:** After his dog Jonnie died, Maxwell was too sad to consider another dog. However, life without a pet felt lonely and empty. This longing for companionship, coupled with the thought that Camusfearna was suitable for an otter, led him to decide on keeping one.

2.  **Describe the circumstances under which Maxwell's mail arrived in Basra.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Maxwell's mail from Europe was delayed for several days due to various issues. First, his friend's mail arrived but not his. He cabled and tried to telephone, but faced problems like the line being out of order, the exchange being closed for a holiday, and another breakdown. His mail finally arrived five days after his friend left.

3.  **What was Maxwell's first impression of Mijbil when he saw him emerge from the sack?**
    *   **Model Answer:** When Mijbil emerged from the sack, Maxwell thought he resembled "a very small, medievally-conceived, dragon." He was covered in symmetrical pointed scales of mud armour, with soft velvet fur like a chocolate-brown mole visible beneath.

4.  **How did Mijbil behave during his first twenty-four hours in Maxwell's care?**
    *   **Model Answer:** For the first twenty-four hours, Mijbil was neither hostile nor friendly. He was aloof and indifferent, choosing to sleep on the floor as far from Maxwell's bed as possible, showing no particular interest in his new surroundings or owner.

5.  **How did Mijbil's attitude change on the second night and day of his arrival?**
    *   **Model Answer:** On the second night, Mijbil overcame his aloofness and came onto Maxwell's bed, sleeping in the crook of his knees. During the day, he lost his apathy and began to show a keen interest in his surroundings, indicating a shift towards accepting his new environment.

6.  **Describe Mijbil's characteristic behaviour with water.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Mijbil's characteristic behaviour with water was extremely playful and energetic. He went wild with joy, plunging, rolling, and shooting up and down the bathtub. He would spread every drop of water, overturning bowls or sitting in them and splashing until they overflowed, finding static water "provoking."

7.  **What scientific name was given to Maxwell's otter?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The otter, Mijbil, was found to be of a race previously unknown to science. Zoologists later christened him *Lutrogale perspicillata maxwelli*, or Maxwell’s otter, after his owner.

8.  **How did Mijbil escape from Maxwell's bedroom, and what was he trying to do?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Mijbil escaped from Maxwell's bedroom by disappearing around the bend of the corridor leading to the bathroom. Maxwell found him on the end of the bathtub, fumbling at the chromium taps with his paws, attempting to turn on the water.

9.  **What does the phrase "thraldom to otters, an otter fixation" imply about Maxwell's relationship with Mijbil?**
    *   **Model Answer:** This phrase implies that Maxwell developed a very strong attachment and a sense of being completely under the influence or control of otters, specifically Mijbil. It suggests an intense, almost obsessive, devotion and connection to his pet, which he found to be a common experience among otter owners.

10. **What does the author mean by stating that otters find static water "provoking"?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The author means that otters, particularly Mijbil, have an inherent need to interact with and manipulate water. For them, water is meant to be played with, moved, and spread. When water is still or confined, it seems to irritate or challenge them, prompting them to make it dynamic and messy.

### 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

**Instructions:** Answer the following questions in 100-120 words. Provide an outline for the answer.

1.  **Describe the series of events leading up to Maxwell finally receiving Mijbil. What challenges did he face?**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Decision:** Maxwell decided to get an otter after his dog's death due as he felt lonely and Camusfearna was suitable.
        *   **Friend's Suggestion:** A friend casually suggested getting one from the Tigris marshes near Basra, where they were common.
        *   **Journey to Basra:** Maxwell and his friend went to the Consulate-General in Basra to collect mail.
        *   **Mail Delays:** Maxwell's mail was delayed. He faced communication challenges: cabled England, tried to telephone but faced a 24-hour booking requirement, a broken line, a religious holiday, and another breakdown.
        *   **Friend Leaves:** His friend left, and Maxwell arranged to meet him in a week.
        *   **Mijbil's Arrival:** Five days later, his mail arrived. Along with it, two Arabs brought a sack containing a note from his friend: "Here is your otter."

2.  **How does the author describe Mijbil's appearance and initial behaviour upon his arrival? How does this initial aloofness transform into affection?**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Appearance:** When Mijbil emerged, he resembled "a very small, medievally-conceived, dragon," coated in symmetrical mud armour, revealing soft, chocolate-brown velvet fur underneath.
        *   **Initial Behaviour (First 24 hours):** He was aloof and indifferent, neither hostile nor friendly. He chose to sleep on the floor, as far from Maxwell's bed as possible, showing no interest.
        *   **Transformation (Second Night/Day):** On the second night, he came onto Maxwell's bed and slept in the crook of his knees. During the day, he shed his apathy and became keenly interested in his surroundings, indicating a growing comfort and budding affection for his owner and environment.

3.  **Detail Mijbil's interaction with water and how it revealed his unique otter characteristics and intelligence.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Initial Joy:** Mijbil's first encounter with water in the bathroom was marked by wild joy. He plunged, rolled, shot up and down the bathtub, making immense splashes like a hippo.
        *   **Otter Characteristic:** Maxwell learned this was typical of otters – every drop of water must be "extended and spread about." They find static water "provoking."
        *   **Bowl Interaction:** Mijbil would overturn bowls of water or sit in them and splash until they overflowed.
        *   **Intelligence with Taps:** Two days later, he escaped to the bathroom and was seen fumbling with chromium taps. He quickly learned to turn the tap on to produce a trickle, then a full flow, demonstrating remarkable observational learning and intelligence regarding mechanical objects.

4.  **"It is, in effect, a thraldom to otters, an otter fixation..." Discuss this statement in the context of Maxwell's relationship with Mijbil.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Meaning of "Thraldom/Fixation":** Implies a powerful, almost enslaving attachment, a deep and enduring fascination.
        *   **Maxwell's Experience:** After initial aloofness, Maxwell became completely captivated by Mijbil's unique personality, intelligence, and playful antics.
        *   **Shared Experience:** Maxwell notes this "fixation" is shared by most people who have owned otters, suggesting a common, intense bond.
        *   **Impact on Life:** This bond became a central part of Maxwell's life, implying that the otter became more than just a pet; it was a companion that shaped his experiences and daily routine. His description of Mijbil's intelligence and playfulness reinforces this deep connection.

5.  **How does the chapter highlight the responsibility involved in pet ownership, especially for an unusual animal like an otter?**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Pre-reading Activity (SPCA):** The chapter explicitly begins with a note from the SPCA, emphasizing that pet ownership is a "lifetime of commitment" involving "considerable responsibility," requiring careful consideration.
        *   **Maxwell's Preparation:** Maxwell had already considered Camusfearna as suitable ("eminently suitable spot for this experiment"), showing some foresight.
        *   **Mijbil's Needs:** Mijbil's specific needs, like his love for water and his tendency to make a mess, require constant attention and accommodation from Maxwell.
        *   **Unique Challenges:** Owning an otter, a non-domesticated animal, presents unique challenges in terms of understanding its behaviour, providing a suitable environment, and managing its distinct playful nature, highlighting that responsibility extends beyond basic care to adapting to the animal's natural instincts.

### 4. HOTS (Higher Order Thinking Skills) Questions

1.  **The text mentions that otters are "as common as mosquitoes" in the Tigris marshes and "often tamed by the Arabs." Given Mijbil's initial aloofness and eventual attachment to Maxwell, what does this suggest about the difference between 'taming' an animal and forming a 'bond' with it?**
    *   **Hint:** Consider the nature of control vs. mutual affection, and how time and individual interaction play a role. Taming implies external control or habituation; bonding implies an emotional connection and understanding.

2.  **Mijbil's journey from being "aloof and indifferent" to sleeping in the "crook of my knees" and showing "keen interest" is a significant shift. Analyze the psychological aspects of this transformation from Mijbil's perspective. What factors might have contributed to him accepting Maxwell?**
    *   **Hint:** Think about security, consistency, the provision of basic needs, the absence of threat, and eventually, the opportunity for play and interaction. Consider how an animal gradually learns to trust.

3.  **Maxwell describes his attachment to otters as a "thraldom" and "fixation." If the general public often views otters as wild creatures, how might Maxwell's unique experience with Mijbil challenge or reinforce preconceived notions about exotic pets?**
    *   **Hint:** Consider the potential for deep human-animal connection regardless of species, the demanding nature of exotic pet ownership, and the anthropomorphic tendencies of owners. Does it suggest that all wild animals can be pets, or does it highlight the unique compatibility between certain individuals?
