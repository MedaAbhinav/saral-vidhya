## I. His First Flight

### 1. Introduction & Characters

*   **Title:** "His First Flight"
*   **Author:** Liam O'Flaherty (not mentioned in the provided text, but useful context).
*   **Setting:** A ledge on a cliff face, overlooking a vast sea.
*   **Main Character:** A young seagull.
*   **Supporting Characters:** His parents, two brothers, and one sister.

### 2. Plot Summary & Key Events

#### A. The Young Seagull's Fear

*   **Isolation:** The young seagull is alone on his ledge, while his siblings have already flown away the day before.
*   **Panic at the Brink:** He tries to run to the brink of the ledge and flap his wings but is seized by fear.
*   **Reason for Fear:** The immense expanse of the sea below, stretching for miles, makes him believe his wings will not support him.
*   **Retreat:** He bends his head and runs back to his sleeping hole.
*   **Insecurity:** His siblings, whose wings are shorter than his, managed to fly, but he lacks the courage for the "plunge."

#### B. Family's Attempts to Motivate

*   **Parents' Efforts:** His father and mother call to him shrilly, scold him (upbraiding), and threaten him with starvation if he doesn't fly.
*   **Sibling's Progress:** He observes his parents perfecting his brothers and sister in flight, teaching them to skim waves and dive for fish.
*   **Brother's Success:** He witnesses his older brother catch his first herring, and the parents celebrate with a "proud cackle."
*   **Taunting:** The entire family walks about on a nearby plateau, openly mocking him for his cowardice.
*   **Hunger:** Twenty-four hours have passed, and he has not eaten since the previous nightfall, feeling the blazing sun and intense heat.

#### C. The Mother's Ingenious Plan

*   **Ploy for Attention:** The young seagull steps to the brink, stands on one leg, hides the other, and pretends to be falling asleep, hoping to attract attention. His family takes no notice.
*   **Mother's Observation:** Only his mother is looking at him, standing on a hump, breast thrust forward.
*   **The Bait:** She tears a piece of fish, scraps her beak, and this sight maddens the hungry young seagull, who loves to eat this way.
*   **Begging for Food:** He cries out (Ga, ga, ga) begging for food. His mother screams back derisively but then picks up a piece of fish.
*   **The Trap:** She flies towards him with the fish but halts just opposite him, wings motionless, the fish almost within reach.

#### D. The First Flight

*   **Desperation:** Maddened by hunger and surprise, he dives at the fish.
*   **The Fall:** With a loud scream, he falls outwards and downwards into space.
*   **Moment of Terror:** A "monstrous terror" seizes him; his heart stands still. This lasts only a minute.
*   **Instinct Takes Over:** His wings suddenly spread outwards. He feels the wind against his feathers and wings, cutting through the air.
*   **Soaring:** He realizes he is not falling "headlong" but "soaring gradually downwards and outwards."
*   **Fear Conquered:** He is no longer afraid, only a bit dizzy. He flaps his wings and soars upwards.
*   **Family's Reaction:** His mother swoops past him screaming; his father joins; his brothers and sister fly around him, curveting, banking, soaring, and diving.
*   **Joy and Freedom:** He completely forgets his past inability to fly and joins them, diving, soaring, and curving, shrieking shrilly.

#### E. Landing and Triumph

*   **Over the Sea:** He flies near and straight over the ocean, seeing the vast green sea with ridges below. He caws amusedly.
*   **Family's Call:** His family lands on the "green flooring" (the sea) ahead of him, beckoning him.
*   **First Landing Attempt:** He drops his legs to stand on the sea, but they sink. He screams with fright and tries to rise again, flapping.
*   **Exhaustion:** He is too tired and weak from hunger and the new exercise to rise.
*   **Floating:** His feet sink, his belly touches the water, and he sinks no farther – he is floating.
*   **Family's Praise:** His family surrounds him, screaming, praising him, and offering him scraps of dog-fish from their beaks.
*   **Conclusion:** He has successfully made his first flight.

### 3. Themes

*   **Overcoming Fear:** The central theme, showing how the seagull conquers his debilitating fear.
*   **Necessity as a Motivator:** Extreme hunger becomes the ultimate catalyst for his first flight.
*   **Parental Encouragement/Tough Love:** The parents' harshness (upbraiding, threatening starvation) is a deliberate strategy to force their child into independence, essential for survival.
*   **Self-Belief and Instinct:** Despite his doubts, his wings are inherently capable, and instinct takes over when faced with the moment of truth.
*   **Growth and Maturity:** The first flight marks a crucial stage in the young seagull's development towards adulthood and self-sufficiency.

### 4. Vocabulary & Meanings

*   **Ledge:** A narrow horizontal shelf projecting from a wall or cliff.
*   **Brink:** The extreme edge of land before a steep slope or a body of water.
*   **Muster up courage:** To gather or summon courage.
*   **Upbraiding:** Scolding; finding fault with someone.
*   **Shrilly:** In a high-pitched and piercing voice.
*   **Skim:** To move lightly just above a surface.
*   **Herring:** A soft-finned sea fish.
*   **Plateau:** An area of relatively level high ground.
*   **Taunting:** Provoking or challenging someone with insulting remarks.
*   **Ascending:** Moving upwards.
*   **Blazing:** Burning fiercely or shining brightly.
*   **Preening:** (Of a bird) cleaning and tidying its feathers with its beak.
*   **Thrust:** Pushed with force.
*   **Scrapped (beak):** Rubbed something roughly against a surface.
*   **Whet (beak):** To sharpen (here, metaphorically, to sharpen appetite or desire).

### 5. "Thinking about the Text" - Analysis & Discussion

1.  **Why was the young seagull afraid to fly? Do you think all young birds are afraid to make their first flight, or are some birds more timid than others? Do you think a human baby also finds it a challenge to take its first steps?**
    *   **Answer:** The young seagull was afraid to fly because he saw the vast expanse of the sea below and felt certain his small wings would never support him. He lacked confidence in his own abilities.
    *   It is plausible that not all young birds are equally afraid; some might be more timid than others due to individual temperament or slight variations in physical development.
    *   Yes, a human baby often finds taking its first steps a significant challenge. It involves overcoming fear of falling, developing balance, and coordinating movements, which can be daunting.

2.  **“The sight of the food maddened him.” What does this suggest? What compelled the young seagull to finally fly?**
    *   **Answer:** This suggests that the young seagull was suffering from extreme hunger, to the point of desperation. The sight of his mother tearing at the fish and rubbing her beak to whet it intensified his hunger to an unbearable level.
    *   It was this overwhelming hunger, combined with his mother's clever strategy of dangling the fish just out of reach, that finally compelled the young seagull to dive and, consequently, to fly. Necessity became the mother of invention (and flight).

3.  **“They were beckoning to him, calling shrilly.” Why did the seagull’s father and mother threaten him and cajole him to fly?**
    *   **Answer:** The seagull's parents threatened and cajoled him because they understood that flying was a fundamental skill for survival as a seagull. They knew he had to overcome his fear to find his own food and become independent. Their actions, though seemingly harsh, were a form of "tough love" to push him towards necessary growth and self-sufficiency, ensuring he wouldn't starve or remain helpless.

4.  **Have you ever had a similar experience, where your parents encouraged you to do something that you were too scared to try? Discuss this in pairs or groups.**
    *   **Answer (Discussion Point):** This is an open-ended question for personal reflection and discussion. Examples could include learning to swim, ride a bicycle, speak in public, join a new club, or try a difficult academic challenge. The key is to discuss the initial fear, the parental encouragement (which might have been gentle or firm), and the eventual outcome.

5.  **In the case of a bird flying, it seems a natural act, and a foregone conclusion that it should succeed. In the examples you have given in answer to the previous question, was your success guaranteed, or was it important for you to try, regardless of a possibility of failure?**
    *   **Answer (Discussion Point):** While flying is natural for a bird, the *first* flight still involves a leap of faith and overcoming instinctual fear, as seen in the story.
    *   In human experiences, success is rarely guaranteed. It is almost always important to try, regardless of the possibility of failure. Trying helps us learn, develop resilience, discover our capabilities, and ultimately grow. Even if the immediate attempt fails, the effort itself is valuable for building courage and knowledge for future attempts.

## II. The Black Aeroplane

### 1. Introduction & Characters

*   **Title:** "The Black Aeroplane"
*   **Author:** Frederick Forsyth (not mentioned in the provided text, but useful context).
*   **Main Character:** The Narrator (a pilot flying an old Dakota aeroplane).
*   **Other Characters Mentioned:** Paris Control personnel (voice on radio), the mysterious pilot of the black aeroplane.
*   **Setting:** Night sky over France, then within a violent storm.
*   **Time:** 1:30 AM.

### 2. Plot Summary & Key Events (Based on provided text only)

#### A. A Pleasant Start

*   **Return Journey:** The narrator is flying his old Dakota aeroplane (DS 088) from France back to England.
*   **Ideal Conditions:** The moon is rising, stars are shining, and the sky is clear. He is "happy to be alone high up above the sleeping countryside."
*   **Dreaming of Home:** He is dreaming of his holiday and looking forward to being with his family and having a "good big English breakfast."
*   **Contact with Paris Control:** At 1:30 AM, he calls Paris Control. He is instructed to turn "twelve degrees west."
*   **Smooth Flight:** He checks his map and compass, switches to his last fuel tank, and follows instructions. Everything is going well; it's an "easy flight."

#### B. Encountering the Storm

*   **Sudden Change:** Paris is about 150 kilometres behind him when he suddenly sees storm clouds.
*   **Appearance:** They are "huge," looking "like black mountains standing in front of me across the sky."
*   **No Escape:** He realizes he cannot fly up and over them, nor does he have enough fuel to fly around them to the north or south.
*   **The Dilemma:** He considers going back to Paris but is strongly driven by his desire to get home for breakfast.
*   **The Risk:** He decides to take the risk and flies his old Dakota straight into the storm.

#### C. Inside the Storm

*   **Total Darkness:** Inside the clouds, everything becomes "suddenly black," making it impossible to see anything outside the aeroplane.
*   **Turbulence:** The old aeroplane "jumped and twisted in the air."
*   **Instrument Failure:**
    *   The compass goes "dead," spinning "round and round and round."
    *   All other instruments also "suddenly dead."
    *   The radio fails – "Paris Control? Paris Control? Can you hear me?" – no answer.
*   **Lost:** The narrator is completely lost in the storm, without radio, compass, or visibility.

#### D. The Mysterious Helper

*   **Another Aeroplane:** In the black clouds, quite near him, he sees another aeroplane.
*   **Distinctive Feature:** It has "no lights on its wings."
*   **The Pilot:** He can see the pilot's face, turned towards him.
*   **Guidance:** The other pilot lifts a hand and waves, silently instructing, "Follow me."
*   **Relief:** The narrator is "very glad to see another person," believing the pilot knows he is lost and is trying to help.
*   **Following:** The mysterious pilot turns his aeroplane slowly to the north, in front of the Dakota, making it easier to follow. The narrator follows "like an obedient child."
*   **Continuing Journey:** He follows the strange black aeroplane for "half an hour."

### 5. "Thinking about the Text" - Analysis & Discussion (Based on provided text only)

1.  **“I’ll take the risk.” What is the risk? Why does the narrator take it?**
    *   **Answer:** The risk is flying his old Dakota aeroplane directly into the huge, black, mountain-like storm clouds, despite knowing he doesn't have enough fuel to fly around them and cannot fly over them.
    *   The narrator takes this risk because he is desperate to get home to England and enjoy his "good big English breakfast" with his family. His strong desire to be home outweighs his caution.

2.  **Describe the narrator’s experience as he flew the aeroplane into the storm.**
    *   **Answer:** As the narrator flew into the storm, everything suddenly turned black, making it impossible to see outside the aeroplane. The old Dakota jumped and twisted violently in the air. All his instruments failed: the compass spun uselessly and died, and the radio stopped working, leaving him completely lost and unable to contact Paris Control.

3.  **Why did the narrator of the Dakota aeroplane follow the strange aeroplane?**
    *   **Answer:** The narrator followed the strange aeroplane out of desperation and a glimmer of hope. He was completely lost, his instruments were dead, and he had no radio. Seeing another pilot in the storm, who explicitly signalled "Follow me," was his only chance of finding a way out of the perilous situation. He trusted the unknown pilot implicitly, following "like an obedient child."
