# STUDY GUIDE: NCERT Class 10 English – "Two Stories about Flying"

## 1) Chapter Overview

"Two Stories about Flying" is a chapter comprising two distinct yet thematically linked narratives: "His First Flight" by Liam O'Flaherty and "The Black Aeroplane" by Frederick Forsyth. Both stories explore themes of overcoming fear, courage, determination, and the mysterious forces that sometimes aid us in times of crisis, all centered around the act of flying.

### I. His First Flight (Liam O'Flaherty)

This story details the poignant struggle of a young seagull who is terrified to make his first flight. Despite his younger siblings having already flown, he lacks the courage to leap off the ledge. His parents try various methods – scolding, threatening starvation, and finally, tantalizing him with food – to compel him to fly. Driven by extreme hunger and the irresistible sight of his mother with a piece of fish, he finally plunges forward, flapping his wings instinctively, and discovers his ability to fly. The story is a powerful allegory for overcoming fear and gaining independence.

### II. The Black Aeroplane (Frederick Forsyth)

This is a suspenseful tale told from the first-person perspective of a pilot flying his old Dakota aeroplane from Paris to London at night. He dreams of his holiday and being with his family. However, his pleasant journey turns perilous when he encounters a massive storm cloud. His instruments fail, and he loses contact with Paris Control, putting his life and his plane in grave danger. Just when he loses hope, a mysterious black aeroplane appears, guiding him out of the storm. Upon landing safely, he learns that there was no other aeroplane in the sky that night, leaving the identity of his rescuer an unexplained mystery.

## 2) Key Concepts

### For "His First Flight":

*   **Fear and Courage:** The central theme revolves around the young seagull's intense fear of flying and how he eventually gathers the courage to overcome it.
*   **Parental Guidance and Tough Love:** The parents' role is crucial. They use various strategies, including 'tough love' (threatening starvation and creating a situation where the seagull *must* fly), to push their child towards independence.
*   **Overcoming Obstacles:** The story is a metaphor for all individuals facing their initial challenges and the struggle to achieve a new skill or phase in life.
*   **Hunger as a Motivator:** Extreme hunger becomes the ultimate catalyst that forces the young seagull to attempt his first flight.
*   **Independence and Self-Reliance:** The flight marks the seagull's transition from dependence to self-reliance.
*   **Coming of Age:** Metaphorically, the story represents a crucial step in maturation and becoming independent.

### For "The Black Aeroplane":

*   **Mystery and Suspense:** The story is built around the unexplained appearance and disappearance of the black aeroplane and its pilot, creating a sense of wonder and suspense.
*   **Presence of Mind in Crisis:** The narrator's ability to remain somewhat calm and make crucial decisions despite extreme danger is highlighted.
*   **Trust and Faith:** The pilot trusts the unknown pilot of the black aeroplane implicitly, despite the perilous situation.
*   **Supernatural/Unexplained Help:** The story explores the idea of inexplicable forces or 'guardian angels' coming to one's aid in desperate times.
*   **Human Vulnerability:** The pilot's helplessness against the storm emphasizes human vulnerability in the face of nature's power.

### Common to Both Stories:

*   **The Act of Flying:** While approached differently, flying is the core activity and a symbol of freedom, aspiration, and overcoming limitations.
*   **Adversity and Triumph:** Both protagonists face significant adversity (fear, storm) and ultimately emerge triumphant.
*   **Psychological Elements:** Both stories delve into the psychological states of the protagonists – fear, desperation, relief, and wonder.

## 3) Definitions (Vocabulary)

### From "His First Flight":

*   **Ledge:** A narrow horizontal shelf projecting from a wall or cliff.
*   **Brink:** The extreme edge of land before a steep slope or a body of water.
*   **Muster up:** To gather or summon (courage, strength, etc.).
*   **Shrilly:** In a high-pitched, piercing tone.
*   **Upbraiding:** Scolding or finding fault with someone.
*   **Skim:** To move lightly and quickly just above a surface.
*   **Devour:** To eat (food or prey) hungrily or quickly.
*   **Plateau:** An area of relatively level high ground.
*   **Taunting:** Provoking or challenging someone with insulting remarks.
*   **Ascending:** Moving upward.
*   **Blazing:** Burning fiercely or shining brightly.
*   **Preening:** (Of a bird) tidying and cleaning its feathers with its beak.
*   **Derisively:** In a manner expressing contempt or ridicule.
*   **Plaintively:** In a mournful or sad manner.
*   **Whet:** To sharpen the blade of a tool or weapon; (here, metaphorically) to sharpen one's appetite or desire.
*   **Dizzy:** Having a sensation of spinning and a tendency to fall; feeling giddy.
*   **Curveting:** (Of a horse) leaping playfully; (here, of a bird) making playful, soaring movements.
*   **Banking:** (Of an aircraft) flying with one wing higher than the other, especially in a turn.
*   **Beckoning:** Making a gesture with the hand, arm, or head to encourage someone to come nearer or follow.
*   **Cajole:** Persuade someone to do something by sustained coaxing or flattery.

### From "The Black Aeroplane":

*   **Dakota:** A type of old aeroplane (specifically, the Douglas DC-3, a common transport aircraft).
*   **Paris Control:** Air traffic control in Paris, responsible for guiding aircraft.
*   **Compass:** An instrument containing a magnetized pointer that shows the direction of magnetic north and bearings from it.
*   **Instrument (panel):** A panel in an aircraft or vehicle with gauges, dials, and other devices indicating various parameters.
*   **Turbulence:** Violent or unsteady movement of air or water, or some other fluid.
*   **Radar:** A system for detecting the presence, direction, distance, and speed of aircraft, ships, and other objects.

## 4) Formulas/Rules

In English literature, we don't have "formulas" in the mathematical sense. However, we have **literary devices and narrative techniques** that function like 'rules' or common patterns used by authors. Understanding these helps in analyzing the text.

### Literary Devices & Techniques:

*   **Personification:** Giving human qualities or actions to inanimate objects or animals. (e.g., "the great expanse of sea stretched down beneath" – sea stretching)
*   **Imagery:** Using descriptive language to create vivid mental pictures for the reader, appealing to the five senses. (e.g., "vast green sea," "blazing on his ledge")
*   **Simile:** Comparing two different things using "like" or "as."
*   **Metaphor:** Directly comparing two unlike things without using "like" or "as." (e.g., the young seagull's flight as a metaphor for overcoming fear)
*   **Symbolism:** Using an object or idea to represent something else. (e.g., flying symbolizes freedom, independence; the storm symbolizes overwhelming challenges)
*   **Suspense:** A feeling of excited or anxious uncertainty about what may happen. (Prominently used in "The Black Aeroplane")
*   **Foreshadowing:** Hints or clues about events that will occur later in the story.
*   **First-person Narrative:** The story is told by one of the characters ("I"). (Used in "The Black Aeroplane")
*   **Third-person Narrative:** The story is told by an outside observer ("he," "she," "they"). (Used in "His First Flight")
*   **Conflict:** The struggle between opposing forces.
    *   **Internal Conflict:** A struggle within a character's mind (e.g., seagull's fear).
    *   **External Conflict:** A struggle between a character and an outside force (e.g., pilot vs. storm, seagull vs. parents' taunts).
*   **Climax:** The most intense, exciting, or important point of something; the turning point.

## 5) Common Mistakes

*   **Confusing the Stories:** Students sometimes mix up details, characters, or themes between "His First Flight" and "The Black Aeroplane." Remember they are distinct stories within one chapter.
*   **Misinterpreting Parental Actions:** In "His First Flight," students might view the parents' actions (upbraiding, threatening starvation) as cruel rather than tough love necessary for the young seagull's development.
*   **Ignoring the Significance of Hunger:** For the young seagull, hunger is not just a biological need but the ultimate motivator that overrides his fear. Missing this point reduces the depth of analysis.
*   **Trying to Rationalize the Black Aeroplane:** In "The Black Aeroplane," the mystery of the helper pilot is meant to remain unexplained. Trying to provide a definitive, logical explanation often misses the point of the story, which is its enigmatic nature.
*   **Focusing Only on Plot, Ignoring Themes:** Simply narrating what happened without discussing *why* it happened or what larger ideas the stories convey (fear, courage, mystery, parental roles) will result in superficial answers.
*   **Lack of Textual Evidence:** When answering questions, especially analytical ones, students often forget to quote or refer back to specific parts of the text to support their points.
*   **Poor Vocabulary Usage:** Not using appropriate vocabulary from the chapter or using it incorrectly.
*   **Grammar and Spelling Errors:** These can detract from the quality of the answer, even if the content is good.
*   **Not Addressing Both Parts in Comparative Questions:** If a question asks for a comparison or contrast between the two stories, ensure both are discussed adequately.

## 6) Exam Tips

1.  **Read Both Stories Thoroughly:** Understand the plot, characters, and sequence of events for each story independently.
2.  **Identify Main Themes:** For "His First Flight," focus on fear, courage, parental guidance, and independence. For "The Black Aeroplane," concentrate on mystery, suspense, trust, and overcoming adversity.
3.  **Learn Key Vocabulary:** Pay attention to the glossary terms and other important words. Be able to use them in your own sentences.
4.  **Practice Character Analysis:**
    *   **Young Seagull:** His internal conflict, transformation, and ultimate triumph.
    *   **Seagull's Mother:** Her role as a motivator, using wisdom and 'tough love'.
    *   **The Pilot (Narrator of "The Black Aeroplane"):** His initial confidence, fear during the storm, presence of mind, and confusion/gratitude afterwards.
    *   **The Black Aeroplane Pilot:** His enigmatic and helpful role.
5.  **Be Ready for Comparative Questions:** Questions might ask you to compare the challenges faced by the protagonists, the role of external help, or the lessons learned in both stories.
6.  **Understand the "Unexplained":** Especially for "The Black Aeroplane," be comfortable discussing the mysterious element without forcing a concrete explanation. Acknowledge the ambiguity.
7.  **Answer Directly and Clearly:** Start your answer by directly addressing the question.
8.  **Support Your Answers with Evidence:** Whenever possible, refer to specific incidents or lines from the text to substantiate your points.
9.  **Structure Your Answers:** For long answer questions, use an introduction, body paragraphs (each with a clear point), and a conclusion.
10. **Focus on "Why" and "How":** Beyond what happened, think about *why* characters acted the way they did, *how* they overcame challenges, and what the deeper meaning of the events is.
11. **Time Management:** Allocate time appropriately for different types of questions (short answer, long answer, value-based).
12. **Revise Grammar and Spelling:** Proofread your answers to avoid unnecessary errors.
