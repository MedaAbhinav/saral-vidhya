### II. Short Answer Questions (2–3 Marks)

1.  Describe the young seagull's initial attempt to fly from the brink of the ledge.
    **Model Answer:** The young seagull took a little run forward to the brink of the ledge and attempted to flap his wings. However, he immediately became afraid, perceiving the great expanse of sea beneath him as miles down. He felt certain his wings would never support him, so he bent his head and ran back to his hole.

2.  Why did the young seagull's parents use both scolding and taunting tactics to get him to fly?
    **Model Answer:** The young seagull's parents were trying to push him towards independence and survival. Scolding ("upbraiding") and taunting him with his "cowardice" were methods to make him realise the urgency of learning to fly. They understood that he needed to overcome his fear for his own well-being and to be able to find food.

3.  How did the mother seagull's actions finally lead to the young seagull's first flight?
    **Model Answer:** The mother seagull strategically picked up a piece of fish and flew across to him, halting just out of his reach. The sight of the food, combined with his maddening hunger, compelled him to dive at the fish. This desperate lunge outwards and downwards forced his wings to spread and led to his first flight.

4.  What feeling swept over the young seagull immediately after his initial fall, and what happened next?
    **Model Answer:** Immediately after his initial fall, a monstrous terror seized him, and his heart stood still. However, this feeling only lasted a minute. The next moment, he felt his wings spread outwards, the wind rushed against him, and he realised he was soaring, not falling headlong, and was no longer afraid.

5.  What was the family's reaction when the young seagull successfully made his first flight and landed on the sea?
    **Model Answer:** When the young seagull landed on the sea and floated, his family surrounded him, screaming, praising him, and offering him scraps of dog-fish from their beaks. They were clearly proud and supportive of his achievement.

6.  What was the pilot's mood and his destination at the beginning of "The Black Aeroplane"?
    **Model Answer:** At the beginning of the story, the pilot was happy and content, flying alone high up above the sleeping countryside. He was dreaming of his holiday and looking forward to being with his family in England, which was his destination.

7.  Describe the terrifying experience of flying inside the storm clouds for the pilot.
    **Model Answer:** Inside the storm clouds, everything turned black, and it became impossible to see anything outside the aeroplane. The plane jumped and twisted violently. His compass stopped working, then his radio, leaving him completely lost, without contact, and with rapidly diminishing fuel.

8.  How did the mysterious black aeroplane provide guidance to the narrator?
    **Model Answer:** The pilot of the black aeroplane waved his hand, signalling the narrator to follow. He flew ahead, turning slowly to the north, guiding the narrator through the storm. The narrator blindly followed this mysterious plane, trusting it to lead him to safety.

9.  Why was the narrator so keen to know the identity of the other pilot after landing safely?
    **Model Answer:** The narrator was keen to know the identity of the other pilot because he believed that pilot had saved his life by guiding him out of the terrible storm. He felt a deep sense of gratitude and wanted to thank his mysterious saviour.

10. What does the ending of "The Black Aeroplane" suggest about the nature of the helper pilot?
    **Model Answer:** The ending of the story, with the control room staff confirming no other planes were flying that night, suggests a mysterious and inexplicable nature to the helper pilot. It implies the possibility of a guardian angel, a hallucination brought on by stress, or an unknown, supernatural intervention rather than a physical presence.

### III. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

1.  "Fear is only as deep as the mind allows." Discuss this statement with reference to the young seagull's experience in "His First Flight."
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** Introduce the young seagull's profound, debilitating fear despite his natural instinct to fly.
    *   **Body Paragraph 1: The Mental Block:** Describe how his fear of the "vast expanse of sea" and the belief that his "wings would never support him" paralysed him, even though his siblings, with shorter wings, had flown. His fear was primarily psychological.
    *   **Body Paragraph 2: Struggle and Isolation:** Explain how he remained alone and starving for 24 hours, his mind unable to overcome the internal barrier, despite external stimuli (parents calling, food).
    *   **Body Paragraph 3: The Catalyst – Hunger and Action:** Detail how maddening hunger finally provided a stronger impulse than his fear. The dive, triggered by the mother's bait, forced him into action, proving his mind wrong.
    *   **Body Paragraph 4: The Discovery and Overcoming Fear:** Describe the moment he felt his wings spread and realised he was soaring, not falling. This physical experience instantly dissolved his mental block, proving that his fear was, indeed, only a mental construct.
    *   **Conclusion:** Conclude by reiterating that the story highlights how perceived limitations are often self-imposed, and real courage comes from taking the leap of faith to overcome them.

2.  Describe the crucial role played by the young seagull's parents in motivating him to overcome his fear and achieve his first flight.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** Emphasise the significant and multifaceted role of the parents in the young seagull's development.
    *   **Body Paragraph 1: Initial Encouragement and Instruction:** Explain how they perfected his siblings in flight, teaching them to skim waves and dive for fish, demonstrating what was possible and expected.
    *   **Body Paragraph 2: Firmness and Pressure (Tough Love):** Detail their use of "upbraiding," "threatening to let him starve," and "taunting him with his cowardice." This was a deliberate strategy to create urgency and force him out of his comfort zone.
    *   **Body Paragraph 3: Strategic Motivation (The Bait):** Highlight the mother's clever and pivotal act of presenting the fish tantalisingly close and then withholding it. This played on his most primal instinct (hunger) to override his greatest fear.
    *   **Body Paragraph 4: Post-Flight Support and Reinforcement:** Describe how the entire family celebrated his success by screaming, praising, and offering him food, thereby reinforcing his achievement and building his confidence for future flights.
    *   **Conclusion:** Conclude that the parents employed a combination of instruction, firm discipline, strategic manipulation, and unwavering post-success support, all crucial for their son's crucial step towards independence and survival.

3.  How does the author build suspense and mystery throughout the narrative of "The Black Aeroplane"? What different interpretations can be drawn from the 'black aeroplane' at the end?
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** State that the story effectively blends a harrowing survival tale with a profound sense of the inexplicable.
    *   **Building Suspense:**
        *   **Initial Calm vs. Sudden Danger:** The story starts with a peaceful, routine flight, which quickly escalates into terrifying danger upon encountering the storm clouds.
        *   **Loss of Control and Isolation:** The failure of instruments (compass, radio) and the darkness inside the storm create extreme vulnerability and helplessness, building tension.
        *   **Appearance of the Unknown:** The sudden, silent emergence of a black aeroplane with no lights introduces an element of mystery and desperate hope.
        *   **Blind Trust and Guidance:** The pilot's decision to follow an unknown plane through the storm heightens the suspense – will it lead to safety or further peril?
        *   **The Vanishing Act:** The black aeroplane disappearing as soon as the narrator lands, leaves him (and the reader) bewildered.
        *   **The Final Revelation:** The control tower's statement that no other plane was flying that night cements the mystery, leaving the event unexplained.
    *   **Interpretations of the 'Black Aeroplane':**
        *   **Supernatural/Divine Intervention:** A guardian angel or a miraculous helper.
        *   **Hallucination/Psychological Manifestation:** A product of the pilot's extreme stress, fear, and desire for survival. His mind created a 'saviour'.
        *   **Symbol of Hope/Inner Strength:** Perhaps the 'other pilot' was a manifestation of the narrator's own courage and instinct to survive, guiding himself.
        *   **An Unidentified Real Plane:** Though contradicted by the control tower, in fiction, it could be an unknown, off-radar aircraft.
    *   **Conclusion:** The author masterfully uses these elements to create a gripping narrative that leaves a lasting impression of wonder and the power of the unknown.

4.  Both "His First Flight" and "The Black Aeroplane" depict overcoming a challenge. Compare and contrast the challenges faced by the young seagull and the pilot.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** Acknowledge that both stories explore the theme of facing and overcoming significant adversity.
    *   **Similarities:**
        *   **Overcoming Fear:** Both protagonists are gripped by intense fear (seagull: fear of falling; pilot: fear of crashing/being lost). Both have to make a 'leap of faith' or trust in an unknown element.
        *   **Instinct for Survival:** Both are driven by powerful instincts – the seagull's hunger, the pilot's desire to reach home and family.
        *   **External Catalyst/Support:** Both receive external help or a 'push' – the seagull from his mother, the pilot from the mysterious black aeroplane.
    *   **Contrasts:**
        *   **Nature of the Challenge:** Seagull's challenge is developmental, internal, and a natural rite of passage (physical/psychological fear of a natural act). Pilot's challenge is external, environmental, and technological (a sudden, severe storm and instrument failure).
        *   **Source of Help:** Seagull's help comes from known family members through deliberate, albeit tough, actions. Pilot's help comes from an unknown, mysterious, and ultimately unverified source.
        *   **Resolution/Outcome:** Seagull achieves self-reliance, gains confidence, and integrates fully with his family's way of life. Pilot is saved, but the mystery of his saviour remains, leaving him with a sense of wonder rather than complete understanding.
    *   **Conclusion:** While both stories celebrate courage and the will to survive, they illustrate different facets of human/animal struggle – one an internal, developmental battle, the other an external, unpredictable confrontation with nature.

5.  What message about the importance of family support and self-belief can be drawn from "His First Flight"?
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** State that "His First Flight" powerfully conveys lessons on the interrelation of family support and the development of self-belief.
    *   **Importance of Family Support:**
        *   **Guidance and Teaching:** Parents initially perfect the siblings, setting an example.
        *   **Firmness and Discipline:** Their "upbraiding" and "threatening" demonstrate a necessary 'tough love' to prevent complacency and encourage growth.
        *   **Strategic Motivation:** The mother's use of food as bait is a calculated act of support, knowing what would push him hardest.
        *   **Unconditional Reinforcement:** Their screaming, praising, and offering food after his flight validates his efforts and builds his confidence. Family acts as a safety net and a motivator.
    *   **Importance of Self-Belief:**
        *   **Initial Lack:** The young seagull's crippling fear stemmed from a lack of self-belief ("his wings would never support him"), despite observing others.
        *   **The Breakthrough:** It's only when he is forced to act (diving for food) that he physically experiences his capabilities.
        *   **Discovery of Innate Ability:** The moment his wings supported him, his self-belief was instantly forged, leading to joyous and confident flying. He realised his potential.
    *   **Conclusion:** The story teaches that while strong, sometimes tough, family support is vital in pushing individuals beyond their comfort zones, ultimately, it is the personal experience of overcoming a challenge that cultivates true self-belief and leads to independence and mastery.

### IV. Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

1.  If the young seagull had not received the 'push' from his mother through the fish, do you think he would have eventually flown on his own? Justify your answer with reasons from the text.
    **Model Answer:**
    It is highly unlikely that the young seagull would have eventually flown on his own without his mother's intervention.
    **Reasons:**
    *   **Prolonged Inaction:** He had been starving for twenty-four hours, feeling the heat and the pangs of hunger, yet he failed to muster courage. He even resorted to pretending to be asleep to elicit a response, showing his desperate state but inability to act.
    *   **Deep-seated Fear:** His fear was so profound that even his siblings, with shorter wings, had flown away, and he remained paralysed. He "felt certain that his wings would never support him," indicating a strong mental block.
    *   **Failure of Earlier Tactics:** His parents had already tried calling him "shrilly," "upbraiding him," and "threatening to let him starve," but "for the life of him he could not move." This suggests that verbal encouragement or threats alone were insufficient.
    *   **The 'Maddening' Effect of Hunger:** It was the *sight of the food* and the *maddening hunger* combined with the mother's proximity that forced him to dive, overriding his mental paralysis. Without this specific, intense stimulus, his fear would likely have continued to dominate, potentially leading to starvation.

2.  The ending of "The Black Aeroplane" leaves the reader with a profound sense of wonder and mystery. Discuss how the author effectively builds this suspense throughout the narrative and what possible interpretations of the 'black aeroplane' exist.
    **Model Answer:**
    The author masterfully builds suspense in "The Black Aeroplane" through several narrative techniques:
    *   **Initial Calm vs. Sudden Peril:** The story opens with the pilot's pleasant, routine flight, dreaming of home. This stark contrast with the sudden, terrifying encounter with monstrous storm clouds immediately creates tension.
    *   **Loss of Control:** The failure of critical instruments like the compass and radio, along with the plane "jumping and twisting" in the dark, renders the pilot completely helpless, amplifying the reader's anxiety.
    *   **Introduction of the Unknown:** The sudden, silent appearance of a "black aeroplane" with no lights, and an unknown pilot waving for him to follow, introduces a mysterious element into an already desperate situation. The pilot's blind trust in this stranger heightens the suspense.
    *   **The Vanishing Act:** The black aeroplane disappears without a trace as soon as the narrator lands, leaving him bewildered and raising questions about its existence.
    *   **The Final Revelation:** The control tower woman's confirmation that there were "no other planes in the sky tonight" seals the mystery, making the event inexplicable and deepening the sense of wonder.

    Possible interpretations of the 'black aeroplane' include:
    *   **A Guardian Angel/Supernatural Entity:** Many interpret it as a divine intervention or a benevolent spirit guiding the pilot out of danger, a miraculous act of salvation.
    *   **A Hallucination:** It could be a figment of the pilot's imagination, induced by extreme stress, fear, and possibly hypoxia (lack of oxygen) at high altitudes within the storm. His mind created a 'saviour' in a desperate situation.
    *   **Symbol of Hope/Inner Strength:** The 'other pilot' could represent the narrator's own subconscious will to survive and his hidden courage, guiding himself through the crisis. He found his own 'inner pilot' when all seemed lost.
    *   **An Unidentified Real Plane:** Though contradicted by the control tower, in a fictional context, it could be a real, unregistered, or off-radar aircraft whose pilot chose to offer aid anonymously.

3.  Imagine you are the air traffic controller at Paris Control during the night the Dakota DS 088 encountered the storm. Write a diary entry describing your experience of that night, specifically regarding the Dakota DS 088 and the subsequent confusion about the 'black aeroplane'.
    **Model Answer:**
    **October 15th, 1946 – 03:00 AM**

    **Diary Entry:**

    What a night! The graveyard shift is usually quiet, but tonight... tonight was something else. It started routinely enough. Dakota DS 088, en route to England, checked in at 01:30. Pilot sounded cheerful, probably dreaming of home and holiday, as they all do. I cleared him for his route, advised him to turn twelve degrees west. Everything clear, moon coming up.

    Then the storm hit. Out of nowhere, these colossal black clouds, like mountains, just *appeared*. I saw them building on radar, tried to warn DS 088, advised him to turn back to Paris or deviate. But he insisted on going straight through. Foolish, perhaps, but a pilot's choice.

    Soon after, static, then silence. DS 088 vanished from the radar, off my frequency. My heart pounded. Fuel critical, lost in a storm, no contact... I immediately initiated emergency protocols, alerted search and rescue, began charting other known aircraft, but there were hardly any in that sector at that hour.

    Just as panic was truly setting in, a miracle. DS 088, out of the black, landing safely on runway 18. The pilot, pale but alive, rushes into the control room. He's asking about... another pilot? A 'black aeroplane' that guided him out of the storm? My blood ran cold. I checked the radar logs, cross-referenced with all other flight plans, even called neighbouring sectors. There was *nothing*. Absolutely zero other aircraft flying in that storm, let alone a black one, or one without lights.

    I told him, "No other planes were flying tonight." The look on his face, a mix of relief and utter confusion, was unsettling. I’ve filed my report, but what do I write about a ghost plane? Was he hallucinating? A guardian angel? I don't know what to believe. This is a story that will haunt my shifts for a long time. Just glad he's safe. But the mystery... it chills me.
