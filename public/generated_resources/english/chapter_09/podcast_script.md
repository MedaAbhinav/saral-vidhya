## Literary Lens: Class 10 English – "The Proposal"

**(Sound of gentle, upbeat podcast intro music fades in and out)**

**Riya (Host):** Hello everyone, and welcome back to "Literary Lens," your weekly podcast where we dive deep into the fascinating world of NCERT English literature! I'm your host, Riya, and today we're unwrapping a delightful and often hilarious one-act play from your Class 10 English textbook: "The Proposal."

To help us explore this engaging piece, we have a wonderful expert with us today, a seasoned English teacher with years of experience, Mrs. Sharma. Welcome, Mrs. Sharma!

**Expert Teacher (Mrs. Sharma):** Thank you, Riya! It's a pleasure to be here and chat about Anton Chekhov's brilliant work.

**Riya:** Mrs. Sharma, let's start right at the beginning. The title, "The Proposal," itself has a couple of meanings, as the 'Before You Read' section points out. What kind of proposal are we talking about here? Is it a business plan, or something else entirely?

**Mrs. Sharma:** That's an excellent point, Riya. The 'Before You Read' activity helps us clarify this right away. While 'proposal' can mean a suggestion or a scheme, in this play, it specifically refers to **the act of asking someone's hand in marriage**. It's a marriage proposal, but as we'll see, it's far from a romantic one! And speaking of marriage, the excerpt also gives us a glimpse into Russian wedding customs, which are quite fun and unique, aren't they? Things like the groom having to 'fight' to get his bride or the bride getting 'stolen' for a fee. It sets a lively, almost farcical tone, even before the play begins.

**Riya:** Absolutely! Those customs sound quite entertaining. So, a marriage proposal is at the heart of it. And this play is written by the famous Russian short story writer and dramatist, Anton Chekhov. What kind of play is it, exactly?

**Mrs. Sharma:** Chekhov called "The Proposal" a **farce**. A farce is a type of comedy that aims to entertain the audience through highly exaggerated, improbable situations, quick wit, and often physical comedy, though here it's more about the rapid-fire verbal sparring. The characters are typically caricatures, not deeply complex individuals, and their reactions are often over-the-top for comedic effect. It's meant to be light-hearted and make you laugh at human follies.

**Riya:** That sounds like fun! Now, let's meet our key players in this farce. Who are the main characters?

**Mrs. Sharma:** We have three main characters:
1.  **Stepan Stepanovitch Chubukov:** A wealthy landowner, who is Natalya's father. He's easily irritable but also very concerned with appearances and economic gain.
2.  **Natalya Stepanovna:** Chubukov's twenty-five-year-old daughter. She's also a landowner, very opinionated, stubborn, and equally quarrelsome.
3.  **Ivan Vassilevitch Lomov:** A long-time wealthy neighbor of Chubukov. He's a hypochondriac, meaning he constantly worries about his health, and is quite nervous and excitable.

**Riya:** So, these three characters, all wealthy landowners, are at the center of this story. Lomov comes to Chubukov's house. What's his actual intention? Is it a grand declaration of love for Natalya?

**Mrs. Sharma:** [Chuckles] Oh, Riya, if only! While he is there to propose marriage to Natalya, the primary motivation isn't love in the romantic sense. Chekhov cleverly highlights the **economic aspect of marriage** during that era. Lomov is seeking Natalya's hand because he believes it's "good economic sense." He's getting on in years, he's anxious, and he wants a stable life. Marrying Natalya would mean securing his future, consolidating his land, and having someone to look after him. Her family is also wealthy, so it’s a strategic alliance for both families to increase their estates.

**Riya:** So, it's more about property and convenience than passion. And this leads to some rather dramatic moments, doesn't it? The play is famous for its endless squabbles. What do these characters quarrel about, despite the serious intention of a marriage proposal?

**Mrs. Sharma:** This is where the farce truly shines! They quarrel over incredibly petty issues, completely forgetting the reason Lomov came in the first place. The two major arguments are:
1.  **Oxen Meadows:** Lomov claims a piece of land called Oxen Meadows belongs to his family, while Natalya insists it's theirs. They go back generations, citing grandmothers and great-aunts, debating the history of the land in ridiculous detail.
2.  **Their Dogs:** The second argument erupts over the superiority of their hunting dogs. Lomov praises his dog, Guess, saying it's the best, while Natalya vehemently defends her dog, Squeezer, calling Guess old and ugly. They argue about pedigree, price, and hunting skills!

These arguments escalate quickly, with insults flying, and Lomov even collapses multiple times due to his weak heart, only to revive and continue the quarrel!

**Riya:** It sounds like a chaotic but hilarious sequence of events! The actual proposal gets lost amidst all this bickering.
Alright, Mrs. Sharma, before we delve deeper, let's quickly test our listeners' understanding with a couple of quiz questions!

**Quiz Time!**

**Riya:** Listeners, grab a pen and paper if you like, or just think of the answers!

**Question 1:** What is the *primary* reason Lomov wants to marry Natalya?
(a) He is deeply in love with her.
(b) He wants to consolidate his family's property and secure his future.
(c) He wants to prove his dog is better than hers.
(d) He wants to settle a dispute over Oxen Meadows.

**Question 2:** Name *one* of the two main topics the characters repeatedly quarrel about in the play.

**Question 3:** What specific genre of play is "The Proposal" classified as by Chekhov?

**Riya:** Alright, Mrs. Sharma, let's quickly give our listeners the answers!

**Mrs. Sharma:**
**Answer 1:** The correct answer is (b) He wants to consolidate his family's property and secure his future.
**Answer 2:** The two main topics are **Oxen Meadows** and **their hunting dogs (Guess and Squeezer)**.
**Answer 3:** It's classified as a **farce**.

**Riya:** Excellent! I hope our listeners got those right. Mrs. Sharma, coming back to the play, what do you think Chekhov is trying to convey through this constant bickering and the seemingly superficial nature of the proposal?

**Mrs. Sharma:** Chekhov is subtly critiquing the society of his time, particularly the landowning class. He highlights how trivial matters often overshadow significant decisions, and how **pride, stubbornness, and material gain** can dominate human relationships. The play satirizes people who are so obsessed with their possessions and status that they forget common courtesy and even their own initial purpose. Despite all the fighting, the "economic good sense" prevails, and the proposal is ultimately accepted, but with the threat of continuous quarrelling hanging over their heads. It's a humorous yet insightful commentary on human nature and societal values.

**Riya:** That's a fantastic insight, Mrs. Sharma. It's a play that makes you laugh but also makes you think. So, in summary, "The Proposal" is a hilarious farce by Anton Chekhov about a marriage proposal driven by economic motives, constantly derailed by petty arguments over land and dogs, offering a sharp commentary on human folly and societal values.

**Mrs. Sharma:** Precisely, Riya! It's a timeless piece that continues to entertain and resonate.
