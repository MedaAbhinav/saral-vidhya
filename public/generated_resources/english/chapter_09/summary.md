"The Proposal" is a one-act farce by Anton Chekhov, satirizing the materialistic approach to marriage prevalent among wealthy Russian families. The play centers on Ivan Lomov, a hypochondriac landowner, who visits his neighbour Stepan Chubukov to propose marriage to his 25-year-old daughter, Natalya.

However, all three characters are excessively quarrelsome and easily provoked. Instead of a straightforward proposal, Lomov and Natalya engage in absurd arguments over petty issues. They first bicker fiercely about the ownership of Oxen Meadows, then move on to a heated dispute regarding the superiority of their dogs, Lomov's Guess and Natalya's Squeezer. These continuous quarrels repeatedly interrupt Lomov's attempts to pop the question, causing him extreme physical and emotional distress.

Despite the constant bickering and insults, Chubukov, recognizing the economic benefit of the match, pushes Natalya to accept Lomov. The play concludes with the couple becoming engaged amidst fresh quarrels, highlighting that practical, financial considerations often supersede genuine affection and compatibility in such alliances, while emphasizing the characters' contentious nature and the inherent absurdity of their pride.
