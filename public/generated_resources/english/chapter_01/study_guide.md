## NCERT Class 10 English: "A Letter to God" – Study Guide

### 1) Chapter Overview

"A Letter to God" is a simple yet profound story about Lencho, a hardworking and simple farmer, who possesses immense faith in God. The story begins with Lencho eagerly awaiting a good harvest, but his hopes are dashed when a devastating hailstorm destroys his entire cornfield. With his family facing starvation, Lencho, whose only hope is help from God, decides to write a letter to Him, requesting one hundred pesos to sow his field again and support his family.

He mails the letter, addressed "To God". A postman discovers the unusual address and shows it to the postmaster, who, initially amused, is deeply moved by Lencho's unwavering faith. To preserve Lencho's faith, the postmaster decides to answer the letter himself. He collects money from his employees, contributes a part of his own salary, and persuades friends to donate for a "charitable act." However, he is only able to gather seventy pesos. He puts the money in an envelope addressed to Lencho, with a single word as a signature: "God."

The following Sunday, Lencho arrives to collect his letter. He shows no surprise upon receiving the money, such was his confidence. However, upon counting the money, he becomes angry, believing God could not have made a mistake nor denied him his request. He concludes that the post office employees must have stolen the missing thirty pesos. He immediately writes another letter to God, asking for the remaining money but specifically requesting that it not be sent through the post office, as he calls them "a bunch of crooks."

The story brilliantly highlights themes of faith, human kindness, and the irony of human perception.

### 2) Key Concepts

*   **Unwavering Faith:** Lencho's absolute and unquestioning belief in God's existence and His power to help in times of distress is the central theme. His faith is so strong that he initiates a direct correspondence with God and fully expects a reply.
*   **Irony (Dramatic & Situational):** The story is rich in irony.
    *   **Dramatic Irony:** The reader knows that the postmaster and his employees are the ones helping Lencho, but Lencho remains ignorant of this fact.
    *   **Situational Irony:** The very people who come to Lencho's aid out of charity and good intentions are ultimately accused by him of being "a bunch of crooks." Lencho, the beneficiary, blames his benefactors.
*   **Humanity and Charity:** The postmaster and his employees represent the best of humanity, demonstrating empathy, selflessness, and a commitment to preserving another's faith, even if it means personal sacrifice.
*   **Misplaced Trust/Misjudgment:** Lencho, despite his profound faith in God, completely misjudges the intentions of the post office employees, viewing their helpful actions as deceit and theft. His trust in God is absolute, but his trust in fellow humans is non-existent.
*   **Optimism vs. Reality:** Lencho's initial optimism about the rain and harvest quickly turns into despair due to the hailstorm, setting the stage for his appeal to God.
*   **Nature's Destructive Power:** The hailstorm serves as a catalyst, highlighting how forces beyond human control can swiftly destroy livelihoods.

### 3) Definitions

Here are some important words and their meanings from the chapter:

*   **Crest:** The top of a hill or mountain. (e.g., "The house... sat on the crest of a low hill.")
*   **Draped:** Covered or decorated with a cloth or covering, usually hanging loosely. (e.g., "regarded the field of ripe corn... draped in a curtain of rain.")
*   **Locusts:** Swarms of grasshoppers that fly in large groups and destroy crops. (e.g., "A plague of locusts would have left more than this.")
*   **Conscience:** An inner sense of what is right or wrong that guides a person's behaviour. (e.g., "whose eyes... see everything, even what is deep in one’s conscience.")
*   **Peso:** The monetary unit (currency) of several Latin American countries.
*   **Amiable:** Having or displaying a friendly and pleasant manner. (e.g., "The postmaster — a fat, amiable fellow...")
*   **Contentment:** A state of happiness and satisfaction. (e.g., "experiencing the contentment of a man who has performed a good deed...")
*   **Resolution:** A firm decision to do or not to do something. (e.g., "But he stuck to his resolution.")
*   **Obliged:** Indebted or grateful; bound by a contract or promise. (Here, meaning felt compelled by duty or generosity to give.) (e.g., "several friends of his were obliged to give something ‘for an act of charity’.")
*   **Crooks:** Dishonest people; thieves. (e.g., "a bunch of crooks.")

### 4) Literary Devices & Analytical Approaches

While there are no "formulas" in English literature, understanding literary devices and analytical approaches is crucial for deeper comprehension:

*   **Metaphor:**
    *   **Rule:** A figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to an object or action to which it is not literally applicable. It implies comparison without using "like" or "as."
    *   **Example:** "These aren’t raindrops falling from the sky, they are new coins. The big drops are ten cent pieces and the little ones are fives." (Raindrops are directly compared to coins, representing their value to Lencho's harvest.)
    *   **Example:** "The hailstones... truly did resemble new silver coins." (Further emphasizing the comparison to coins.)
*   **Simile:**
    *   **Rule:** A figure of speech involving the comparison of one thing with another thing of a different kind, used to make a description more emphatic or vivid, using "like" or "as."
    *   **Example:** "The field was white, as if covered with salt." (Comparing the hail-covered field to salt.)
    *   **Example:** "Lencho was an ox of a man, working like an animal in the fields..." (Comparing Lencho's strength and work ethic to an ox/animal.)
*   **Irony:**
    *   **Rule:** The expression of one's meaning by using language that normally signifies the opposite, typically for humorous or emphatic effect (verbal irony), or a state of affairs or an event that seems deliberately contrary to what one expects and is often amusing as a result (situational irony).
    *   **Example:** The ultimate irony is Lencho calling the post office employees "a bunch of crooks" when they were the ones who secretly helped him.
*   **Characterisation:**
    *   **Rule:** The creation or construction of a fictional character. This involves describing their personality, actions, dialogues, thoughts, and how other characters perceive them.
    *   **Analysis:** Lencho is characterized by his deep faith, simplicity, and hard work. The Postmaster is characterized by his amiability, empathy, and humanity.
*   **Theme Analysis:**
    *   **Rule:** Identifying and discussing the underlying messages or main ideas the author conveys through the story.
    *   **Analysis:** Explore how faith, charity, the conflict between human intention and perception, and the power of nature are developed throughout the narrative.

### 5) Common Mistakes

*   **Misinterpreting Lencho's character:** Some might view Lencho as selfish or ungrateful. It's important to understand that his anger stems from his *absolute faith* in God, making him believe God couldn't make a mistake, and therefore, the missing money *must* have been stolen by the humans around him. His faith is pure but naive concerning human honesty.
*   **Failing to grasp the central irony:** Not understanding that the very people Lencho curses are his saviors is missing the core message and literary brilliance of the story.
*   **Underestimating the Postmaster's role:** The postmaster is a key character, representing selfless human kindness. His actions are crucial to the plot and theme.
*   **Focusing too much on the literal:** The story is a parable-like narrative. While the plot is straightforward, its underlying themes of faith, humanity, and perception are more significant than just the events themselves.
*   **Ignoring descriptive details:** The descriptions of the valley, the cornfield, the hailstones as "frozen pearls," and Lencho's expressions contribute to the story's atmosphere and character development.

### 6) Exam Tips

*   **Read the Chapter Thoroughly:** Understand the plot, character motivations, and the sequence of events.
*   **Identify Key Characters and Their Traits:** Focus on Lencho (hardworking, immense faith, naive, simple) and the Postmaster (amiable, empathetic, kind, selfless).
*   **Grasp the Central Irony:** This is a frequently tested concept. Be prepared to explain how Lencho's perception differs from reality and why it's ironic.
*   **Analyze Themes:** Be ready to discuss themes like faith (Lencho's), human kindness/charity (Postmaster's), misjudgment, and the power of nature. Think about how these themes are developed.
*   **Vocabulary and Figurative Language:** Pay attention to new words and phrases. Be able to identify and explain metaphors and similes used in the story (e.g., raindrops as "new coins," hailstones as "frozen pearls," field "as if covered with salt").
*   **Practice Comprehension Questions:** Answer questions about:
    *   What Lencho hoped for and why.
    *   How the rain changed and its impact.
    *   Lencho's feelings after the hailstorm.
    *   Who Lencho had faith in and what he did.
    *   The Postmaster's reaction and actions.
    *   Lencho's reaction to receiving the money.
    *   The irony of the ending.
*   **Focus on Value-Based Questions:** Questions might ask about the values portrayed by the Postmaster (empathy, generosity) or the nature of Lencho's faith.
*   **Structure Your Answers:** For short answer questions, be direct and to the point. For long answer questions, include an introduction, body paragraphs (with relevant points and textual evidence), and a conclusion.
