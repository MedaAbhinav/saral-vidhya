## 1. Before You Read

### Theme: The Power of Faith

*   **Core Idea:** The introductory section sets the stage by asking a profound question: "They say faith can move mountains. But what should we put our faith in?" This immediately introduces the central theme of the story – the transformative power of faith and its object.
*   **Story Preview:** It briefly outlines Lencho's predicament (ruined crops, letter to God for 100 pesos) and prompts the reader to predict the outcome. This encourages active engagement with the narrative.
*   **Key Question for Reflection:** Does Lencho's letter reach God? Does God send him the money? These questions invite personal thought before delving into the story.

## 2. Activity

The activities provided aim to connect the story's theme (sending money, communication) with real-world scenarios and vocabulary.

### Activity 1: Sending Money Through the Post Office

*   **Purpose:** To familiarize students with the process of sending money via a post office, which is directly relevant to Lencho's actions.
*   **Key Vocabulary & Definitions:**
    *   **Counter:** A long flat-topped fixture in a bank, shop, or post office across which business is conducted with customers. (e.g., "Go to the post office counter to buy stamps.")
    *   **Counter Clerk:** An employee working at a counter, assisting customers. (e.g., "The counter clerk helped me fill out the form.")
    *   **Appropriate:** Suitable or proper in the circumstances. (e.g., "Fill in the appropriate details in the form.")
    *   **Acknowledgement:** Confirmation or acceptance of receipt. (e.g., "You will receive an acknowledgement once the money is delivered.")
    *   **Counterfoil:** The part of a form, ticket, etc., that is torn off and kept as a record by the person who issued it. (e.g., "Keep the money order counterfoil for your records.")
    *   **Record:** A piece of information or an account of an event, typically preserved in a permanent form. (e.g., "The post office maintains a record of all transactions.")

### Activity 2: Filling a Money Order Form

This activity focuses on understanding the structure and function of a Money Order (MO) form.

*   **Scenario:** Hypothetically sending money to a friend, relative, or for a subscription.
*   **Practical Learning:** Encourages hands-on experience by suggesting filling out a form and submitting it.
*   **Parts of a Money Order Form:**
    *   **Money Order Form (Main Part):** Contains details of sender, receiver, amount, and sometimes purpose.
    *   **Part for Official Use:** Reserved for post office stamps, signatures, and internal processing.
    *   **Acknowledgement:** A slip detached and sent back to the sender as proof of payment and delivery, often signed by the receiver.
*   **"Space for Communication":** This section is typically used for writing a brief, personal message to the receiver or stating the purpose of the money (e.g., "Happy Birthday," "For your fees," "Magazine subscription").

#### Completion Statements:

Based on the typical structure and process of a Money Order:

(i) In addition to the sender, the form has to be signed by the **receiver** (on the acknowledgement part, confirming receipt).
(ii) The ‘Acknowledgement’ section of the form is sent back by the post office to the **sender** after the **receiver** signs it.
(iii) The ‘Space for Communication’ section is used for **writing a short, personal message or specifying the purpose of the money transfer**.
(iv) The form has six sections. The sender needs to fill out sections **pertaining to sender's details, receiver's details, the amount, and the communication message** and the receiver **signs the acknowledgement part upon receiving the money**.

## 3. Story Analysis: "A Letter to God"

### Part 1: Setting, Anticipation, and Disaster (Pages 3-5)

#### Setting the Scene: Lencho's House and Field
*   **Location:** Lencho's house is the **only one** in the entire valley, perched on the crest of a low hill. This immediately emphasizes his isolation.
*   **View:** From his house, one could see the river and his field of ripe corn, dotted with flowers, which promised a **good harvest**. This sets a hopeful tone.
*   **Immediate Need:** The earth needed only one thing: a **downpour or at least a shower** for the crops to yield fully.

#### Lencho's Anticipation of Rain
*   **Expert Farmer:** Lencho knew his fields intimately. He spent the entire morning gazing at the sky towards the **north-east**, anticipating rain.
*   **Family Interaction:** He tells his wife, "Now we’re really going to get some water, woman." His wife, preparing supper, replies, "Yes, God willing," showing their shared faith and dependence on nature.
*   **Children:** The older boys were working in the field, while the younger ones played near the house. They are all called for dinner.

#### The Blessing Turns into a Curse
*   **Rain Begins:** Just as Lencho predicted, big drops of rain began to fall during the meal. Huge mountains of clouds were seen approaching from the north-east, and the air was fresh and sweet.
*   **Lencho's Joy and Comparison:** Lencho goes out to feel the rain, describing it ecstatically: "These aren’t raindrops falling from the sky, they are new coins. The big drops are ten cent pieces and the little ones are fives." This shows his initial happiness and the economic importance of the rain to his livelihood.
*   **Sudden Turn:** A strong wind begins to blow, and with the rain, **very large hailstones** (resembling "new silver coins" and "frozen pearls") begin to fall. The boys, initially excited, run out to collect them.
*   **Growing Despair:** Lencho realizes the gravity of the situation: "It’s really getting bad now." He hopes it passes quickly, but it doesn't.
*   **Complete Devastation:** The hail rains for an hour, covering the house, garden, hillside, and cornfield—the whole valley—making it appear **"white, as if covered with salt."**
    *   **Consequences:** Not a leaf remains on the trees. The corn is totally destroyed. The flowers are gone from the plants.
*   **Lencho's Profound Sadness:** Lencho's "soul was filled with sadness." Standing in the middle of his ruined field, he bitterly compares the damage to a "plague of locusts" which would have left more.
*   **Future bleak:** He declares to his sons, "The hail has left nothing. This year we will have no corn." This leads to a night of sorrow and the fearful realization: "We’ll all go hungry this year."

---

### Oral Comprehension Check (Page 5)

1.  **What did Lencho hope for?**
    *   Lencho hoped for a downpour or at least a shower for his ripe cornfields to have a good harvest.
2.  **Why did Lencho say the raindrops were like ‘new coins’?**
    *   Lencho said the raindrops were like 'new coins' because they were crucial for his harvest. A good harvest would mean prosperity and money for his family, so the rain was directly linked to financial gain. The big drops were compared to ten-cent pieces and the little ones to fives, highlighting their monetary value to him.
3.  **How did the rain change? What happened to Lencho’s fields?**
    *   The rain initially started as a gentle downpour, but it quickly changed into a destructive hailstorm. Very large hailstones began to fall along with a strong wind.
    *   Lencho’s fields were completely destroyed. The corn was totally ruined, the flowers were gone from the plants, and not a single leaf remained on the trees. The entire valley was covered in hail, looking like it was covered with salt.
4.  **What were Lencho’s feelings when the hail stopped?**
    *   When the hail stopped, Lencho's soul was filled with deep sadness and despair. He felt that all their hard work had been for nothing and they would go hungry that year, as the hailstorm had left nothing behind, even worse than a plague of locusts.

---

### Part 2: Unwavering Faith and The Letter to God (Pages 5-6)

#### The Single Hope: God
*   **Solitary House:** Despite the total loss, the hearts of all who lived in that solitary house clung to a "single hope: help from God."
*   **Proverbial Wisdom:** Lencho reminds himself and his family, "Don’t be so upset... no one dies of hunger," a common saying that reflects their deep-seated belief in divine providence.
*   **Lencho's Faith in God:** All through the night, Lencho thought only of this one hope. He believed God's eyes "see everything, even what is deep in one’s conscience" (an inner sense of right and wrong).

#### Lencho: A Man of Hard Work and Faith
*   **Character Description:** Lencho is described as "an ox of a man," signifying his immense physical strength and tireless hard work in the fields.
*   **Literacy:** Despite his arduous farming life, he possessed the ability to write.

#### The Letter to God
*   **Decision:** The following Sunday, at daybreak, Lencho decides to write a letter, which he would personally carry to town and mail.
*   **Content of the Letter:**
    *   He addresses it directly to "God."
    *   He explains his desperate situation: "if you don’t help me, my family and I will go hungry this year."
    *   He asks for **a hundred pesos** (currency of several Latin American countries) to "sow my field again and to live until the crop comes," detailing the reason: "because the hailstorm...."
*   **Mailing the Letter:** He writes 'To God' on the envelope, puts the letter inside, and, still troubled, goes to town. At the post office, he affixes a stamp and drops it into the mailbox.

---

### Oral Comprehension Check (Page 6)

1.  **Who or what did Lencho have faith in? What did he do?**
    *   Lencho had complete and unwavering faith in God.
    *   After his crops were destroyed, he decided to write a letter directly to God, asking for a hundred pesos to replant his field and support his family until the next harvest.
2.  **Who read the letter?**
    *   One of the post office employees, who was a postman, found the letter addressed to "God." He read it and then showed it to the postmaster.
3.  **What did the postmaster do then?**
    *   The postmaster, initially amused, soon became serious and deeply impressed by Lencho's immense faith. To preserve Lencho's faith in God, he decided to answer the letter himself. He collected money from his employees, gave a part of his own salary, and asked friends for contributions as an "act of charity." He managed to collect a little more than half of the requested amount (around seventy pesos) and sent it to Lencho, signing the accompanying letter as "God."

---

### Part 3: The Postmaster's Charity and Lencho's Second Letter (Pages 6-7)

#### The Post Office's Reaction and the Postmaster's Plan
*   **Postman's Amusement:** A postman, who also helped at the post office, found the letter addressed "To God." He laughed heartily, having never encountered such an address in his career, and showed it to his boss.
*   **Postmaster's Character:** The postmaster is described as a "fat, amiable (friendly and pleasant) fellow."
*   **Postmaster's Reaction:** He initially laughed but then immediately turned serious, deeply moved by Lencho's profound faith. He exclaimed, "What faith! I wish I had the faith of the man who wrote this letter. Starting up a correspondence with God!"
*   **The Resolution:** To "not to shake the writer’s faith in God," the postmaster decided to answer the letter.
*   **The Challenge:** He realized that answering it required "something more than goodwill, ink and paper"—it required money.
*   **The Collection:** He stuck to his resolution. He asked for money from his employees, contributed a part of his own salary, and persuaded several friends to donate "for an act of charity."
*   **The Outcome:** He couldn't gather the full hundred pesos but managed to collect "a little more than half" (implied to be seventy pesos). He put the money in an envelope addressed to Lencho and, as a signature, wrote only "God" on the accompanying letter.

#### Lencho's Reaction to the Money
*   **Unwavering Confidence:** The following Sunday, Lencho arrived early to check for a letter. He showed "not the slightest surprise on seeing the money; such was his confidence." This highlights the depth of his faith – he fully expected God to respond.
*   **Anger, Not Gratitude:** However, when he counted the money, he became angry. His logic was simple: "God could not have made a mistake, nor could he have denied Lencho what he had requested."

#### The Second Letter to God
*   **Immediate Action:** Angry, Lencho went to the window to ask for paper and ink.
*   **Writing the Letter:** He wrote with much "wrinkling of his brow," indicating his effort and frustration.
*   **Content of the Second Letter:**
    *   Addressed to "God."
    *   He states: "Of the money that I asked for, only seventy pesos reached me."
    *   He demands the rest: "Send me the rest, since I need it very much."
    *   **The Irony:** He adds a crucial instruction: "But don’t send it to me through the mail because the post office employees are a bunch of crooks." He signs it "Lencho."
*   **Mailing:** He bought a stamp, licked it, affixed it with a punch of his fist, and dropped the letter into the mailbox. The postmaster immediately went to open it.

---

### Oral Comprehension Check (Page 7)

1.  **Was Lencho surprised to find a letter for him with money in it?**
    *   No, Lencho was not surprised at all to find a letter with money. His confidence and faith in God were so strong that he expected God to send him the money.
2.  **What made him angry?**
    *   Lencho became angry when he counted the money and found only seventy pesos, instead of the hundred he had requested. He believed that God could not make a mistake or deny him his request.

## 4. Textbook Questions: Thinking about the Text

Here are detailed answers to the questions based on the story:

1.  **Who does Lencho have complete faith in? Which sentences in the story tell you this?**
    *   Lencho has complete and unwavering faith in **God**.
    *   The sentences that reveal this are:
        *   "But in the hearts of all who lived in that solitary house in the middle of the valley, there was a single hope: help from God."
        *   "All through the night, Lencho thought only of his one hope: the help of God, whose eyes, as he had been instructed, see everything, even what is deep in one’s conscience."
        *   "Lencho showed not the slightest surprise on seeing the money; such was his confidence."
        *   "God could not have made a mistake, nor could he have denied Lencho what he had requested."

2.  **Why does the postmaster send money to Lencho? Why does he sign the letter ‘God’?**
    *   The postmaster sends money to Lencho **to preserve Lencho's profound faith in God**. He was deeply impressed by the farmer's unquestioning belief and did not want to shatter it. He saw it as a noble act of charity and humanity.
    *   He signs the letter ‘God’ because he wants Lencho to believe that the money truly came from God, thus maintaining his illusion and reinforcing his faith. This way, Lencho wouldn't know it was an act of human kindness.

3.  **Did Lencho try to find out who had sent the money to him? Why/Why not?**
    *   No, Lencho did not try to find out who had sent the money to him.
    *   This is because his faith in God was absolute. He had no doubt that the money could only have come from God, in response to his prayer. For him, there was no other possible sender; questioning it would be to question his very faith.

4.  **Who does Lencho think has taken the rest of the money? What is the irony in the situation? (Remember that the irony of a situation is an unexpected aspect of it. An ironic situation is strange or amusing because it is the opposite of what is expected.)**
    *   Lencho thinks that the **post office employees** have taken the rest of the money. He refers to them as "a bunch of crooks."
    *   **The irony in the situation is profound:** The very people (the post office employees and the postmaster) who went out of their way, sacrificing their own money and time, to help Lencho and preserve his faith in God, are the ones he accuses of being thieves. Lencho, in his blind faith and simple understanding, curses his saviours, unaware of their generous act. The unexpected outcome is that the beneficiaries of charity accuse their benefactors.

5.  **Are there people like Lencho in the real world? What kind of a person would you say he is? You may select appropriate words from the box to answer the question: greedy, naive, stupid, ungrateful, selfish, comical, unquestioning.**
    *   While perhaps not common to the extreme shown in the story, people with unwavering faith and simple beliefs, often leading to certain limitations in their understanding of the world, do exist in the real world.
    *   Based on the story, Lencho can be described as:
        *   **Naive:** He has a childlike simplicity and innocent belief that God will directly answer his letter and fulfill his exact request. He lacks worldly wisdom.
        *   **Unquestioning:** His faith in God is absolute, without any doubt or skepticism. He doesn't question the source of the money.
        *   **Ungrateful:** He accuses the post office employees, his actual benefactors, of being "crooks" without any hesitation or hint of gratitude for the money he did receive.
        *   **Comical:** His extreme faith and his accusations against the very people who helped him can appear humorous to the reader, creating a sense of dramatic irony.
        *   He is **not greedy** in the sense of wanting excessive wealth; he only asked for what he needed to survive. He is **not stupid**, as he is literate and a hardworking farmer. He is **not necessarily selfish**, as his concern is for his family's survival, though his lack of empathy for the post office workers could be perceived as self-centered.

6.  **There are two kinds of conflict in the story: between humans and nature, and between humans themselves. How are these conflicts illustrated?**
    *   **1. Conflict between Humans and Nature:**
        *   This conflict is clearly illustrated at the beginning of the story. Lencho, a farmer, is entirely dependent on nature for his livelihood. He initially anticipates a good harvest, eagerly hoping for rain.
        *   However, nature turns hostile with the **hailstorm**, which completely destroys his crops and livelihood. This devastating event represents nature's power and its indifference to human suffering, leading to Lencho's despair and fear of hunger. His hard work is undone by the natural disaster.
    *   **2. Conflict between Humans Themselves:**
        *   This conflict is primarily an **ironic one** and arises from a misunderstanding. It's not an open, hostile conflict but a perceived one.
        *   On one side are the **postmaster and his employees**, who embody human kindness, empathy, and charity. They unite to help Lencho, respecting his faith.
        *   On the other side is **Lencho**, who, due to his unwavering and naive faith in God, completely misunderstands the human act of kindness. He perceives the discrepancy in the money received (70 vs. 100 pesos) as a betrayal, not by God, but by the very people who helped him. He accuses the "post office employees" of being "a bunch of crooks," creating an ironic conflict where the saviours are seen as villains. This conflict highlights the gap between perception and reality, and the potential for misunderstanding even good intentions.

## 5. Working With Language

### I. Storm Vocabulary

*   **Hailstones:** Small balls of ice that fall like rain.
*   **Hailstorm:** A storm in which hailstones fall.
*   **Storm:** Bad weather with strong winds, rain, thunder, and lightning.

Here's a match of the storm names with their descriptions:

1.  A violent tropical storm in which strong winds move in a circle: **Cyclone**
2.  An extremely strong wind : **Gale**
3.  A violent tropical storm with very strong winds, especially in the Caribbean Sea: **Hurricane**
4.  A violent rotating column of air extending from a thunderstorm to the ground: **Tornado**
5.  A violent tropical storm, especially in the West Pacific Ocean: **Typhoon**
6.  A current of air moving rapidly in a spiral motion: **Whirlwind**
