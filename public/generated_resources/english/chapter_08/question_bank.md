## 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

**Instructions:** Answer the following questions in 30-40 words.

1.  **Why did Prince Siddhartha leave his home and family?**
    **Model Answer:** Prince Siddhartha, heretofore shielded from the world's sufferings, was deeply moved by the sights of a sick man, an aged man, a funeral procession, and a monk begging. These experiences prompted him to leave his home to seek enlightenment concerning the sorrows he had witnessed.

2.  **Describe Kisa Gotami's state of mind after her son's death.**
    **Model Answer:** Kisa Gotami was overwhelmed with grief and despair after her only son died. She lost her senses and carried her dead child to neighbours, desperately seeking medicine to bring him back to life, refusing to accept the reality of death.

3.  **What was the Buddha's purpose in asking Kisa Gotami to procure mustard seeds under a specific condition?**
    **Model Answer:** The Buddha's purpose was not to get mustard seeds, but to make Kisa Gotami realize the universality of death through her own experience. By asking her to find seeds from a house untouched by death, he guided her towards understanding that sorrow and loss are common to all.

4.  **Why couldn't Kisa Gotami find the mustard seeds as per the Buddha's condition?**
    **Model Answer:** Kisa Gotami couldn't find the mustard seeds because every house she visited had experienced the death of a loved one – a son, daughter, father, mother, husband, or friend. This made her realize that death is an inevitable part of every family's existence.

5.  **What significant observation led Kisa Gotami to her ultimate realization about life and death?**
    **Model Answer:** While sitting by the wayside, Kisa Gotami observed the city lights flickering up and being extinguished. This observation made her reflect on the transient nature of human lives, which similarly flicker up and are extinguished, leading her to understand the commonality of death.

6.  **How does the Buddha describe the nature of human life in his sermon?**
    **Model Answer:** In his sermon, the Buddha describes human life as troubled, brief, and combined with pain. He states that mortals are always in danger of death, comparing their lives to ripe fruits destined to fall and earthen vessels certain to break.

7.  **What does the Buddha mean by "draw out the arrow of lamentation"?**
    **Model Answer:** By "draw out the arrow of lamentation," the Buddha means to actively remove oneself from the overwhelming sorrow, complaint, and grief associated with loss. It implies accepting death, letting go of excessive mourning, and finding inner peace.

8.  **According to the Buddha, what is the consequence of grieving excessively for the dead?**
    **Model Answer:** The Buddha teaches that weeping and grieving excessively will not bring peace of mind; instead, it will increase one's pain and make the body suffer, causing sickness and paleness. Moreover, it cannot bring the dead back to life.

9.  **What did Kisa Gotami understand the second time that she failed to understand the first time?**
    **Model Answer:** The second time, Kisa Gotami understood that death is universal and an inevitable part of human existence. She realized the selfish nature of her grief and that lamenting over it would not bring back her son, a truth she couldn't grasp initially due to her overwhelming sorrow.

10. **What is the primary message the Buddha conveys to those afflicted with sorrow and grief?**
    **Model Answer:** The Buddha conveys that grief and sorrow are natural but excessive lamentation is futile and harmful. He teaches that by accepting the inevitability of death and letting go of selfish attachment, one can overcome sorrow and attain peace of mind and wisdom.

## 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

**Instructions:** Answer the following questions in 100-120 words. Outline answers are provided.

1.  **Trace the journey of Siddhartha Gautama to becoming the Buddha. What prompted his quest for enlightenment?**
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** Siddhartha Gautama, a prince, lived a life of luxury, shielded from suffering.
    *   **The Four Sights:** At age twenty-five, he chanced upon a sick man, an aged man, a funeral procession, and finally, a monk begging for alms. These deeply moved him.
    *   **Quest for Enlightenment:** The sights made him realize the universality of suffering (sorrow, old age, death) and the peace of the monk. He decided to leave his princely life to seek the truth about these sorrows.
    *   **Wandering and Meditation:** He wandered for seven years, searching for answers.
    *   **Enlightenment:** He finally sat under a peepal tree, vowing not to rise until enlightened. After seven days, he attained enlightenment.
    *   **Transformation:** He became known as the Buddha (the Awakened or the Enlightened) and began sharing his wisdom.
    *   **Conclusion:** His personal encounter with suffering propelled him to understand the human condition and find a path to liberation.

2.  **Elaborate on Kisa Gotami's transformation. How did the Buddha's unique teaching method help her accept death?**
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Initial State:** Kisa Gotami was consumed by selfish grief after her son's death, refusing to accept reality and desperately seeking a cure.
    *   **Buddha's Indirect Method:** The Buddha did not directly preach but gave her a seemingly simple task: to find mustard seeds from a house where no one had ever died.
    *   **Experiential Learning:** Her journey from house to house was crucial. She encountered universal sorrow and realized that death spares no one.
    *   **Moment of Realization:** Watching the city lights flicker and extinguish, she had an epiphany: human lives are transient, and death is common to all. Her grief was selfish.
    *   **Acceptance and Wisdom:** This experience transformed her perspective. She surrendered her personal sorrow and accepted the truth of impermanence.
    *   **Conclusion:** The Buddha's method of making her discover the truth herself, rather than simply being told, was highly effective in leading her from despair to wisdom and acceptance.

3.  **Summarize the core message of the Buddha's sermon about life, death, and grief. How is it relevant even today?**
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** The sermon at Benares contains the fundamental truths about human existence as taught by the Buddha.
    *   **Universality of Death:** Life is troubled, brief, and painful. All mortals, regardless of age, wisdom, or status, are subject to death, like ripe fruits falling or earthen vessels breaking. No one can escape it, nor can relatives save others.
    *   **Futility of Excessive Grief:** Weeping and grieving will not bring peace of mind; instead, it will increase suffering, make one sick, and cannot revive the dead.
    *   **Path to Peace:** The wise do not grieve because they understand the terms of the world. To obtain peace, one must "draw out the arrow of lamentation, complaint, and grief," becoming composed and overcoming sorrow.
    *   **Relevance Today:** The message remains profoundly relevant in modern times, offering a framework for coping with loss, emphasizing acceptance, resilience, and finding inner peace amidst inevitable suffering. It encourages a shift from self-centered grief to a broader understanding of human experience.
    *   **Conclusion:** The sermon provides timeless wisdom on accepting life's impermanence and finding strength in understanding.

4.  **Discuss the role of suffering in shaping Kisa Gotami's understanding and eventually leading her to wisdom.**
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Initial Suffering:** Kisa Gotami's intense suffering from the loss of her only son was the catalyst. It drove her to desperate measures, initially blinding her to reason.
    *   **Search for Relief:** Her suffering led her to seek help from others and eventually to the Buddha, demonstrating her profound need for relief.
    *   **Suffering as a Teacher:** The Buddha used her suffering as a teaching tool. Instead of alleviating it directly, he directed it towards a path of self-discovery.
    *   **Empathy and Universality:** As she went from house to house, her suffering allowed her to connect with the universal suffering of others who had also experienced loss. This broadened her perspective beyond her personal pain.
    *   **Realization through Contrast:** Her intense initial suffering, contrasted with the universal experience of death, finally allowed her to perceive the truth. Her individual grief transformed into an understanding of the collective human condition.
    *   **Wisdom through Acceptance:** By accepting death as an inevitable part of life, she transcended her "selfish grief" and attained wisdom, understanding the path to immortality (freedom from sorrow).
    *   **Conclusion:** Kisa Gotami's suffering was not merely an event but a transformative journey that ultimately led her to profound spiritual insight and peace.

5.  **"Death is common to all; yet in this valley of desolation there is a path that leads him to immortality who has surrendered all selfishness." Explain this statement in the context of the chapter, highlighting how Kisa Gotami and the Buddha elaborate on it.**
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** This statement, Kisa Gotami's realization, encapsulates the core teaching of the chapter regarding the human condition and the path to peace.
    *   **"Death is common to all":**
        *   **Kisa Gotami's Realization:** She discovers this experientially while collecting mustard seeds. Every house has lost someone, illustrating that death is a universal experience, not just her personal tragedy.
        *   **Buddha's Sermon:** He elaborates on this, stating that life is brief, troubled, and painful, and all mortals (young, old, wise, foolish) are subject to death, comparing them to ripe fruits and earthen vessels.
    *   **"Valley of desolation":** This refers to the profound grief and sorrow that accompanies loss, a state Kisa Gotami was deeply immersed in.
    *   **"Path to immortality who has surrendered all selfishness":**
        *   **Kisa's Transformation:** She initially grieved selfishly, wanting her son back. Her realization leads her to see the futility of this attachment and the need to let go.
        *   **Buddha's Teaching:** He advocates drawing out the "arrow of lamentation, complaint, and grief." By accepting the terms of the world and overcoming sorrow (which stems from attachment/selfishness), one becomes "free from sorrow, and blessed," achieving a state of peace that transcends mortal suffering—a form of spiritual immortality or liberation.
    *   **Conclusion:** The statement signifies the journey from self-centered grief and denial to universal understanding and acceptance, leading to ultimate peace and freedom from the cycle of sorrow.
