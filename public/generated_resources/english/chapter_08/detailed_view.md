# NCERT Class 10 English: "The Sermon at Benares" - Detailed Chapter Notes

## Chapter Overview

"The Sermon at Benares" is a profound and poignant narrative that introduces the life journey of Gautama Buddha and presents his first sermon. The chapter intertwines the biographical account of Siddhartha Gautama's enlightenment with the touching story of Kisa Gotami, a grieving mother, to deliver a timeless message about the universality of death, the futility of personal grief, and the path to true peace. It explores themes of suffering, impermanence, wisdom, and compassion.

## 1. BEFORE YOU READ - Activity Discussion

This section prepares the reader for the chapter by introducing key concepts and vocabulary.

### Question 1: What is a sermon? Is it different from a lecture or a talk? Can this word also be used in a negative way or as a joke?

*   **What is a sermon?**
    *   A **sermon** is a discourse or lecture on a religious or moral subject, usually delivered by a member of the clergy as part of a church service. Its primary aim is to instruct, inspire, or admonish the audience on spiritual or ethical matters.
*   **Is it different from a lecture or a talk?**
    *   **Sermon:**
        *   **Purpose:** Primarily religious or moral instruction, spiritual guidance, ethical teaching.
        *   **Context:** Often delivered in a religious setting (church, temple, mosque).
        *   **Tone:** Typically solemn, authoritative, often inspirational or persuasive towards a spiritual truth.
        *   **Content:** Focuses on sacred texts, doctrines, or moral principles.
    *   **Lecture:**
        *   **Purpose:** To impart knowledge or information on an academic, scientific, or general subject.
        *   **Context:** Delivered in educational institutions, conferences, or public forums.
        *   **Tone:** Informative, analytical, objective.
        *   **Content:** Based on research, facts, theories.
    *   **Talk:**
        *   **Purpose:** More informal; can be on any subject, often sharing personal experiences, insights, or opinions.
        *   **Context:** Can be anywhere – casual gatherings, small events, or even formal settings with an informal approach.
        *   **Tone:** Conversational, relaxed, sometimes humorous.
        *   **Content:** Highly varied.
    *   **Key Difference:** While all involve speaking to an audience, a sermon is specifically religious/moral, a lecture is academic/informative, and a talk is more general and informal.
*   **Can this word also be used in a negative way or as a joke?**
    *   Yes, absolutely. When someone gives a long, preachy, or unsolicited piece of advice, often perceived as nagging, it can be jokingly or negatively referred to as a "sermon."
    *   **Example:** "My mother gave me a long sermon about keeping my room tidy." (Here, it implies a lengthy, lecturing, perhaps unwelcome piece of advice.)
    *   **Example:** "Oh, don't start with your usual sermon on punctuality!" (Used to express annoyance at someone's repetitive lecturing.)

## 2. GAUTAMA BUDDHA (563 B.C. – 483 B.C.): The Enlightened One

This section provides a concise biography of the Buddha, setting the context for his teachings.

*   **Early Life as Prince Siddhartha Gautama:**
    *   Born in northern India as a prince.
    *   At age twelve, sent for schooling in Hindu sacred scriptures.
    *   Returned home four years later and married a princess.
    *   Had a son and lived for ten years in royal luxury, shielded from the world's sufferings.
*   **The Four Sights and Renunciation:**
    *   At about twenty-five, while hunting, he **chanced upon** (came across by chance) four significant sights:
        1.  A sick man
        2.  An aged man
        3.  A funeral procession (a dead man)
        4.  A monk begging for alms
    *   These sights profoundly moved him, revealing the pervasive nature of suffering in the world.
    *   He immediately left his royal life to **seek enlightenment** (a state of high spiritual knowledge) concerning the sorrows he had witnessed.
*   **The Path to Enlightenment:**
    *   Wandered for seven years in search of truth.
    *   Finally sat down under a peepal tree, vowing not to leave until enlightenment came.
    *   Achieved enlightenment after seven days.
    *   Renamed the tree the **Bodhi Tree** (Tree of Wisdom).
*   **The Buddha (The Awakened/Enlightened):**
    *   Began to teach and share his new understandings.
    *   Became known as "The Buddha."
*   **First Sermon at Benares:**
    *   Preached his first sermon at the city of Benares, considered one of the holiest **dipping places** (bathing places) on the River Ganges.
    *   This sermon, preserved through time, reflects his wisdom about an **inscrutable** (something which cannot be understood) kind of suffering – the suffering caused by death.

## 3. Kisa Gotami's Story: The Lesson of Universal Suffering

This narrative illustrates the Buddha's method of teaching through experience.

*   **The Tragedy:**
    *   Kisa Gotami had an only son, who tragically died.
    *   Overwhelmed by grief, she lost her senses and carried her dead child to all her neighbours, pleading for medicine to cure him.
    *   People dismissed her as insane, stating the boy was dead.
*   **Seeking Help from the Wise:**
    *   Eventually, she met a man who directed her to Sakyamuni, the Buddha, stating he knew a physician who could help.
    *   Kisa Gotami **repaired to** (went to) the Buddha, crying for medicine to cure her son.
*   **Buddha's Condition - The Mustard Seed:**
    *   The Buddha, instead of offering direct medicine, set a unique condition:
        *   She needed to **procure** (obtain) a handful of mustard-seed.
        *   Crucially, the mustard-seed had to be taken *from a house where no one had lost a child, husband, parent, or friend*.
*   **Kisa's Vain Quest:**
    *   Filled with initial joy and hope, Kisa Gotami went from house to house.
    *   People pitied her and readily offered mustard-seed.
    *   However, when she asked if death had visited their family (a son, daughter, father, or mother), every household had the same sorrowful answer: "Alas! the living are few, but the dead are many. Do not remind us of our deepest grief."
    *   There was **no house but some beloved one had died in it** (meaning, every single house had experienced death).
*   **The Realisation:**
    *   Kisa Gotami became weary and hopeless.
    *   She sat by the wayside, watching the city lights flicker up and extinguish, mirroring the transient nature of human lives.
    *   As darkness reigned, she reflected: "How selfish am I in my grief! Death is common to all; yet in this **valley of desolation** (an area filled with deep sorrow) there is a path that leads him to immortality who has surrendered all selfishness."
    *   She understood that her individual sorrow was part of a universal experience and that clinging to her grief was selfish.

## 4. The Buddha's Sermon: The Inevitability of Death and the Path to Peace

This section contains the core teachings delivered by the Buddha, elaborating on the insights Kisa Gotami gained.

*   **The Nature of Mortals:**
    *   The Buddha states that the life of **mortals** (those bound to die) in this world is "troubled and brief and combined with pain."
    *   There is no escape from death for those who are born; old age inevitably leads to death.
    *   This is the inherent nature of all living beings.
*   **Analogies for Impermanence:**
    *   **Ripe fruits:** Just as ripe fruits are in danger of falling early, so too are mortals always in danger of death from the moment of birth.
    *   **Earthen vessels:** All earthen vessels made by a potter eventually break, similarly, the life of mortals ends.
*   **Universality of Death:**
    *   Death spares no one: "Both young and adult, both those who are fools and those who are wise, all fall into the power of death; all are **subject to** (under the power of) death."
*   **The Futility of Lamentation:**
    *   When overcome by death, no one can save another: "a father cannot save his son, nor kinsmen their relations."
    *   **Mark!** (Pay attention!) Relatives watch and mourn, yet one by one, mortals are carried off "like an ox that is led to the slaughter."
    *   The world is universally **afflicted with** (affected by suffering) death and decay.
*   **The Wisdom of Non-Grief:**
    *   The wise do not grieve because they understand the "terms of the world" – the reality of death.
    *   Weeping and grieving *will not* bring peace of mind; instead, it will only intensify pain and cause physical suffering (making one "sick and pale").
    *   The dead cannot be saved by the **lamentation** (expression of sorrow) of the living.
*   **The Path to Peace of Mind:**
    *   "He who seeks peace should draw out the arrow of lamentation, and complaint, and grief."
    *   One who has removed this 'arrow' of sorrow and has become **composed** (calm and controlled) will achieve peace of mind.
    *   Ultimately, "he who has overcome all sorrow will become free from sorrow, and be blessed."

## 5. Textbook Questions - Solved Problems

### Working with the Text (Page 114)

**Question 1: When her son dies, Kisa Gotami goes from house to house. What does she ask for? Does she get it? Why not?**
**Answer:** When her son dies, Kisa Gotami goes from house to house asking for medicine that will cure her dead child. She does not get it. She fails to get it because death is an irreversible process, and no medicine can bring a dead person back to life. The people she approached understood this, which is why they said, "She has lost her senses. The boy is dead."

**Question 2: Kisa Gotami again goes from house to house after she speaks with the Buddha. What does she ask for, the second time around? Does she get it? Why not?**
**Answer:** The second time, after speaking with the Buddha, Kisa Gotami goes from house to house asking for a handful of mustard-seed from a house where no one has lost a child, husband, parent, or friend. She does not get it. She fails because she discovers that there is no such house. Every single household she visits has experienced the loss of a loved one – a child, a husband, a parent, or a friend.

**Question 3: What does Kisa Gotami understand the second time that she failed to understand the first time? Was this what the Buddha wanted her to understand?**
**Answer:** The first time, Kisa Gotami was consumed by her personal grief, believing her suffering to be unique and seeking a miraculous cure for death. The second time, through her experience of visiting every house, she understood the universality of death. She realized that death is an inevitable part of life, common to all mortals, and that her individual sorrow was not an isolated incident but a shared human experience. She understood that clinging to her grief was "selfish" in the face of this universal truth. Yes, this was exactly what the Buddha wanted her to understand – that sorrow and suffering are inherent in life, and acceptance of this reality is the first step towards peace.

**Question 4: Why do you think Kisa Gotami understood this only the second time? In what way did the Buddha change her understanding?**
**Answer:** Kisa Gotami understood this only the second time because the Buddha employed a practical, experiential method of teaching. The first time, she was too deeply immersed in her personal grief to comprehend abstract philosophical truths about death. She needed a tangible experience to internalize the lesson.
The Buddha changed her understanding by:
1.  **Shifting her focus:** Instead of directly telling her that her son couldn't be revived, he gave her a seemingly simple task.
2.  **Facilitating self-discovery:** By sending her to every house for the mustard seed under a specific condition, he allowed her to discover the truth for herself. This personal journey made the realization far more impactful and undeniable than if he had merely told her.
3.  **Broadening her perspective:** Her interactions with numerous families revealed that death is not a personal tragedy unique to her, but a universal sorrow experienced by everyone. This shifted her from selfish, individual grief to an understanding of shared human mortality.

**Question 5: How do you usually understand the idea of ‘selfishness’? Do you agree with Kisa Gotami that she was being ‘selfish in her grief ’?**
**Answer:**
*   **Understanding of 'Selfishness':** Usually, 'selfishness' is understood as prioritizing one's own desires, needs, or comfort above others, often to the detriment of others or without considering their feelings. It implies a lack of concern for others and an excessive focus on oneself.
*   **Kisa Gotami's 'Selfishness':** In the context of the story, Kisa Gotami realizes she was being 'selfish in her grief' not in the conventional sense of harming others, but in her *preoccupation with her own sorrow to the exclusion of the universal truth*. She was so absorbed in her personal suffering that she failed to see that death is a common fate for all, and that millions suffer similar losses. Her grief was isolating her and preventing her from accepting reality. It was "selfish" in the sense that it made her blind to the collective human experience of mortality and kept her from finding a path to peace that lies in acceptance. I agree with her realization, as it represents a profound shift from self-pity to a broader, more compassionate understanding of life and death.

### I. Rephrasing old-fashioned language (Page 115)

Here are the phrases from the text rephrased into more current language:

*   **give thee medicine for thy child**
    *   Give you medicine for your child.
*   **Pray tell me**
    *   Please tell me. / Could you tell me?
*   **Kisa repaired to the Buddha**
    *   Kisa went to the Buddha. / Kisa approached the Buddha.
*   **there was no house but someone had died in it**
    *   There was no house where someone had not died. / Every house had experienced a death.
*   **kinsmen**
    *   Relatives. / Family members.
*   **Mark!**
    *   Listen! / Pay attention! / Note this!

### II. Combining Sentences using semicolons or dashes (Page 115)

The given sentence: "For there is not any means by which those who have been born can avoid dying; after reaching old age there is death; of such a nature are living beings."

**Breaking up into three simple sentences:**

1.  There is no means by which those who have been born can avoid dying.
2.  After reaching old age, there is death.
3.  Such is the nature of living beings.

**Rhythm comparison:**
The **single sentence using semicolons** generally has a **better rhythm** when read aloud, especially in a formal or oratorical context like a sermon.
*   The semicolons create a natural pause, connecting the ideas logically without completely breaking the flow. They build a cumulative effect, reinforcing the central message of inevitability. The ideas flow smoothly from one to the next, creating a more cohesive and impactful statement.
*   The three simple sentences, while grammatically correct, can sound a bit more abrupt and disjointed when read consecutively, losing some of the gravitas and rhetorical power of the original construction. The semicolons provide a sense of progression and consequence that individual sentences might lack.

## 7. Thinking about the Text - Comparing with Modern Texts on Grief (Page 115-117)

This section encourages critical thinking by comparing ancient wisdom with modern perspectives on grief.

### Summary of the Two Recent Texts on Grief:

**I. A Guide to Coping with the Death of a Loved One:**
*   **Definition of Grief:** A natural emotion experienced after loss or significant change.
*   **Common Feelings/Behaviors:** Lists a range of common responses to grief, including sadness, anger, guilt/self-reproach, anxiety, loneliness, fatigue, and disbelief. It notes that grief is unique and personal, but these feelings are common.
*   **Helping Others:** Advises understanding that grief is expressed in various ways. Suggests reaching out with patience and care, respecting that some may not want help, while others will want to talk.

**II. Good Grief (Amitai Etzioni):**
*   **Personal Experience:** The author shares two personal tragedies: his wife's death and his son's sudden death.
*   **Challenges Conventional Stages of Grief:** Directly challenges the idea of fixed "stages" of grief (like denial being the first stage) as taught by a school psychologist. His own experience and his family's response (anger, not denial) contradict this rigid framework. This text suggests that grief is highly personal and does not always follow a prescribed pattern.

### Comparison with Buddha's Sermon and Discussion:

*   **Buddha's Ideas and Way of Teaching:**
    *   **Core Message:** Acceptance of death as universal and inevitable. The futility of clinging to personal grief. The path to peace lies in drawing out the "arrow of lamentation" and achieving inner composure.
    *   **Method:** Experiential (Kisa Gotami's quest), allegorical (ripe fruits, earthen vessels), direct philosophical instruction.
    *   **Focus:** On overcoming suffering through wisdom and detachment from attachment, leading to a state of being "free from sorrow, and be blessed."

*   **Comparison and Discussion:**

    1.  **Universality of Grief:** Both Buddha and the modern texts acknowledge the profound impact of loss and grief. Buddha highlights the *universal experience* of death as a fundamental truth, while the modern texts focus on the *universal feelings* associated with grief.

    2.  **Nature of Grief:**
        *   **Buddha:** Views intense, clinging grief as a form of suffering that *prevents* peace and worsens one's condition ("his pain will be the greater and his body will suffer"). His solution is acceptance and non-attachment.
        *   **Modern Texts (I):** Describe grief as a "natural emotion" with a broad range of feelings (sadness, anger, guilt, etc.). They normalize these feelings as part of the process.
        *   **Modern Texts (II):** Emphasizes the *personal and non-linear* nature of grief, challenging rigid prescriptive models.

    3.  **Coping Mechanisms/Help:**
        *   **Buddha:** Offers a philosophical and spiritual path: understanding the impermanence of life, accepting death, and releasing lamentation to attain peace. This is an internal, wisdom-based approach.
        *   **Modern Texts (I):** Offer psychological understanding of common feelings and practical advice on how to *help others* (patience, listening, support). This focuses on external support and emotional processing.
        *   **Modern Texts (II):** Highlights that there is "no set" way to grieve, implying the importance of individual processing and rejecting external impositions on the grieving process.

    4.  **Continuity of Meaning:**
        *   **Yes, Buddha's ideas and way of teaching absolutely continue to hold meaning for us.** The fundamental truth he conveys – that death is inevitable and grief is a universal experience – remains unchanged. His teaching method, using an experiential journey to lead to self-discovery (as with Kisa Gotami), is incredibly insightful and still effective today. The concept of acceptance and finding peace amidst sorrow is a timeless quest.

    5.  **Have we found better ways to deal with grief?**
        *   **"Better" is subjective.** Modern psychology offers valuable contributions:
            *   **Validation of feelings:** Modern approaches validate a wider range of emotions (anger, guilt) as natural, whereas Buddha focuses more on overcoming them directly.
            *   **Individualized approach:** The idea that "grief is unique and personal" and "no set" way to grieve (Text II) is a significant advancement, recognizing diversity in human experience.
            *   **Support systems:** Modern texts emphasize the role of community support, therapy, and understanding from others, which complements the Buddha's internal wisdom path.
        *   **Complementary, not mutually exclusive:** It's more accurate to say that modern approaches *complement* Buddha's wisdom rather than replace it. Buddha's core message of acceptance and seeking inner peace provides a profound philosophical framework. Modern psychology adds practical tools for emotional processing, validation, and social support.
        *   For example, understanding that anger is a normal part of grief (modern) can help someone process it, and then, armed with that understanding, they can work towards the Buddha's goal of drawing out the 'arrow of lamentation' through acceptance.

**Conclusion:** The Buddha's wisdom provides a profound, timeless perspective on death and suffering, rooted in acceptance and the pursuit of inner peace. Modern texts offer valuable psychological insights into the diverse emotional landscape of grief and the importance of individual processing and supportive communities. Both perspectives are valuable and can work together to help individuals navigate the complex journey of loss.

## 8. Conclusion and Key Takeaways

*   **Universality of Death:** Death is an inescapable reality for all living beings, regardless of age, status, or wisdom.
*   **Futility of Clinging Grief:** Excessive personal grief and lamentation do not alleviate suffering or bring back the dead; instead, they increase pain and cause self-harm.
*   **The Path to Peace:** True peace and freedom from sorrow come through understanding the nature of the world, accepting impermanence, and releasing selfish attachment to personal grief.
*   **Experiential Learning:** The Buddha's method of teaching Kisa Gotami illustrates the power of self-discovery and direct experience in internalizing profound truths.
*   **Wisdom and Compassion:** The sermon is an act of profound compassion, guiding the suffering towards wisdom and inner tranquility.
