**Passage 1: Redox Reactions in Everyday Life**

Many processes in our daily lives involve chemical reactions where electrons are transferred between species. These are known as redox reactions. For instance, the rusting of iron is a common example where iron loses electrons (gets oxidized) and oxygen gains electrons (gets reduced). Similarly, batteries utilize controlled redox reactions to produce electrical energy. Combustion, the process of burning fuels, is also a rapid redox reaction. The type of reaction and the nature of the reactants and products can be understood by examining changes in oxidation states.

**Passage 2: Decomposition Reactions and Their Applications**

Decomposition reactions are those in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. These reactions often require energy in the form of heat (thermolysis), light (photolysis), or electricity (electrolysis). For example, the decomposition of calcium carbonate by heating produces calcium oxide and carbon dioxide gas. This reaction has significant industrial applications, such as in the production of quicklime. Electrolysis of water, on the other hand, produces hydrogen and oxygen gases, which can be used as fuels.

**Passage 3: Displacement Reactions and Reactivity Series**

Displacement reactions occur when a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. The reactivity series of metals is a list of metals arranged in order of their decreasing reactivity. For example, zinc is more reactive than copper. Therefore, when a zinc strip is placed in a copper sulphate solution, zinc displaces copper from the solution, forming zinc sulphate and copper metal. This reaction is often accompanied by a visible change in colour, as copper sulphate solution is blue and zinc sulphate solution is colourless.

2.  **(a) Both forward and backward rates increase, with the forward rate increasing more significantly.**
    *   **Explanation:** Decreasing the volume of a container containing gases at constant temperature increases the pressure and thus the concentration of all gaseous species. According to Le Chatelier's principle and collision theory, increasing the concentration of reactants increases the rate of reaction. In this equilibrium, the forward reaction has 2 moles of gas on the reactant side and 1 mole of gas on the product side ($\text{NO}_2 \rightarrow \text{N}_2\text{O}_4$). When the volume is halved, the concentrations (and partial pressures) of both $\text{NO}_2$ and $\text{N}_2\text{O}_4$ double. The rate of the forward reaction depends on $[\text{NO}_2]^2$, so it increases by a factor of $2^2 = 4$. The rate of the backward reaction depends on $[\text{N}_2\text{O}_4]$, so it increases by a factor of $2$. Thus, the forward rate increases more significantly, shifting the equilibrium towards products.

2.  **Prevention of Rusting of Iron:**

    Rusting is the oxidation of iron, which is a slow process of corrosion, forming hydrated iron(III) oxide. It requires the presence of oxygen and moisture. It is a redox reaction where iron is oxidized and oxygen is reduced.
    $\text{Fe} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{3+}$
    $2\text{Fe} + \frac{3}{2}\text{O}_2 \rightarrow \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3$
    $\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + \text{nH}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3.\text{nH}_2\text{O}$ (Rust)

    The principles of chemical reactions and reactivity series are applied to prevent rusting by:

    *   **Preventing Contact with Oxygen and Moisture:** This is the most direct approach.
        *   **Painting/Oiling/Greasing:** Applying a layer of paint, oil, or grease on the iron surface creates a barrier, preventing oxygen and moisture from reaching the metal. This physically isolates the iron. Example: Painting iron gates, tools.
        *   **Galvanization:** This involves coating iron with a layer of zinc. Zinc is more reactive than iron according to the reactivity series. If the zinc coating is scratched and iron is exposed, zinc still protects iron. This is because zinc preferentially oxidizes (acts as a sacrificial anode) in the presence of an electrolyte (like moisture). This sacrificial protection is a key application of the reactivity series. The reaction is:
            Anode (Zn): $\text{Zn(s)} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+}\text{(aq)} + 2\text{e}^-$
            Cathode (Fe exposed): Oxygen reduction occurs, consuming electrons released by Zinc.
            Even if the zinc layer is damaged, it corrodes instead of iron.
        *   **Electroplating:** Coating iron with less reactive metals like tin or chromium. Tin is less reactive than iron. If the tin layer is intact, it protects iron. However, if the tin layer is scratched and iron is exposed, iron corrodes preferentially in the presence of an electrolyte (forming a galvanic cell where Fe is the anode). This is why galvanization (with zinc) is superior to tin plating for preventing rust.
        *   **Alloying:** Making stainless steel by mixing iron with chromium and nickel. These metals form a protective oxide layer on the surface of stainless steel, which prevents further corrosion. The chromium and nickel also alter the crystal structure, making it less prone to rusting.
