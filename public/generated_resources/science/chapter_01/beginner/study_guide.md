### **Chemical Reactions and Equations: Easy Study Guide**

This chapter is all about how things change and what happens when they do! We'll learn about how new substances are made from old ones.

---

#### **1. What is this chapter about?**

This chapter explains how everyday changes, like cooking food or food getting digested, are actually *chemical reactions*. We'll learn how to describe these reactions using special "chemical languages" called equations.

---

#### **2. Key Terms**

*   **Chemical Reaction:** When one or more things change to make totally new things.
    *   *Analogy:* Like baking a cake – flour, eggs, and sugar *react* to become a delicious cake!
*   **Reactants:** The "ingredients" that go into a chemical reaction.
    *   *Analogy:* The flour, eggs, and sugar before you bake the cake.
*   **Products:** The "new things" that are made after a chemical reaction.
    *   *Analogy:* The finished cake!
*   **Chemical Equation:** A short way to show a chemical reaction using symbols.
    *   *Analogy:* Like a recipe written with symbols instead of words.
*   **Balanced Chemical Equation:** A chemical equation where the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides.
    *   *Analogy:* Making sure you have the exact same number of apples on both sides of a balancing scale.

---

#### **3. Main Points to Remember**

*   Chemical reactions happen all around us, in nature and in our bodies.
*   We can tell if a chemical reaction has happened by noticing:
    *   A change in how something looks (like color).
    *   If a gas is produced (bubbles!).
    *   If the temperature changes (gets hot or cold).
    *   If something changes its state (like a solid turning into a liquid).
*   A chemical reaction involves **reactants** turning into **products**.
*   We can write reactions using words (word-equations) or symbols (chemical equations).
*   A **chemical equation** uses the chemical formulas of the reactants and products.
*   The **law of conservation of mass** means that matter isn't lost or gained in a reaction. So, the number of atoms of each element must be the same before and after the reaction.
*   We need to **balance chemical equations** to make sure the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides. This is like making sure all the ingredients are accounted for.
*   To balance an equation, we add numbers (called **coefficients**) in front of the chemical formulas. **Never** change the small numbers within the formulas themselves.

---

#### **4. Simple Examples**

*   **Burning Magnesium Ribbon (Activity 1.1):**
    *   When you burn magnesium in air, it turns into a white powder (magnesium oxide).
    *   **Word-Equation:** Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium Oxide
    *   **Chemical Equation:** Mg + O₂ → MgO
    *   *This equation needs to be balanced!* Think of it like: 1 atom of Magnesium and 2 atoms of Oxygen on one side, but only 1 of each on the other.
    *   **Balanced Equation:** 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
        *   *Analogy:* You need two pieces of magnesium to react with one molecule of oxygen to make two units of magnesium oxide.

*   **Zinc + Acid (Activity 1.3):**
    *   When zinc granules are added to dilute acid, hydrogen gas bubbles up, and the flask might get warmer.
    *   **Word-Equation:** Zinc + Sulphuric Acid → Zinc Sulphate + Hydrogen
    *   **Chemical Equation:** Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂
    *   *This equation is already balanced!* You have the same number of Zn, H, S, and O atoms on both sides.

*   **Iron + Water:**
    *   Iron (like a rusty nail) reacts with water to form iron oxide (rust) and hydrogen gas.
    *   **Word-Equation:** Iron + Water → Iron Oxide + Hydrogen
    *   **Chemical Equation:** Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
    *   *This is unbalanced!* We have 1 Fe on the left and 3 on the right. We have 2 H on both sides, but 1 O on the left and 4 on the right.
    *   **To balance it (like the book shows):**
        *   We need more iron on the left: 3Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
        *   We need more oxygen on the left: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
        *   Now we have 8 hydrogen atoms on the left (4x2) and only 2 on the right. So we add a 4 in front of H₂:
        *   **Balanced Equation:** 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂
        *   *Analogy:* You need 3 pieces of iron, 4 units of water to make 1 unit of rust and 4 units of hydrogen gas.

#### **6. Remember This!**

*   **Chemical reactions change substances into new ones.**
*   **Balancing chemical equations makes sure matter isn't lost or created.**
*   **Coefficients are added in front of formulas to balance equations; formulas themselves are NEVER changed.**
