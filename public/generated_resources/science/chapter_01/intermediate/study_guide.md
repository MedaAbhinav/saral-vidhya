# NCERT Class 10 Science Study Guide: Chemical Reactions and Equations

## 1. Chapter Overview

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of chemical reactions and equations. We'll explore how to identify chemical changes, represent them using word equations and chemical equations, and understand the importance of balancing these equations based on the Law of Conservation of Mass. The chapter also touches upon the conditions that can influence chemical reactions.

## 2. Key Concepts (with explanations)

*   **Physical Change vs. Chemical Change:**
    *   **Physical Change:** A temporary change where the chemical composition of the substance does not change. Examples: melting of ice, boiling of water, dissolving sugar in water.
    *   **Chemical Change:** A permanent change where a new substance with new properties is formed. Examples: burning of wood, rusting of iron, digestion of food. Chemical reactions are the basis of chemical changes.

*   **Identifying a Chemical Reaction:** We can infer that a chemical reaction has occurred if we observe any of the following:
    *   **Change in State:** Solid to liquid, liquid to gas, etc. (e.g., burning magnesium ribbon forms a solid powder).
    *   **Change in Colour:** A distinct colour change indicates a new substance has formed (e.g., reaction between lead nitrate and potassium iodide).
    *   **Evolution of a Gas:** Bubbles of gas are produced (e.g., reaction of zinc with acid produces hydrogen gas).
    *   **Change in Temperature:** The reaction might release heat (exothermic) or absorb heat (endothermic), causing a change in temperature (e.g., reaction of zinc with acid can make the container warm).

*   **Chemical Reactions:** The process in which one or more substances (reactants) change to form one or more new substances (products) with different properties.

*   **Word Equations:** A way to represent a chemical reaction in words. It shows the reactants on the left side and the products on the right side, separated by an arrow (→) indicating the direction of the reaction.
    *   Example: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide

*   **Chemical Equations:** A symbolic representation of a chemical reaction using chemical formulae of reactants and products.
    *   Example: Mg + O₂ → MgO (This is a skeletal equation)

*   **Skeletal Chemical Equation:** A chemical equation that is not balanced, meaning the number of atoms of each element is not the same on both sides of the arrow.

*   **Balanced Chemical Equation:** A chemical equation where the number of atoms of each element is the same on both the reactant side and the product side. This is done to satisfy the **Law of Conservation of Mass**.

*   **Law of Conservation of Mass:** This law states that mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. Therefore, the total mass of the elements present in the reactants must be equal to the total mass of the elements present in the products.

*   **Steps to Balance a Chemical Equation:**
    1.  **Write the skeletal chemical equation.**
    2.  **Draw boxes around each formula.** (Do not change anything inside the boxes).
    3.  **List the number of atoms of each element** on both sides of the equation.
    4.  **Start balancing with the compound containing the maximum number of atoms** and the element with the maximum number of atoms within that compound.
    5.  **Balance other elements one by one.** Use coefficients (numbers placed before the chemical formulae) to equalize the number of atoms.
    6.  **Check if the equation is balanced** by counting atoms of each element on both sides.
    7.  **Finally, remove the boxes.**

*   **Conditions for Reactions:** Chemical reactions can be influenced by various conditions like temperature, pressure, catalyst, light, etc. (Though not elaborated in detail in this section, it's a relevant concept for advanced understanding).

## 3. Definitions and Terminology

*   **Reactants:** Substances that undergo chemical change in a reaction. (Left-hand side of the arrow).
*   **Products:** New substances formed during a chemical reaction. (Right-hand side of the arrow).
*   **Chemical Formula:** A symbolic representation of the constituent elements and their relative number of atoms in a compound (e.g., H₂O for water).
*   **Coefficient:** A number placed before a chemical formula in a balanced chemical equation to equalize the number of atoms of an element on both sides.
*   **Subscript:** A number written below and to the right of an element symbol in a chemical formula, indicating the number of atoms of that element in a molecule (e.g., '2' in H₂O). Subscripts should **never** be changed when balancing equations.
*   **Catalyst:** A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself undergoing any chemical change.

## 4. Formulas / Rules / Diagrams to Know

*   **Law of Conservation of Mass:** Total mass of reactants = Total mass of products.
*   **Balancing Rule:** Number of atoms of each element on LHS = Number of atoms of that element on RHS.
*   **Common Chemical Formulae to Remember:**
    *   Magnesium oxide: MgO
    *   Magnesium: Mg
    *   Oxygen: O₂
    *   Lead nitrate: Pb(NO₃)₂
    *   Potassium iodide: KI
    *   Zinc: Zn
    *   Sulphuric acid: H₂SO₄
    *   Zinc sulphate: ZnSO₄
    *   Hydrogen: H₂
    *   Iron: Fe

## 5. Common Mistakes Students Make

*   **Confusing Physical and Chemical Changes:** Failing to distinguish between temporary and permanent changes.
*   **Not Balancing Equations:** Submitting skeletal equations instead of balanced ones, leading to loss of marks.
*   **Changing Subscripts:** Incorrectly altering subscripts within a chemical formula to balance the equation (e.g., changing H₂O to H₂O₂). This changes the identity of the substance.
*   **Incorrectly Applying Coefficients:** Misplacing or miscalculating coefficients, leading to unbalanced equations.
*   **Not Checking the Final Balanced Equation:** Forgetting to re-count atoms after balancing to ensure accuracy.
*   **Forgetting the Plus Sign (+) between Reactants/Products:** While writing equations.
*   **Incorrectly Identifying Reactants and Products:** Swapping their positions.

## 6. Exam Tips (CBSE-specific)

*   **Understand the "Why":** Don't just memorize balancing steps. Understand *why* balancing is necessary (Law of Conservation of Mass).
*   **Practice, Practice, Practice:** Balancing chemical equations is a skill that improves with practice. Solve as many problems as possible from the NCERT textbook and other sample papers.
*   **Identify Key Observations:** Be ready to explain the observations made in the activities (e.g., dazzling white flame, white powder, bubble formation, colour change) and relate them to chemical reactions.
*   **Focus on Definitions:** Clearly define terms like reactants, products, chemical equation, balanced equation.
*   **Write Clearly:** Ensure your handwriting is legible, especially when writing chemical formulae and equations. Use proper symbols and arrows.
*   **Show Steps:** When asked to balance an equation, it's good practice to show your steps (listing atoms, applying coefficients) to get partial credit even if the final answer is incorrect.
*   **Know the Formulae:** Memorize common chemical formulae as they are crucial for writing and balancing equations.
*   **Read the Question Carefully:** Pay attention to what is being asked – to write a word equation, a chemical equation, or to balance a given equation.

## 7. Quick Revision Points

*   Chemical changes lead to chemical reactions.
*   Observe changes in state, colour, gas evolution, or temperature to identify reactions.
*   Word equations use words; chemical equations use formulae.
*   Reactants are on the left (→), products on the right.
*   **Law of Conservation of Mass** is the basis for balancing equations.
*   Balanced equations have equal atoms of each element on both sides.
*   **NEVER CHANGE SUBSCRIPTS.** Only use coefficients.
*   Practice balancing from simple to complex equations.
