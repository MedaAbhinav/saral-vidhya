**Host (Riya):** Namaste and welcome back to Science Unpacked, your go-to podcast for making NCERT science concepts crystal clear! I'm your host, Riya. Today, we're diving into Chapter 3 of your Class 10 Science textbook: "Metals and Non-metals." Get ready to explore the fascinating world of elements and their unique properties!

**(Short, engaging sound effect - like a gentle 'ping' or a subtle metallic chime)**

**Riya:** And to guide us through this exciting chapter, we have our resident science expert, Ms. Sharma. Welcome to the show, Ms. Sharma!

**Expert Teacher (Ms. Sharma):** Thank you, Riya. It's a pleasure to be here and help our students understand the difference between these fundamental building blocks of matter.

**Riya:** Ms. Sharma, the chapter starts by mentioning that we've already learned about elements in Class 9. Can you briefly remind us what elements are and how metals and non-metals fit into that picture?

**Ms. Sharma:** Absolutely, Riya. Elements are pure substances that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. Think of them as the fundamental ingredients in the universe. We classify these elements into two main categories based on their properties: metals and non-metals. And these properties dictate how they are used in our daily lives.

**Riya:** That makes sense! So, the chapter focuses on these properties. Let's start with the physical properties. Ms. Sharma, what's the first striking physical characteristic of most metals?

**Ms. Sharma:** The very first thing you'll notice when you look at a pure metal, like iron, copper, or gold, is its shine. This property is called **metallic lustre**. If you clean their surface, that shiny appearance comes back.

**Riya:** Metallic lustre! So, that's why jewelry and many kitchen utensils are so shiny. What about their hardness? Are all metals equally hard?

**Ms. Sharma:** Not at all. Metals are *generally* hard, but the degree of hardness varies. For instance, sodium is a metal that's surprisingly soft and can be cut with a knife, which is quite different from iron. This is one of the first points of distinction we explore through activities like Activity 3.2 in the textbook.

**Riya:** That's a great example to highlight the variation. Now, I've often seen metal sheets used for roofing or in car bodies. This brings me to the property of **malleability**. Can you explain that?

**Ms. Sharma:** Excellent observation, Riya! Malleability is the ability of a metal to be beaten into thin sheets without breaking. This is why we can use metals like aluminum to make foil for packaging, or iron to create car bodies. The chapter mentions that gold and silver are the most malleable metals, which is why they are used for intricate designs in jewelry.

**Riya:** And it’s not just sheets, right? We also see metal wires everywhere – in electrical appliances and even in overhead power lines. What's that property called?

**Ms. Sharma:** That property is called **ductility**. It's the ability of a metal to be drawn into thin wires. Imagine this: a single gram of gold can be stretched into a wire about 2 kilometers long! That's the power of ductility. It's because of malleability and ductility that metals can be shaped into countless useful forms.

**Riya:** Wow, 2 kilometers! That's incredible. Now, moving on to heat and electricity. The chapter talks about metals being good conductors. Can you elaborate on Activity 3.5 and 3.6?

**Ms. Sharma:** Certainly. Activity 3.5 demonstrates that metals are good conductors of heat. When you heat one end of a metal wire, the heat travels to the other end, causing a pin attached with wax to fall off. This is why metals are used for making cooking vessels – they efficiently transfer heat to our food. Silver and copper are the best conductors, while metals like lead and mercury are relatively poor conductors.

**Riya:** And electricity?

**Ms. Sharma:** Activity 3.6 shows that metals are also good conductors of electricity. When you place a metal in an electric circuit, the bulb glows, indicating the flow of electric current. This is why electrical wires are made of metals, but they have an outer coating of non-conductive materials like PVC or rubber to prevent electric shocks.

**Riya:** That practical application is so important for our safety! Lastly, there's a property that explains why school bells are made of metal. What is that?

**Ms. Sharma:** That's **sonorous**. Metals are sonorous, meaning they produce a ringing sound when struck. This property is utilized in making bells and musical instruments.

**(Short sound effect - a cheerful 'ding!')**

**Riya:** And finally, Quiz Question 3: Which property allows metals to be drawn into thin wires?

**Riya:** Correct! It's **ductility**!

**Riya:** Excellent job, everyone! Ms. Sharma, you've made these concepts so accessible. Before we wrap up, could you briefly mention how non-metals generally differ from metals in these physical properties?

**Ms. Sharma:** Of course, Riya. Non-metals, like carbon, sulphur, and oxygen, generally do not exhibit these metallic properties. They are typically dull in appearance, not malleable or ductile, poor conductors of heat and electricity, and not sonorous. However, there are exceptions, like graphite, which is a non-metal but a good conductor of electricity. We'll explore these nuances further in the chapter.
