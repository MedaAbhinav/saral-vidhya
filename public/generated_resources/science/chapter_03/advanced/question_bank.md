### 2. Case Study / Passage-Based Questions

**Instructions:** Read the passage and answer the questions that follow. Each question is worth 3-5 marks.

**Passage 1: The Protective Shield**

In many modern electronic devices, wires carrying electrical current are insulated. This insulation is crucial for safety and efficient functioning. The materials used for insulation are typically non-metals. For instance, PVC (polyvinyl chloride) and rubber are commonly used. These materials are chosen because they possess specific properties. Metals, on the other hand, are excellent conductors of electricity. However, some metals, like copper and aluminum, are used as the conductors themselves due to their high conductivity. When metals are used in their pure form or as alloys, their reactivity with the environment becomes a significant consideration. For example, iron rusts in the presence of moisture and oxygen, losing its structural integrity. This is a chemical change where iron reacts to form iron oxides. To prevent such reactions and extend the lifespan of metal objects, various methods are employed, such as galvanization (coating with zinc) or painting.

1.  (a) Explain why non-metals like PVC and rubber are used as insulators for electrical wires, referencing their general physical properties. (2 marks)
    (b) Given that copper is an excellent conductor, why is it still coated with plastic? (1 mark)
    (c) What chemical property of iron makes it susceptible to rusting, and what is the general nature of the product formed? (2 marks)

**Passage 2: The Reactive Trio**

Metals belonging to Group 1 of the periodic table, known as alkali metals, exhibit striking chemical properties. Elements like Lithium (Li), Sodium (Na), and Potassium (K) are characterized by their extreme reactivity. They readily lose their single valence electron to form positive ions. Their reaction with water is a classic demonstration of their reactivity; they react to produce hydrogen gas and the corresponding metal hydroxide. The vigour of this reaction increases as we move down the group. For example, sodium metal floats on water and reacts to produce hydrogen gas, which catches fire due to the heat generated. Potassium reacts even more violently, and the hydrogen gas produced ignites instantaneously. These metals are so soft that they can be cut with a knife and have relatively low melting points, distinguishing them from many other metals.

2.  (a) Describe the general trend in reactivity with water among Lithium, Sodium, and Potassium, and explain the reason for this trend in terms of electron loss. (2 marks)
    (b) What are the products formed when sodium reacts with water? Write a balanced chemical equation for the reaction. (2 marks)
    (c) Mention two physical properties of alkali metals that make them different from commonly observed metals like iron or copper. (1 mark)

**Passage 3: The Amphoteric Nature**

Metal oxides are generally basic in nature. For example, magnesium oxide reacts with water to form magnesium hydroxide, a base, and it neutralizes acids. However, some metal oxides exhibit a dual nature; they can react with both acids and bases. These are known as amphoteric oxides. Aluminum oxide ($Al_2O_3$) and Zinc oxide (ZnO) are prime examples. For instance, aluminum oxide reacts with hydrochloric acid (an acid) to form aluminum chloride and water. It also reacts with sodium hydroxide (a base) to form sodium aluminate and water. This dual reactivity arises from the ability of these metal oxides to act as either acids or bases depending on the reactant. Non-metal oxides, on the other hand, are typically acidic.

3.  (a) State the general nature of metal oxides and provide an example. (1 mark)
    (b) Define amphoteric oxides and give two examples. (2 marks)
    (c) Write balanced chemical equations for the reactions of aluminum oxide ($Al_2O_3$) with (i) hydrochloric acid and (ii) sodium hydroxide. (2 marks)

**Passage 4: From Ore to Pure Metal**

The extraction of metals from their ores is a complex process that heavily relies on the chemical properties of the metals. Highly reactive metals like potassium, sodium, and calcium are found in nature as their compounds and cannot be obtained by simple reduction with carbon because they are more reactive than carbon. They are usually extracted by electrolysis of their molten salts. Moderately reactive metals, such as iron, zinc, and lead, can be extracted by reduction of their oxides with carbon. Less reactive metals, such as mercury and silver, are often found in their native form or as sulfides and can be easily extracted. For example, mercury can be obtained by heating its sulfide ore in the presence of air, causing it to decompose into mercury and sulfur dioxide.

4.  (a) Why are highly reactive metals like potassium extracted by electrolysis of their molten salts, rather than by reduction with carbon? (2 marks)
    (b) How would you expect to extract a metal that is less reactive than hydrogen, like gold, from its ore? (1 mark)
    (c) Consider the extraction of copper from its sulfide ore. Would it likely involve electrolysis of molten copper sulfide or heating its sulfide in air? Justify your answer based on copper's reactivity. (2 marks)

**Passage 5: The Allotropic Puzzle**

Carbon, a non-metal, showcases remarkable allotropy, meaning it exists in different physical forms while maintaining the same chemical composition. Diamond and graphite are two well-known allotropes of carbon. Diamond is renowned for its extreme hardness, high refractive index, and status as an electrical insulator. Its crystalline structure, where each carbon atom is bonded to four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement, accounts for these properties. Graphite, conversely, is soft, flaky, and an excellent conductor of electricity. In graphite, carbon atoms are arranged in hexagonal layers, with weak forces between these layers allowing them to slide past each other, and free electrons contributing to its electrical conductivity. Other allotropes like fullerenes also exist.

5.  (a) Explain why diamond is an electrical insulator while graphite is a good conductor of electricity, relating their structures to their electrical properties. (3 marks)
    (b) How do the physical properties of diamond (hardness, brittleness) differ from those of graphite (softness, flakiness), and how do these differences arise from their structural arrangements? (2 marks)

**Passage 5: The Allotropic Puzzle**

5.  (a) **Explanation:** In diamond, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms in a rigid, three-dimensional tetrahedral structure. Within each layer, carbon atoms are bonded to three other carbon atoms, forming delocalized pi electrons above and below the plane of the ring. These delocalized electrons are free to move along the layers, making graphite an excellent conductor of electricity. (3 marks)

    *   **Structure:** Diamond's tetrahedral arrangement of carbon atoms forms a rigid lattice. Graphite's hexagonal arrangement forms planar layers, but the weak forces between these layers dictate its mechanical properties. (2 marks)

    **Experimental Determination using Displacement Reactions:**
    We can determine the relative reactivity of metals by observing displacement reactions in solutions. If a more reactive metal is placed in the salt solution of a less reactive metal, the more reactive metal will displace the less reactive metal from its compound. This is because the more reactive metal has a greater tendency to lose electrons and form ions, while the less reactive metal ions have a lesser tendency to gain electrons (or the metal itself has less tendency to be formed from ions).

    **Principle:**
    Metal A + Salt solution of Metal B $\rightarrow$ Salt solution of Metal A + Metal B
    This reaction occurs if Metal A is MORE reactive than Metal B.

    **Examples:**
    1.  **Zinc displacing Copper:** If a zinc strip is placed in copper sulfate solution:
        $Zn(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow ZnSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)$
        Here, zinc is more reactive than copper. Zinc displaces copper from copper sulfate solution. The blue color of copper sulfate fades, and reddish-brown copper metal deposits on the zinc strip.

    2.  **Iron displacing Copper:** If an iron nail is placed in copper sulfate solution:
        $Fe(s) + CuSO_4(aq) \rightarrow FeSO_4(aq) + Cu(s)$
        Iron is more reactive than copper, so it displaces copper. The solution turns pale green (due to $Fe^{2+}$ ions) and reddish-brown copper deposits on the iron nail.

    3.  **Copper NOT displacing Iron:** If a copper strip is placed in iron sulfate solution:
        $Cu(s) + FeSO_4(aq) \rightarrow No\ Reaction$
        Copper is less reactive than iron, so it cannot displace iron from iron sulfate solution.

This is because they have a very strong tendency to lose electrons, meaning their oxides are very stable and cannot be reduced by common reducing agents like carbon. The powerful electric current in electrolysis forces the reduction of their metal ions.
    *   **Simple Chemical Reduction:** Moderately reactive metals like Iron (Fe), Zinc (Zn), Lead (Pb), and Tin (Sn) can be extracted by reducing their oxides using reducing agents like carbon (heating with carbon) or carbon monoxide. These metals are less reactive than the alkali and alkaline earth metals, so their oxides are less stable and can be reduced by carbon.

        *   **Metals:** Potassium (K), Sodium (Na), Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Aluminum (Al).

In electrolysis, a strong electric current is passed through the molten substance. At the cathode, the metal ions ($M^{n+}$) gain electrons and are reduced to the molten metal ($M$). For example, for sodium:
            $NaCl(l) \xrightarrow{Electrolysis} Na(l) + \frac{1}{2}Cl_2(g)$
            At cathode: $Na^+ + e^- \rightarrow Na$
            At anode: $Cl^- \rightarrow \frac{1}{2}Cl_2 + e^-$ (5 marks)

        *   **Metals:** Iron (Fe), Zinc (Zn), Lead (Pb), Copper (Cu - though often considered less reactive).
        *   **Principle:** These metals are less reactive than the highly reactive metals. Their oxides are relatively less stable and can be reduced by chemical reducing agents.
        *   **Method:**
            *   **Reduction of Oxides:** The metal oxide ore is heated strongly in the presence of a reducing agent, usually carbon (coke). For example, zinc oxide is heated with carbon:
                $ZnO(s) + C(s) \xrightarrow{Heat} Zn(l) + CO(g)$
            *   **Reduction by Carbon Monoxide:** Sometimes, carbon monoxide is used as the reducing agent. For example, in the extraction of iron in a blast furnace.
                $Fe_2O_3(s) + 3CO(g) \xrightarrow{Heat} 2Fe(l) + 3CO_2(g)$
            *   **Roasting and Reduction:** If the ore is a sulfide (e.g., $ZnS$), it is first roasted (heated in the presence of air) to convert it to the oxide, which is then reduced.
                $2ZnS(s) + 3O_2(g) \xrightarrow{Roasting} 2ZnO(s) + 2SO_2(g)$

        *   **Metals:** Mercury (Hg), Silver (Ag), Gold (Au), Platinum (Pt).

        *   **Method:**
            *   **Heating of Sulfides:** For mercury and silver, sulfide ores are often heated in the presence of air (roasting). The oxide formed decomposes on further heating to give the metal.

                $2HgO(s) \xrightarrow{Heat} 2Hg(l) + O_2(g)$

    **Materials:**

    *   One small piece of aluminum (e.g., a foil ball or a small ingot).

    **Procedure:**
    1.  **Preparation:** Fill both identical containers with the same amount of water up to a clearly marked level (e.g., using a marker). Ensure the water level is the same in both containers. Record this initial water level or volume if using measuring cylinders.
    2.  **Adding Aluminum:** Carefully place the piece of aluminum into one container of water. Observe if it floats or sinks. If it sinks, note how much the water level rises. If it floats, this comparison becomes qualitative rather than quantitative for simple demonstration. For metals typically used in such demos, they will sink.
    3.  **Adding Lead:** Carefully place the piece of lead into the second container of water (which has the same initial water level as the first). Observe if it floats or sinks.

        *   Observe the behavior: Do both sink? Does one sink lower than the other?

            *   Density is mass per unit volume ($ \rho = m/V $). If we assume the pieces of aluminum and lead are of *approximately* similar *volumes* (e.g., we cut them to be similar-sized cubes or spheres), then the one that causes a greater rise in water level is displacing more water, indicating it has a larger mass for that volume. Therefore, it has a higher density.
            *   Alternatively, if we are comparing pieces of *similar mass*, the denser metal will occupy a smaller volume and thus cause a smaller rise in water level.

When submerged, they both displace an equal volume of water (equal to their own volume). The heavier object (denser one) will tend to sink more forcefully, or if the container is full, cause a greater overflow. The key is comparing the rise.

The difference in *weight* of the displaced water will be proportional to the density.

The one with more mass (lead) will cause a greater total weight on the base of the container, but the water level rise will be the same IF the volumes are identical.

                1.  Take two identical containers, fill them with the same volume of water to a marked level.

3.  **Answer:**
    The periodic table organizes elements based on their electronic configuration, specifically the number of valence electrons and the principal energy levels they occupy. This configuration directly dictates an element's chemical behavior, including its metallic or non-metallic character and its tendency to lose or gain electrons.

    **Metallic Character Trend:**
    *   **Across a Period (Left to Right):** As we move from left to right across a period, the number of valence electrons increases, and these electrons are added to the same outermost shell. The nuclear charge also increases, pulling these valence electrons more strongly towards the nucleus. This increased effective nuclear charge makes it harder for the atom to lose electrons. Consequently, the metallic character decreases, and non-metallic character increases across a period. Elements on the left (like alkali metals) readily lose electrons (are metals), while elements on the right (like halogens) tend to gain electrons (are non-metals).
    *   **Down a Group (Top to Bottom):** As we move down a group, new electron shells are added. The valence electrons are further from the nucleus and are shielded by more inner electron shells. Although the nuclear charge increases, the shielding effect significantly reduces the attraction between the nucleus and the valence electrons. This makes it easier for the atom to lose its valence electrons. Therefore, metallic character increases down a group. Elements at the top of a group may be non-metals or less metallic, while those further down are more metallic.

    **Connection to Chemical Properties:**
    *   **Metals:** Due to their tendency to lose electrons (low ionization enthalpy, increased metallic character down a group), metals readily form positive ions (cations). This explains why metals typically form basic oxides (e.g., $Na_2O$, $MgO$) by reacting with oxygen, as the metal oxide can accept protons or react with acids. They are also good conductors of heat and electricity due to the presence of mobile electrons.
    *   **Non-metals:** Conversely, non-metals tend to gain or share electrons (high ionization enthalpy, high electron affinity, increased non-metallic character across a period). This leads them to form negative ions (anions) or covalent bonds. Their oxides are generally acidic (e.g., $CO_2$, $SO_2$), as they can donate protons or react with bases. They are typically poor conductors of electricity and heat due to the absence of free electrons.

    In essence, the electronic configuration determines the ease with which electrons are lost or gained, which in turn governs whether an element behaves as a metal or a non-metal, dictating its characteristic chemical and physical properties.
