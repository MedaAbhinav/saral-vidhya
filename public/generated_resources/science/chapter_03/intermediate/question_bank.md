### 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

1.  **What is metallic lustre? Give an example of a metal and a non-metal that exhibit this property differently.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Metallic lustre is the shining appearance of a metal in its pure state. Metals like iron, copper, and silver have a prominent metallic lustre. Non-metals like sulphur or phosphorus do not typically exhibit this shining property, though iodine, a non-metal, is an exception and is lustrous.

2.  **Define malleability and ductility. Provide one example of each use in daily life.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   **Malleability:** The property of metals to be beaten into thin sheets without breaking is called malleability. Example: Aluminium is used to make thin aluminium foil for wrapping food.
        *   **Ductility:** The property of metals to be drawn into thin wires is called ductility. Example: Copper is used to make electrical wires.

3.  **Explain why metals are generally good conductors of heat and electricity.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Metals are good conductors of heat and electricity because they have free electrons in their structure. These free electrons are mobile and can move throughout the metal. When heat is applied or an electric potential is applied, these free electrons can easily transfer energy or charge, thus conducting heat and electricity efficiently.

4.  **What are alloys? Why are they prepared? Give an example of an alloy and its uses.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Alloys are homogeneous mixtures of two or more metals, or a metal with one or more non-metals. Alloys are prepared to improve the properties of the constituent metals, such as increasing hardness, preventing corrosion, or changing the melting point. Example: Brass (an alloy of copper and zinc) is used to make musical instruments and utensils.

5.  **Explain the difference in the nature of oxides formed by metals and non-metals.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Metals generally form basic oxides. When these oxides dissolve in water, they form alkaline solutions. For example, sodium oxide reacts with water to form sodium hydroxide, which is basic. Non-metals generally form acidic oxides. When these oxides dissolve in water, they form acidic solutions. For example, sulphur dioxide reacts with water to form sulphurous acid, which is acidic.

6.  **Mention two common physical properties that distinguish metals from non-metals.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   **Conductivity:** Metals are generally good conductors of heat and electricity, while non-metals are poor conductors (insulators).
        *   **Sonority:** Metals are sonorous, meaning they produce a ringing sound when struck, whereas non-metals are usually not sonorous.

7.  **What is the difference between diamond and graphite in terms of their properties?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Diamond is an allotrope of carbon that is extremely hard, has a high melting point, and is an electrical insulator. Graphite, another allotrope of carbon, is soft, has a high melting point, and is a good conductor of electricity.

8.  **Explain why sodium and potassium metals are stored under kerosene.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Sodium and potassium are highly reactive alkali metals. They react vigorously with oxygen and moisture present in the air, leading to rapid corrosion and even ignition. Storing them under kerosene prevents their contact with air and moisture, thus preventing such reactions and keeping them safe.

9.  **Name a metal that is a liquid at room temperature and a non-metal that is a solid at room temperature.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   Metal that is a liquid at room temperature: Mercury.
        *   Non-metal that is a solid at room temperature: Sulphur or Carbon.

10. **Why are electric wires usually coated with an insulating material like PVC?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Electric wires carry electric current. The core of the wire is made of a metal (good conductor). The coating of PVC (a non-metal) is an electrical insulator. This coating prevents electric shocks to people and animals and also prevents short circuits, ensuring safety.

3.  **Describe the chemical properties of metals with respect to their reactions with air and water. Provide balanced chemical equations where possible.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Introduction to chemical properties.
        *   **Reaction with Air (Oxygen):**
            *   General: Metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides.
            *   Examples:
                *   Magnesium burning in air (Mg + O₂ → MgO).
                *   Copper reacting with oxygen (Cu + O₂ → CuO).
            *   Mention that reactivity varies.
        *   **Reaction with Water:**
            *   General: Some metals react with water to form metal hydroxides or oxides and hydrogen gas.
            *   Examples:
                *   Sodium reacting with water (Na + H₂O → NaOH + H₂).
                *   Calcium reacting with water (Ca + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂).
            *   Mention that less reactive metals do not react with cold water but may react with steam (e.g., Aluminium, Iron).
        *   Concluding statement about reactivity series.

4.  **Explain the formation of ionic compounds. How are ionic compounds formed from metals and non-metals? Illustrate with an example.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Define ionic compounds (formed by transfer of electrons).
        *   Explain the formation of ions: metals form positive ions (cations) by losing electrons, non-metals form negative ions (anions) by gaining electrons.
        *   Explain electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions forming the ionic bond.
        *   **Illustration:** Formation of Sodium Chloride (NaCl).
            *   Sodium atom (Na) loses 1 electron to become Na⁺.
            *   Chlorine atom (Cl) gains 1 electron to become Cl⁻.
            *   Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions attract electrostatically to form NaCl.
            *   Show electronic configuration change for Na and Cl.
        *   Mention common properties of ionic compounds (high melting/boiling points, solubility in water, conduction of electricity in molten/aqueous state).

5.  **What happens when a metal reacts with a dilute acid? Explain with examples and mention the exceptions.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   General Reaction: Metal + Dilute Acid → Salt + Hydrogen Gas.
        *   Explain why hydrogen gas is evolved (metal is more reactive than hydrogen).
        *   **Examples:**
            *   Magnesium reacting with dilute HCl (Mg + HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂).
            *   Zinc reacting with dilute H₂SO₄ (Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂).
        *   **Exceptions:**
            *   Metals less reactive than hydrogen (e.g., Copper, Silver, Gold) do not produce hydrogen gas when reacted with dilute acids.
            *   Metals like Aluminium and Zinc form a protective oxide layer that prevents further reaction with dilute acids unless the acid is hot and concentrated.
            *   Nitric acid: Dilute nitric acid oxidizes the hydrogen produced to water and is itself reduced to various nitrogen oxides, so hydrogen gas is not always liberated.
