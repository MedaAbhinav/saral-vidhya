# CHAPTER 3: Metals and Non-metals

## Introduction
*   Elements are broadly classified into metals and non-metals based on their properties.
*   **Key Questions to Consider**:
    *   Uses of metals and non-metals in daily life.
    *   Properties used for categorising elements as metals or non-metals.
    *   How these properties relate to their uses.

Let's explore these properties in detail.

## 3.1 Physical Properties

### 3.1.1 Metals

To study physical properties, samples of metals like iron, copper, aluminium, magnesium, sodium, lead, and zinc are commonly used.

#### Activity 3.1: Metallic Lustre
*   **Procedure**: Observe the appearance of iron, copper, aluminium, and magnesium samples. Clean their surfaces by rubbing with sandpaper and observe again.
*   **Observation**: Metals in their pure state have a shining surface after cleaning.
*   **Property**: This shining property is called **metallic lustre**.

#### Activity 3.2: Hardness
*   **Procedure**:
    *   Try to cut small pieces of iron, copper, aluminium, and magnesium with a sharp knife.
    *   Carefully handle sodium metal (dry it with filter paper) and try to cut it with a knife on a watch-glass.
*   **Observation**:
    *   Most metals (iron, copper, aluminium, magnesium) are generally hard and cannot be cut easily with a knife.
    *   Sodium metal is very soft and can be easily cut with a knife.
*   **Property**: Metals are generally **hard**. The hardness varies from metal to metal.
*   **Exception**: Alkali metals (lithium, sodium, potassium) are soft enough to be cut with a knife.

#### Activity 3.3: Malleability
*   **Procedure**: Place pieces of iron, zinc, lead, and copper on an iron block and strike them repeatedly (four or five times) with a hammer.
*   **Observation**: Metals change shape; they can be beaten into thin sheets without breaking.
*   **Property**: The ability of metals to be beaten into thin sheets is called **malleability**.
*   **Examples**: Gold and silver are the most malleable metals.

#### Activity 3.4: Ductility
*   **Procedure**: List metals whose wires are commonly seen in daily life (e.g., copper, aluminium).
*   **Observation**: Many metals can be drawn into thin wires.
*   **Property**: The ability of metals to be drawn into thin wires is called **ductility**.
*   **Example**: Gold is the most ductile metal. A wire of approximately 2 km length can be drawn from one gram of gold.
*   **Application**: Malleability and ductility allow metals to be given different shapes according to our needs (e.g., cooking vessels).

#### Activity 3.5: Heat Conduction and Melting Point
*   **Procedure**:
    *   Clamp an aluminium or copper wire on a stand.
    *   Fix a pin to the free end of the wire using wax.
    *   Heat the wire with a spirit lamp, candle, or burner near the clamped end.
*   **Observation**: After some time, the wax melts, and the pin falls off, indicating heat transfer along the wire. The metal wire itself does not melt.
*   **Conclusion**: Metals are **good conductors of heat** and have **high melting points**.
*   **Best Conductors**: Silver and copper.
*   **Poor Conductors**: Lead and mercury are comparatively poor conductors of heat.

#### Activity 3.6: Electrical Conduction
*   **Procedure**:
    *   Set up an electric circuit with a battery, bulb, and switch.
    *   Place the metal to be tested (e.g., iron, copper, aluminium) between terminals A and B in the circuit.
*   **Observation**: The bulb glows when metals are placed in the circuit.
*   **Conclusion**: Metals are **good conductors of electricity**.
*   **Application**: Electric wires are coated with polyvinylchloride (PVC) or rubber-like materials because these are insulators, preventing electric shock and short circuits.

#### Sonority
*   **Property**: Metals that produce a sound on striking a hard surface are said to be **sonorous**.
*   **Application**: School bells are made of metals because of their sonorous property.

### 3.1.2 Non-metals

*   **Introduction**: Non-metals are fewer in number compared to metals.
*   **Examples**: Carbon (coal, graphite), sulphur, iodine, oxygen, hydrogen.
*   **Physical State**: Non-metals are either **solids** (e.g., carbon, sulphur, iodine) or **gases** (e.g., oxygen, hydrogen) at room temperature, except for **bromine**, which is a liquid.

#### Activity 3.7: Physical Properties of Non-metals
*   **Procedure**: Collect samples of carbon (coal or graphite), sulphur, and iodine. Carry out Activities 3.1 to 3.4 and 3.6 (testing for lustre, hardness, malleability, ductility, and electrical conductivity) with these non-metals.
*   **Observation (General)**: Non-metals generally lack metallic lustre, are not hard (often brittle), are non-malleable, non-ductile, and poor conductors of electricity (insulators).

#### Exceptions to General Physical Properties
It is important to note that elements cannot be grouped based on physical properties alone, as there are many exceptions:

1.  **State at Room Temperature**: All metals exist as solids at room temperature, **except mercury**, which is a liquid.
2.  **Melting Points**: Most metals have high melting points. However, **gallium** and **caesium** have very low melting points; they will melt if kept on your palm.
3.  **Lustre**: **Iodine** is a non-metal but is lustrous (has a shining appearance).
4.  **Carbon Allotropes**: Carbon is a non-metal that exists in different forms called **allotropes**.
    *   **Diamond**: An allotrope of carbon, is the hardest natural substance known and has a very high melting and boiling point.
    *   **Graphite**: Another allotrope of carbon, is a conductor of electricity (unlike most non-metals).
5.  **Softness and Melting Points of Metals**: **Alkali metals** (lithium, sodium, potassium) are so soft that they can be cut with a knife. They also have low densities and low melting points.

Elements can be more clearly classified as metals and non-metals based on their **chemical properties**.

#### Activity 3.8: Nature of Oxides of Metals and Non-metals
*   **Procedure**:
    *   Burn a magnesium ribbon. Collect the ashes (magnesium oxide) and dissolve them in water. Test the solution with red and blue litmus paper.
    *   Burn sulphur powder. Place a test tube over the burning sulphur to collect the fumes (sulphur dioxide). Add some water to the test tube and shake. Test this solution (sulphurous acid) with blue and red litmus paper.
*   **Observation**:
    *   **Magnesium (Metal)**: Magnesium oxide dissolves in water to form magnesium hydroxide, which turns red litmus blue.
    *   **Sulphur (Non-metal)**: Sulphur dioxide dissolves in water to form sulphurous acid, which turns blue litmus red.
*   **Conclusion**:
    *   Products formed on burning **magnesium (metal)** are **basic** in nature.
    *   Products formed on burning **sulphur (non-metal)** are **acidic** in nature.
*   **Equations**:
    *   Burning magnesium:
        $2Mg(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2MgO(s)$
    *   Dissolving magnesium oxide in water:
        $MgO(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow Mg(OH)_2(aq)$ (Magnesium hydroxide is a base)
    *   Burning sulphur:
        $S(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow SO_2(g)$
    *   Dissolving sulphur dioxide in water:
        $SO_2(g) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow H_2SO_3(aq)$ (Sulphurous acid is an acid)
*   **General Rule**: Most non-metals produce **acidic oxides** when dissolved in water. Most metals, on the other hand, give rise to **basic oxides**.

## 3.2 Chemical Properties of Metals

This section explores how metals react with various substances. Samples of aluminium, copper, iron, lead, magnesium, zinc, and sodium will be considered.

### 3.2.1 What happens when Metals are burnt in Air?

**Recall**: From Activity 3.8, magnesium burns in air with a dazzling white flame. Not all metals react in the same vigorous manner.

#### Activity 3.9: Reaction of Metals with Oxygen
*   **CAUTION**: This activity requires teacher's assistance and eye protection.
*   **Procedure**:
    *   Hold various metal samples (e.g., Al, Cu, Fe, Pb, Mg, Zn, Na) with tongs and try burning them over a flame.
    *   Collect any product formed.
    *   Let products and metal surfaces cool down.
*   **Observations and Analysis**:
    *   **Which metals burn easily?**: Some metals, like magnesium, burn vigorously. Others, like iron, may glow or burn slowly.
    *   **Flame colour when metal burnt**: Magnesium burns with a dazzling white flame. Other metals might show different flame colours or just glow.
    *   **Metal surface appearance after burning**: A layer of oxide forms on the surface (e.g., copper forms black CuO).
    *   **Reactivity towards oxygen**: Metals show different reactivities. Some react very vigorously, others less so, and some hardly react at all under normal heating conditions.
    *   **Solubility of products in water**: Some metal oxides (like sodium oxide, potassium oxide) are soluble in water, forming alkalis. Most metal oxides are insoluble.

#### General Reaction with Oxygen
Almost all metals combine with oxygen to form **metal oxides**.
$\text{Metal} + \text{Oxygen} \rightarrow \text{Metal oxide}$

*   **Examples**:
    *   When copper is heated in air, it combines with oxygen to form **copper(II) oxide**, which is black.
        $2Cu(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2CuO(s)$
        (Copper) (Copper(II) oxide)
    *   Similarly, aluminium forms **aluminium oxide**.
        $4Al(s) + 3O_2(g) \rightarrow 2Al_2O_3(s)$
        (Aluminium) (Aluminium oxide)

#### Nature of Metal Oxides
*   From Chapter 2, we know that metal oxides are generally **basic in nature**. For example, copper oxide reacts with hydrochloric acid:
    $CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) \rightarrow CuCl_2(aq) + H_2O(l)$

*   **Amphoteric Oxides**: Some metal oxides, such as **aluminium oxide ($Al_2O_3$)** and **zinc oxide ($ZnO$)**, show both acidic as well as basic behaviour.
    *   **Definition**: Such metal oxides which react with both acids as well as bases to produce salts and water are known as **amphoteric oxides**.
    *   **Reactions of Aluminium Oxide**:
        *   With acid (acting as a base):
            $Al_2O_3(s) + 6HCl(aq) \rightarrow 2AlCl_3(aq) + 3H_2O(l)$
        *   With base (acting as an acid):
            $Al_2O_3(s) + 2NaOH(aq) \rightarrow 2NaAlO_2(aq) + H_2O(l)$
            (Sodium aluminate)

#### Solubility of Metal Oxides in Water
*   Most metal oxides are **insoluble in water**.
*   However, some of these dissolve in water to form **alkalis** (soluble bases).
    *   **Sodium oxide ($Na_2O$)** and **potassium oxide ($K_2O$)** dissolve in water to produce alkalis:
        $Na_2O(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2NaOH(aq)$
        $K_2O(s) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2KOH(aq)$

#### Reactivity of Metals towards Oxygen
*   As observed in Activity 3.9, all metals do not react with oxygen at the same rate. Different metals show different reactivities towards oxygen.
*   **Highly Reactive Metals**: Metals such as **potassium (K)** and **sodium (Na)** react so vigorously with oxygen that they catch fire if kept in the open air.
    *   **Protective Measure**: To protect them and to prevent accidental fires, they are stored immersed in **kerosene oil**.
