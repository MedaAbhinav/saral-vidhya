# Chapter 6: Control and Coordination

## 1. Introduction: The Need for Control and Coordination

*   **Observation:** Living organisms exhibit movement. Some movements are linked to growth (e.g., seedling emerging from soil), while others are not (e.g., animals running, plants growing towards sunlight).
*   **Association of Movement with Life:** Visible movements are often perceived as a sign of life.
*   **Movement as a Response:** Organisms move in response to changes in their environment.
    *   **Purpose:** To utilize environmental changes to their advantage (e.g., plants growing towards light, animals seeking pleasure/fun).
    *   **Protection:** Responding to dangerous stimuli (e.g., moving away from a hot object).
*   **Controlled Movement:** These movements are not random but carefully controlled.
*   **Recognition and Response:** Organisms need to:
    1.  Recognize specific environmental events (stimuli).
    2.  Produce an appropriate movement (response) in reaction.
*   **Control and Coordination Systems:** Multicellular organisms use specialized tissues to achieve this control and coordination.

## 2. Control and Coordination in Animals: The Nervous System

### 2.1. Nervous Tissue and Neurons

*   **Components:** In animals, control and coordination are primarily managed by **nervous tissue** and **muscular tissue**.
*   **Detection of Stimuli:** Specialized nerve cells called **neurons** have specialized tips that detect information from the environment.
*   **Receptors:** These specialized tips are called **receptors**.
    *   **Types of Receptors and Their Functions:**
        *   **Gustatory receptors:** Detect taste (located on the tongue).
        *   **Olfactory receptors:** Detect smell (located in the nose).
        *   Other receptors are located in sense organs like the inner ear (hearing/balance) and eyes (sight).
*   **Generation of Electrical Impulses:**
    1.  When a receptor detects a stimulus, a chemical reaction occurs.
    2.  This reaction generates an **electrical impulse**.
*   **Conduction of Electrical Impulses:**
    1.  The electrical impulse travels from the **dendritic tip** (where information is acquired) to the **cell body**.
    2.  From the cell body, it travels along the **axon** (the long part of the neuron) to its end.
*   **Transmission of Signals to Other Cells:**
    1.  At the end of the axon, the electrical impulse triggers the release of **chemical signals**.
    2.  These chemicals cross a small gap called a **synapse**.
    3.  The chemicals initiate a similar electrical impulse in the dendrite of the **next neuron** or another target cell.
*   **Synapse:** The junction between two neurons (or a neuron and another cell) where information is transmitted chemically.
*   **Neuromuscular Junction:** A specific type of synapse where a neuron communicates with a muscle cell.
*   **Structure of a Neuron:**
    *   **Dendrites:** Branch-like extensions that receive signals and carry them towards the cell body. (Point (i) where information is acquired).
    *   **Cell Body (Soma):** Contains the nucleus and integrates signals.
    *   **Axon:** A long projection that carries electrical impulses away from the cell body. (Point (ii) through which information travels).
    *   **Axon Terminal:** The end of the axon where chemical signals are released to transmit information to the next cell. (Point (iii) where the impulse is converted to a chemical signal).
*   **Nervous Tissue:** Composed of an organized network of neurons specialized for conducting information via electrical impulses throughout the body.

### 2.2. Reflex Actions

*   **Definition:** A sudden, involuntary, and rapid response to a stimulus, often without conscious thought or control.
    *   *Examples:* Pulling hand away from a hot object, jumping out of the way of a vehicle, mouth watering when hungry.
*   **Necessity of Reflex Actions:**
    *   **Speed:** Conscious thinking (involving the brain) takes time. For dangerous situations (like touching a hot object), a quick response is crucial to prevent injury.
    *   **Efficiency:** Reflex actions bypass the conscious thinking part of the brain for immediate action.
*   **Reflex Arc:** The pathway followed by a nerve impulse during a reflex action.
    1.  **Receptor:** Detects the stimulus (e.g., heat receptors in the skin).
    2.  **Sensory Neuron (Afferent Neuron):** Carries the impulse from the receptor to the spinal cord.
    3.  **Relay Neuron (Interneuron):** Located in the spinal cord, it connects the sensory neuron to the motor neuron.
    4.  **Motor Neuron (Efferent Neuron):** Carries the impulse from the spinal cord to the effector.
    5.  **Effector:** The muscle or gland that responds to the stimulus (e.g., muscles in the arm to pull the hand away).
*   **Location of Reflex Arc Formation:** Reflex arcs are primarily formed in the **spinal cord**.
*   **Information Pathway:**
    *   The impulse travels along the sensory neuron to the spinal cord.
    *   In the spinal cord, it is transmitted to a relay neuron and then to a motor neuron.
    *   Simultaneously, the impulse also travels up the spinal cord to the **brain**, so that we become aware of the sensation (e.g., pain) *after* the reflex action has occurred.
*   **Evolutionary Significance:** Reflex arcs are highly efficient mechanisms for rapid functioning, especially in organisms with less developed brains. They remain important even in organisms with complex brains for quick responses.
*   **Example: Bright Light in Eyes:**
    1.  **Receptor:** Photoreceptors in the retina of the eye detect bright light.
    2.  **Sensory Neuron:** Transmits the impulse to the brainstem.
    3.  **Motor Neuron:** Impulses are sent back to the muscles of the eyelid.
    4.  **Effector:** Muscles contract, causing the eyelids to close.
    *   *Note:* While the reflex happens quickly, the brain also receives the signal, allowing us to consciously register the brightness.

### 2.3. Human Brain

*   **Role:** The main coordinating centre of the body. It also receives sensory information and sends out motor commands.
*   **Central Nervous System (CNS):** Consists of the **brain** and the **spinal cord**.
*   **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):**
    *   Consists of **cranial nerves** (originating from the brain) and **spinal nerves** (originating from the spinal cord).
    *   Facilitates communication between the CNS and the rest of the body.
*   **Functions of the Brain:**
    *   **Thinking and Decision Making:** Processing information, making decisions about voluntary actions.
    *   **Coordinating Voluntary Actions:** Sending signals to muscles for actions like writing, talking, walking.
    *   **Processing Sensory Information:** Integrating signals from various receptors.
*   **Major Parts of the Brain:**
    1.  **Fore-brain:**
        *   **Main Thinking Part:** Responsible for intelligence, memory, learning, and processing sensory information.
        *   **Sensory Areas:** Specific regions for hearing, smell, sight, etc.
        *   **Association Areas:** Integrate information from different senses and connect it with past experiences.
    2.  **Mid-brain:**
        *   Acts as a relay centre for sensory and motor pathways.
        *   Involved in reflexes like visual and auditory reflexes.
    3.  **Hind-brain:**
        *   Consists of three parts: cerebellum, pons, and medulla oblongata.
        *   **Cerebellum:** Controls posture, balance, and precise voluntary movements.
        *   **Medulla Oblongata:** Controls involuntary actions like heartbeat, breathing, and digestion.

## 3. Control and Coordination in Plants

*   **Movement in Plants:** Unlike animals, plants don't have a nervous system. Their control and coordination are achieved through **chemical signals** and **hormones**.
*   **Types of Plant Movements:**
    1.  **Nastic Movements:** Non-directional movements in response to a stimulus. The direction of the stimulus does not determine the direction of the response.
        *   *Example:* **Thigmonasty** (touch) in the *Mimosa pudica* (Touch-me-not) plant. When touched, the leaves fold down.
        *   *Mechanism:* Changes in the amount of water in the swollen cells (pulvini) at the base of the leaf stalks.
    2.  **Tropism (Tropic Movements):** Directional movements in response to a stimulus. The direction of the movement is determined by the direction of the stimulus.
        *   **Phototropism:** Movement in response to light.
            *   **Positive Phototropism:** Growth towards light (e.g., shoots of plants).
            *   **Negative Phototropism:** Growth away from light (e.g., roots).
            *   *Hormone Involved:* **Auxin**. Auxin accumulates on the shaded side of a shoot, promoting cell elongation on that side. This causes the shoot to bend towards the light.
        *   **Geotropism (Gravitropism):** Movement in response to gravity.
            *   **Positive Geotropism:** Growth downwards along with gravity (e.g., roots).
            *   **Negative Geotropism:** Growth upwards against gravity (e.g., shoots).
            *   *Hormone Involved:* **Auxin**. Auxin influences root growth differently; higher concentrations inhibit root elongation.
        *   **Hydrotropism:** Movement in response to water.
            *   **Positive Hydrotropism:** Growth towards water (e.g., roots). Roots show hydrotropism even if it means growing against gravity.
        *   **Thigmotropism:** Movement in response to touch (e.g., tendrils of climbing plants wrapping around a support).
*   **Plant Hormones (Phytohormones):** Chemical substances produced in certain parts of the plant that regulate growth and other functions.
    *   **Auxin:**
        *   Produced at the shoot tips.
        *   Promotes cell elongation.
        *   Responsible for phototropism and geotropism.
    *   **Gibberellins:**
        *   Promote stem elongation and bud growth.
        *   Help in flowering and fruit development.
    *   **Cytokinins:**
        *   Promote cell division.
        *   Help in the formation of new shoots and leaves.
    *   **Abscisic Acid (ABA):**
        *   Inhibits growth.
        *   Causes wilting of leaves (stomata closure).
        *   Helps plants withstand drought.
    *   **Ethylene (Ethylen):**
        *   Gaseous hormone.
        *   Promotes fruit ripening.
        *   Causes abscission (shedding) of leaves and flowers.

## 4. Chemical Coordination in Animals: Hormones

*   **Endocrine System:** A system of glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream.
*   **Hormones:** Chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands that travel through the bloodstream to target cells or organs and regulate specific functions.
*   **Difference from Nervous Control:**
    *   **Nervous Control:** Rapid, short-lived, electrical signals, acts on specific cells/muscles/glands.
    *   **Hormonal Control:** Slower, long-lasting, chemical signals, acts on target cells/organs throughout the body.
*   **Endocrine Glands and Hormones:**
    *   **Pituitary Gland (Master Gland):** Located at the base of the brain.
        *   *Hormones:* Growth Hormone (regulates growth), TSH (stimulates thyroid), FSH & LH (reproductive hormones), etc.
        *   *Function:* Controls many other endocrine glands.
    *   **Thyroid Gland:** Located in the neck.
        *   *Hormone:* Thyroxine (regulates metabolism, iodine is essential for its production).
    *   **Adrenal Glands:** Located on top of the kidneys.
        *   *Hormone:* Adrenaline (involved in "fight or flight" response, increases heart rate, blood pressure, breathing rate).
    *   **Pancreas:** Located behind the stomach.
        *   *Hormone:* Insulin (regulates blood sugar levels; deficiency causes diabetes).
    *   **Testes (in males):**
        *   *Hormone:* Testosterone (male sex characteristics).
    *   **Ovaries (in females):**
        *   *Hormone:* Estrogen and Progesterone (female sex characteristics, menstrual cycle).
*   **Action of Hormones:**
    *   Hormones are secreted into the bloodstream.
    *   They travel throughout the body but only affect **target cells** that have specific receptors for that hormone.
    *   This ensures that hormones exert their effects only where needed.
*   **Example: Insulin and Blood Sugar Regulation:**
    *   When blood sugar levels rise (e.g., after a meal), the pancreas releases insulin.
    *   Insulin promotes the uptake of glucose from the blood into cells (like liver and muscle cells) and converts it into glycogen for storage.
    *   This lowers blood sugar levels.
    *   If blood sugar levels drop too low, the pancreas stops releasing insulin.
*   **Example: Adrenaline and "Fight or Flight":**
    *   In stressful situations, the adrenal glands release adrenaline.
    *   Adrenaline prepares the body for intense physical activity by:
        *   Increasing heart rate.
        *   Increasing blood pressure.
        *   Increasing breathing rate.
        *   Diverting blood flow to skeletal muscles.
        *   Releasing stored glucose into the blood for energy.

## Summary Points

*   **Control and Coordination** are essential for all living organisms to respond to stimuli, adapt to their environment, and maintain life.
*   In animals, this is achieved by the **nervous system** (neurons, electrical impulses, brain, spinal cord) and the **endocrine system** (hormones, glands).
*   **Neurons** transmit information via electrical and chemical signals.
*   **Reflex actions** are rapid, involuntary responses mediated by **reflex arcs**, often involving the spinal cord.
*   The **brain** is the main control and coordination centre, responsible for thinking, memory, and voluntary actions.
*   In plants, **plant hormones** (like Auxin, Gibberellins, Cytokinins, ABA, Ethylene) regulate growth and responses to stimuli like light, gravity, and water.
*   **Tropisms** are directional growth responses in plants, while **nastic movements** are non-directional.
*   **Hormones** in animals are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands that regulate various physiological processes with slower but longer-lasting effects.
