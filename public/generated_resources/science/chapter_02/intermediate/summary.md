Acids are known for their sour taste and ability to turn blue litmus red, while bases are bitter and change red litmus to blue. Indicators, like litmus, turmeric, and synthetic ones such as phenolphthalein and methyl orange, help identify acids and bases by changing colour. Olfactory indicators, like onion and vanilla, change their odour in acidic or basic solutions.

Acids and bases react with metals to produce hydrogen gas. For example, when acids react with active metals, they form salt and hydrogen gas, which can be tested by bringing a burning matchstick near the gas; it burns with a pop sound. Acids also react with metal carbonates and bicarbonates to produce carbon dioxide gas, a salt, and water. This CO2 can be tested by passing it through lime water, which turns milky.

Neutralization is a key reaction where an acid and a base react to form salt and water, cancelling each other's properties. The heat produced during this reaction is exothermic. Acids and bases also react with metal oxides (basic in nature) and non-metal oxides (acidic in nature) respectively, forming salt and water.

The strength of acids and bases is measured on the pH scale, which ranges from 0 to 14. A pH of 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, and above 7 is basic. For instance, dilute HCl has a low pH, while dilute NaOH has a high pH.

Salts are compounds formed from the reaction of an acid and a base. They can be acidic, basic, or neutral depending on the relative strengths of the acid and base from which they are formed. For example, NaCl (sodium chloride) is a neutral salt, while ammonium chloride is an acidic salt.

Important salts like sodium chloride, sodium hydroxide, bleaching powder, baking soda, and washing soda have various applications in everyday life, from food preservation and cleaning to industrial uses.
