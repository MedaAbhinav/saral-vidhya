## 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

**Instructions:** Answer the following questions in 2-3 sentences or points.

1.  **Question:** You are provided with three test tubes. One contains distilled water, another an acidic solution, and the third a basic solution. If you are given only red litmus paper, how will you identify the contents of each test tube?
    **Model Answer:**
    Dip the red litmus paper into each test tube. The solution that turns red litmus blue is the basic solution. Now, use this blue litmus paper in the remaining two test tubes. The solution that turns blue litmus red is the acidic solution. The solution that shows no change with blue litmus (and originally with red litmus) is distilled water.

2.  **Question:** Give two examples each of natural, synthetic, and olfactory indicators mentioned in the chapter.
    **Model Answer:**
    *   **Natural Indicators:** Litmus, Turmeric (Red cabbage leaves, Hydrangea petals)
    *   **Synthetic Indicators:** Phenolphthalein, Methyl orange
    *   **Olfactory Indicators:** Onion, Vanilla essence (Clove oil)

3.  **Question:** Describe the observation and a simple test to identify the gas produced when dilute sulphuric acid reacts with zinc granules.
    **Model Answer:**
    When dilute sulphuric acid reacts with zinc granules, bubbles of a gas are observed on the surface of the zinc. To test this gas, bring a burning candle near the gas-filled bubble. The gas burns with a 'pop' sound, confirming it is hydrogen gas (H₂).

4.  **Question:** Write the chemical equation for the reaction of sodium carbonate with hydrochloric acid. What are the products formed?
    **Model Answer:**
    Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
    The products formed are sodium chloride (a salt), water, and carbon dioxide gas.

5.  **Question:** Explain why a stain of curry on a white cloth becomes reddish-brown when soap is scrubbed on it, and turns yellow again when washed with plenty of water.
    **Model Answer:**
    Curry contains turmeric, which is a natural indicator. Turmeric turns reddish-brown in basic medium. Soap is basic in nature, so when scrubbed, the curry stain turns reddish-brown. When the cloth is washed with plenty of water, the basic soap is removed, and the turmeric returns to its original yellow color in a neutral or slightly acidic medium.

6.  **Question:** What happens when carbon dioxide gas is passed through lime water (calcium hydroxide solution)? Write the chemical equation for the initial reaction.
    **Model Answer:**
    When carbon dioxide gas is passed through lime water, the solution turns milky due to the formation of a white precipitate of calcium carbonate.
    Equation: Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)

7.  **Question:** What is an olfactory indicator? Give an example and describe its behavior in acidic and basic media.
    **Model Answer:**
    Olfactory indicators are substances whose odour changes in acidic or basic media. An example is onion. The pungent smell of onion persists in acidic medium (e.g., dilute HCl) but disappears or diminishes significantly in basic medium (e.g., dilute NaOH).

8.  **Question:** Write the balanced chemical equation for the reaction of sodium hydroxide solution with zinc metal. What are the products formed?
    **Model Answer:**
    2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na₂ZnO₂(s) + H₂(g)
    The products formed are sodium zincate (a salt) and hydrogen gas.

9.  **Question:** Why are substances like lemon juice, vinegar, or baking soda solution suggested as remedies for acidity?
    **Model Answer:**
    Acidity is caused by excess acid in the stomach. Lemon juice and vinegar are acidic, so they would worsen the problem. Baking soda solution is basic in nature. It is suggested as a remedy because acids and bases nullify each other's effects. The baking soda, being a base, would neutralize the excess acid and relieve the discomfort.

10. **Question:** What is the similarity and difference in the products formed when metal carbonates and metal hydrogencarbonates react with acids?
    **Model Answer:**
    **Similarity:** Both metal carbonates and metal hydrogencarbonates react with acids to produce the same set of products: a corresponding salt, carbon dioxide gas, and water.
    **Difference:** The amount of acid required to react completely with a given mass of metal carbonate is different from that required for the same mass of metal hydrogencarbonate due to their different chemical formulas (e.g., Na₂CO₃ vs NaHCO₃).

## 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

**Instructions:** Provide an outline answer for each question.

1.  **Question:** Describe the general reactions of acids with metals, metal carbonates, and metal hydrogencarbonates. For each type of reaction, write a general word equation and a specific balanced chemical equation with examples provided in the chapter. Also, describe the test for any gas produced in these reactions.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Acids with Metals:**
        *   **General Word Equation:** Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas
        *   **Example (Eq.):** H₂SO₄(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
        *   **Test for H₂ gas:** Bring a burning candle near the gas. It burns with a 'pop' sound.
    *   **Acids with Metal Carbonates:**
        *   **General Word Equation:** Metal carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
        *   **Example (Eq.):** Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
        *   **Test for CO₂ gas:** Pass the gas through lime water (Ca(OH)₂ solution). It turns milky.
    *   **Acids with Metal Hydrogencarbonates:**
        *   **General Word Equation:** Metal hydrogencarbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water
        *   **Example (Eq.):** NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
        *   **Test for CO₂ gas:** Same as above, passes through lime water, turns milky.

2.  **Question:** Explain different types of indicators used to test for acids and bases, as described in your chapter. Include natural, synthetic, and olfactory indicators, giving at least two examples for each and how they indicate the presence of an acid or a base.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction to Indicators:** Substances that change colour or odour in acidic or basic media.
    *   **Natural Indicators:**
        *   **Description:** Derived from plants.
        *   **Examples:** Litmus (red in acid, blue in base), Turmeric (yellow in acid/neutral, reddish-brown in base).
        *   **How they work:** Change colour in response to pH.
    *   **Synthetic Indicators:**
        *   **Description:** Man-made chemical indicators.
        *   **Examples:** Phenolphthalein (colorless in acid, pink in base), Methyl orange (red in acid, yellow in base).
        *   **How they work:** Chemical structure changes with pH, leading to colour change.
    *   **Olfactory Indicators:**
        *   **Description:** Substances whose odour changes in acidic or basic media.
        *   **Examples:** Onion (smell persists in acid, diminishes/disappears in base), Vanilla essence (smell persists in acid, diminishes/disappears in base).
        *   **How they work:** Olfactory receptors are affected by the presence of acids or bases, altering the perception of smell.

3.  **Question:** Design an experiment to demonstrate that acids react with metals to produce hydrogen gas. Include the apparatus setup, materials required, procedure, observations, and the conclusion.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Aim:** To show that acids react with metals to produce hydrogen gas.
    *   **Materials Required:** Test tube, dilute sulphuric acid (or HCl), zinc granules, cork with delivery tube, trough, soap solution, burning candle.
    *   **Apparatus Setup (referencing Fig. 2.1):** A test tube with zinc granules and dilute acid, stoppered with a delivery tube leading into a trough containing soap solution.
    *   **Procedure:**
        1.  Take about 5 mL of dilute sulphuric acid in a test tube.
        2.  Add a few pieces of zinc granules to it.
        3.  Fit a cork with a delivery tube to the test tube.
        4.  Pass the evolved gas through a soap solution.
        5.  Bring a burning candle near the gas-filled bubbles collected in the soap solution.
    *   **Observations:**
        1.  Bubbles of gas are observed on the surface of zinc granules.
        2.  Bubbles are formed in the soap solution.
        3.  The gas-filled bubbles burn with a 'pop' sound when a burning candle is brought near them.
    *   **Conclusion:** The 'pop' sound indicates the presence of hydrogen gas, confirming that acids react with metals to produce hydrogen gas.
    *   **Relevant Equation:** H₂SO₄(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)

4.  **Question:** Explain the complete chemistry behind the "lime water test" for carbon dioxide gas. Describe what happens when CO₂ is passed through lime water initially, and then what happens if excess CO₂ is passed. Write balanced chemical equations for both stages.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Introduction:** Lime water (calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂) is used to detect carbon dioxide gas.
    *   **Stage 1: Initial Passage of CO₂:**
        *   **Observation:** When CO₂ is passed through lime water, it turns milky/cloudy.
        *   **Reason:** This is due to the formation of an insoluble white precipitate, calcium carbonate (CaCO₃).
        *   **Equation:** Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)
    *   **Stage 2: Passage of Excess CO₂:**
        *   **Observation:** If excess CO₂ is passed through the milky solution, the milkiness disappears, and the solution becomes clear again.
        *   **Reason:** The insoluble calcium carbonate reacts further with excess carbon dioxide and water to form calcium hydrogencarbonate (Ca(HCO₃)₂), which is soluble in water.
        *   **Equation:** CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) → Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq)
    *   **Significance:** This two-stage process helps confirm the presence of CO₂ uniquely.

5.  **Question:** Compare and contrast the reaction of zinc metal with dilute sulphuric acid and with dilute sodium hydroxide solution. Highlight the similarities and differences in terms of products and general applicability.
    **Outline Answer:**
    *   **Similarities:**
        *   Both reactions produce hydrogen gas (H₂).
        *   Both involve zinc metal as a reactant.
    *   **Differences:**
        *   **Reactant Type:** In the first case, zinc reacts with an acid (dilute H₂SO₄). In the second, it reacts with a base (dilute NaOH).
        *   **Other Product:** With acid, the other product is a salt (zinc sulphate, ZnSO₄). With base, the other product is sodium zincate (Na₂ZnO₂), which is also a salt.
        *   **General Applicability:** The reaction of metals with acids to produce hydrogen gas is a general property of most reactive metals. However, the reaction of metals with bases to produce hydrogen gas is not applicable to all metals; only a few metals like zinc and aluminium react with strong bases.
    *   **Equations:**
        *   Zn(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
        *   Zn(s) + 2NaOH(aq) → Na₂ZnO₂(s) + H₂(g)
    *   **Conclusion:** Zinc is an amphoteric metal, capable of reacting with both acids and strong bases to produce hydrogen gas, though the overall reaction pattern and applicability differ.

## 4. Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) Questions

1.  **Question:** A housewife observes that her marble kitchen countertop (calcium carbonate) has developed dull spots over time, especially near where she uses lemons and vinegar. Explain the chemical reason behind the dullness and suggest a way to prevent further damage.
    **Explanation:**
    *   Marble is primarily calcium carbonate (CaCO₃).
    *   Lemons contain citric acid, and vinegar contains acetic acid (both acids).
    *   When acidic substances like lemon juice or vinegar come into contact with marble, they react with calcium carbonate.
    *   The reaction follows: Metal carbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water.
    *   CaCO₃(s) + 2H⁺(aq) → Ca²⁺(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) (simplified, where H⁺ represents acid)
    *   This reaction slowly dissolves the marble surface, creating pits and etching, which leads to the dull, uneven spots observed. The smooth, polished surface is corroded away.
    **Prevention:**
    *   Immediately wipe off any acidic spills (lemon juice, vinegar, fruit juices, sodas, etc.) from the marble surface.
    *   Use cutting boards or coasters under acidic food items or liquids.
    *   Avoid cleaning marble with acidic cleaners. Use pH-neutral cleaners instead.

2.  **Question:** You are given two unlabeled test tubes, one containing hydrogen gas and the other containing carbon dioxide gas. How would you chemically distinguish between these two gases without using any complex laboratory equipment, using only the materials readily available in a basic lab (e.g., lime water, burning splint)?
    **Approach:**
    *   **Test for Hydrogen Gas:** Bring a burning splint (or candle) near the mouth of each test tube. If the gas burns with a 'pop' sound, it is hydrogen gas (H₂).
    *   **Test for Carbon Dioxide Gas:** After identifying hydrogen, the remaining test tube will contain carbon dioxide. To confirm, pour a small amount of lime water (calcium hydroxide solution) into this test tube, close it, and shake gently. If the lime water turns milky, it is carbon dioxide gas (CO₂).
    *   **Reasoning:** These are specific and distinct tests for each gas mentioned in the chapter. Hydrogen is combustible and gives a 'pop' sound, while carbon dioxide is non-combustible and reacts with lime water to form a white precipitate.

3.  **Question:** A student performs Activity 2.2 using dilute HCl and dilute NaOH with vanilla essence. They record that vanilla essence's odour remains unchanged with HCl but disappears with NaOH. Based on this observation, the student concludes that vanilla essence is a good olfactory indicator for both acids and bases. Is the student's conclusion entirely correct? Justify your answer based on the definition of an olfactory indicator.
    **Justification:**
    *   The student's conclusion that vanilla essence is an olfactory indicator is partially correct, but not for *both* acids and bases in the way described.
    *   An olfactory indicator is a substance whose *odour changes* in acidic or basic media.
    *   In the student's experiment:
        *   With HCl (acid): Odour remains unchanged. This means it *does not indicate* the presence of an acid by a change in odour.
        *   With NaOH (base): Odour disappears. This *does indicate* the presence of a base by a change (disappearance) in odour.
    *   Therefore, vanilla essence, based on this observation, is effective as an olfactory indicator for *bases* (as its odour changes/disappears), but not for *acids* (as its odour does not change). The conclusion that it's a good olfactory indicator for *both* is incorrect; it only shows a significant change in basic medium.
