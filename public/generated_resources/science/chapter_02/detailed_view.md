# NCERT Class 10 Science: Chapter 2 - Acids, Bases and Salts (Detailed Chapter Notes)

## Introduction

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of acids, bases, and salts, exploring their properties, reactions, and applications in daily life.

*   **Sour taste** in food items is due to **acids**.
*   **Bitter taste** in food items is due to **bases**.
*   **Remedy for acidity:** If someone has acidity after overeating, **baking soda solution** would be suggested.
    *   **Reasoning:** Acidity is caused by excess acid. Baking soda is a base, and it will **neutralize** the effect of the acid. This demonstrates the property of acids and bases to nullify each other.
*   **Testing substances:** We use **indicators** to test if a substance is sour (acidic) or bitter (basic) without tasting them.

### Indicators

Indicators are substances that change their colour or odour when added to acidic or basic solutions.

*   **Litmus:**
    *   A **natural indicator**.
    *   Extracted from **lichen** (a plant from the Thallophyta division).
    *   **Litmus solution** is **purple** when neither acidic nor basic.
    *   **Acid:** Changes **blue litmus** to **red**.
    *   **Base:** Changes **red litmus** to **blue**.
*   **Turmeric:**
    *   Another **natural indicator**.
    *   A stain of curry (turmeric) on a white cloth turns **reddish-brown** when soap (basic) is scrubbed on it.
    *   It turns **yellow** again when washed with plenty of water.
*   **Synthetic Indicators:**
    *   **Phenolphthalein:** Colourless in acid, **pink** in base.
    *   **Methyl Orange:** **Red** in acid, **yellow** in base.
*   **Other Natural Indicators:** Red cabbage leaves, coloured petals of flowers like Hydrangea, Petunia, and Geranium. These also indicate the presence of acid or base by changing colour.

### In-Text Question (Page 18)

**1. You have been provided with three test tubes. One of them contains distilled water and the other two contain an acidic solution and a basic solution, respectively. If you are given only red litmus paper, how will you identify the contents of each test tube?**

**Solution:**

1.  **Test all three solutions with red litmus paper.**
    *   **Basic solution:** The solution that turns **red litmus paper blue** is the basic solution.
2.  **Now you have a blue litmus paper** (from the basic solution) and the remaining two solutions (acidic and distilled water).
3.  **Use the blue litmus paper to test the remaining two solutions.**
    *   **Acidic solution:** The solution that turns **blue litmus paper red** is the acidic solution.
    *   **Distilled water:** The solution that shows **no change** in the colour of blue litmus paper (and subsequently red litmus paper) is distilled water (which is neutral).

### Olfactory Indicators

*   **Definition:** These are substances whose **odour changes** in acidic or basic media.
*   **Examples:** Onion, vanilla essence, clove oil.

**Activity 2.2: Testing Olfactory Indicators**

*   **Objective:** To identify substances that act as olfactory indicators.
*   **Materials:** Finely chopped onions, cloth strips, dilute HCl, dilute NaOH, dilute vanilla essence, clove oil.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Prepare onion strips: Keep chopped onions with cloth strips overnight in a plastic bag in the fridge.
    2.  Check the initial odour of onion strips.
    3.  Put dilute HCl on one strip and dilute NaOH on another. Rinse and recheck odour.
    4.  Similarly, test vanilla essence and clove oil with dilute HCl and dilute NaOH.
*   **Observations:**
    *   **Onion:**
        *   In **acidic** medium (HCl): Odour **persists**.
        *   In **basic** medium (NaOH): Odour **disappears**.
    *   **Vanilla Essence:**
        *   In **acidic** medium (HCl): Odour **persists**.
        *   In **basic** medium (NaOH): Odour **disappears/reduced**.
    *   **Clove Oil:**
        *   In **acidic** medium (HCl): Odour **persists**.
        *   In **basic** medium (NaOH): Odour **disappears/reduced**.
*   **Conclusion:** Onion, vanilla, and clove can be used as **olfactory indicators** because their odour changes in basic media, while remaining unchanged in acidic media.

### 2.1.2 How do Acids and Bases React with Metals?

**Activity 2.3: Reaction of Sulphuric Acid with Zinc**

*   **Objective:** To observe the reaction of an acid with a metal and identify the gas produced.
*   **Materials:** Dilute sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), zinc granules, test tube, delivery tube, trough, soap solution, burning candle.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Take ~5 mL dilute H₂SO₄ in a test tube.
    2.  Add a few zinc granules.
    3.  Observe the surface of zinc granules.
    4.  Pass the evolved gas through soap solution.
    5.  Bring a burning candle near the gas-filled bubbles.
    6.  Repeat with HCl, HNO₃, CH₃COOH.
*   **Observations:**
    1.  **Bubbles** are observed on the surface of zinc granules.
    2.  Gas bubbles are formed in the soap solution, indicating gas evolution.
    3.  When a burning candle is brought near the bubbles, the gas burns with a **'pop' sound**. This confirms the presence of **hydrogen gas (H₂)**.
    4.  Similar observations are seen with other acids like HCl. (Note: Highly oxidizing acids like concentrated HNO₃ react differently and don't typically produce H₂ gas).
*   **General Reaction:**
    **Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas**
*   **Example Reactions:**
    *   2HCl(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g)
    *   H₂SO₄(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
    *   2CH₃COOH(aq) + Zn(s) → (CH₃COO)₂Zn(aq) + H₂(g) (Zinc acetate)
*   **Note:** The metal displaces hydrogen atoms from the acid as hydrogen gas and forms a salt.

**Activity 2.4: Reaction of Sodium Hydroxide with Zinc**

*   **Objective:** To observe the reaction of a base with a metal.
*   **Materials:** Granulated zinc metal, sodium hydroxide solution (NaOH), test tube.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Place a few pieces of granulated zinc in a test tube.
    2.  Add 2 mL of NaOH solution and warm.
    3.  Test the evolved gas similar to Activity 2.3.
*   **Observations:** Hydrogen gas is formed, confirmed by the 'pop' sound.
*   **Reaction:**
    **2NaOH(aq) + Zn(s) → Na₂ZnO₂(s) + H₂(g)**
    (Sodium zincate)
*   **Conclusion:** Some metals react with strong bases to produce hydrogen gas and a salt. However, such reactions are **not possible with all metals**. (e.g., Aluminium also reacts with strong bases like NaOH to produce H₂).

### 2.1.3 How do Metal Carbonates and Metal Hydrogencarbonates React with Acids?

**Activity 2.5: Reaction with Acids**

*   **Objective:** To observe the reaction of metal carbonates and hydrogencarbonates with acids and identify the gas produced.
*   **Materials:** Test tubes (A & B), sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃), sodium hydrogencarbonate (NaHCO₃), dilute HCl, lime water [calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)₂ solution], delivery tube.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  **Test tube A:** 0.5 g Na₂CO₃ + 2 mL dilute HCl.
    2.  **Test tube B:** 0.5 g NaHCO₃ + 2 mL dilute HCl.
    3.  Observe both test tubes.
    4.  Pass the gas produced in each case through lime water.
*   **Observations:**
    1.  **Effervescence** (brisk bubbling) is observed in both test tubes, indicating the evolution of a gas.
    2.  When the gas from both reactions is passed through lime water:
        *   The lime water **turns milky (or cloudy)**. This is a characteristic test for **carbon dioxide (CO₂)** gas.
*   **Reactions:**
    *   **Test tube A (Metal Carbonate):**
        Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
    *   **Test tube B (Metal Hydrogencarbonate):**
        NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
*   **Test for CO₂ (Reaction with Lime Water):**
    *   Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)
        (Lime water)           (White precipitate)
    *   The white precipitate of **calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)** causes the milkiness.
*   **Effect of Excess CO₂:**
    *   If excess CO₂ is passed through the milky solution, the milkiness **disappears**.
    *   CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) → Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq)
        (Soluble in water)
    *   Calcium hydrogencarbonate [Ca(HCO₃)₂] is soluble in water, so the precipitate dissolves.
*   **General Reaction:**
    **Metal carbonate / Metal hydrogencarbonate + Acid → Salt + Carbon dioxide + Water**
*   **Examples:** Limestone, chalk, and marble are all different forms of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃).

### 2.1.4 How do Acids and Bases React with each other? (Neutralisation Reaction)

**Activity 2.6: Neutralisation**

*   **Objective:** To demonstrate the reaction between an acid and a base.
*   **Materials:** Dilute NaOH solution, phenolphthalein solution, dilute HCl solution.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Take ~2 mL of dilute NaOH solution in a test tube.
    2.  Add two drops of phenolphthalein solution. Note the colour.
    3.  Add dilute HCl solution drop by drop to the above solution, stirring gently.
    4.  Observe any colour change.
    5.  Add a few drops of NaOH to the mixture.
*   **Observations:**
    1.  NaOH solution with phenolphthalein turns **pink** (as NaOH is a base).
    2.  As dilute HCl is added drop by drop, the pink colour **disappears** when enough acid has been added. This indicates that the base has been neutralized by the acid.
    3.  When a few drops of NaOH are added back, the pink colour **reappears**, showing that the solution has again become basic.
*   **Conclusion:** The effect of a base is nullified by an acid, and vice-versa. This mutual nullification is called **neutralisation**.
*   **Definition:** The reaction between an acid and a base to give a salt and water is known as a **neutralisation reaction**.
*   **General Reaction:**
    **Base + Acid → Salt + Water**
*   **Example Reaction:**
    NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
    (Sodium hydroxide) (Hydrochloric acid) (Sodium chloride) (Water)

### 2.1.5 Reaction of Metallic Oxides with Acids

**Activity 2.7: Copper Oxide with HCl**

*   **Objective:** To observe the reaction between a metallic oxide and an acid.
*   **Materials:** Small amount of copper oxide (CuO), dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl), beaker, stirring rod.
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Take a small amount of copper oxide in a beaker.
    2.  Add dilute hydrochloric acid slowly while stirring.
    3.  Note the colour of the solution and what happens to the copper oxide.
*   **Observations:**
    1.  The colour of the solution becomes **blue-green**.
    2.  The copper oxide **dissolves**.
*   **Explanation:** The blue-green colour is due to the formation of **copper(II) chloride (CuCl₂)** in the solution.
*   **Reaction:**
    **CuO(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CuCl₂(aq) + H₂O(l)**
*   **General Reaction:**
    **Metal oxide + Acid → Salt + Water**
*   **Conclusion:** Since metallic oxides react with acids to produce salt and water, which is similar to the reaction of a base with an acid (neutralisation reaction), **metallic oxides are considered basic oxides**.

### 2.1.6 Reaction of a Non-metallic Oxide with Base

*   **Reference:** Recall Activity 2.5, where carbon dioxide (a non-metallic oxide) was passed through lime water (calcium hydroxide, a base).
*   **Reaction from Activity 2.5:**
    Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)
    (Base) (Non-metallic oxide) (Salt) (Water)
*   **Conclusion:** This reaction is similar to a neutralisation reaction between a base and an acid. Therefore, **non-metallic oxides are acidic in nature** because they react with bases to form salt and water.
