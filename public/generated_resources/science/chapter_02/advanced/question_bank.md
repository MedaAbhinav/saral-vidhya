### 2. Case Study / Passage-Based Questions (5 Questions)

**Passage 1: The Importance of pH in Biological Systems**

The pH of a solution is a measure of its acidity or alkalinity. In biological systems, maintaining a specific pH range is crucial for the proper functioning of enzymes and other biological processes. For instance, the human stomach has a highly acidic environment (pH 1.5-3.5) due to the presence of hydrochloric acid, which is essential for digesting food and killing harmful microorganisms. However, if the stomach produces too much acid, it can lead to discomfort and ulcers. In contrast, the small intestine is slightly alkaline (pH 7-8.5), which is the optimal pH for the action of digestive enzymes like trypsin. Similarly, blood plasma is maintained at a very narrow pH range of 7.35-7.45. Deviations from this range can be life-threatening. Indicators play a vital role in monitoring pH in various applications, from laboratory experiments to environmental testing and even in biological assays.

1.  **Question (3 marks):** A person experiencing heartburn due to excess stomach acid is advised to take an antacid. Based on the information provided and your knowledge of acids and bases, explain why an antacid helps. Give an example of a common antacid and the chemical reaction involved.

2.  **Question (4 marks):** The enzymes in our small intestine function optimally in a slightly alkaline medium. If the chyme (partially digested food) entering the small intestine from the stomach is too acidic, how might the body regulate this to ensure proper enzyme activity? Mention the role of any specific secretion if you know it.

3.  **Question (3 marks):** Explain why the pH of blood is maintained within a very narrow range, and what might happen if this pH deviates significantly. What term is used to describe systems that resist changes in pH?

**Passage 2: The Chemistry of Cleaning Agents**

Soaps and detergents are widely used as cleaning agents. Soaps are typically sodium or potassium salts of long-chain fatty acids. When soap is added to water containing grease and oil, the soap molecules surround the grease particles, forming micelles. The hydrophobic tails of the soap molecules point inwards towards the grease, while the hydrophilic heads point outwards towards the water, allowing the grease to be suspended in water and washed away. Detergents are synthetic cleaning agents that often contain sulfates or sulfonates. They are generally more effective than soaps in hard water. Hard water contains dissolved calcium and magnesium ions, which react with soap to form insoluble precipitates (soap scum), reducing the soap's effectiveness. Detergents, however, are designed to be less affected by these ions.

1.  **Question (3 marks):** A person experiencing heartburn due to excess stomach acid is advised to take an antacid. Based on the information provided and your knowledge of acids and bases, explain why an antacid helps. Give an example of a common antacid and the chemical reaction involved.

        Mg(OH)₂(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂(aq) + 2H₂O(l)

    *   **Importance of Narrow pH Range:** Blood pH must be maintained within a narrow range (7.35-7.45) because enzymes and other proteins in the body are highly sensitive to pH. It undergoes hydrolysis, making the solution slightly alkaline. Phenolphthalein is pink in alkaline solutions). Let's re-label these identified solutions as **Water** and **Sodium Acetate**.

Sodium acetate is a salt that hydrolyzes to produce a slightly alkaline solution, whereas distilled water is neutral.

2.  **Question (5 marks):** A common laboratory preparation involves reacting dilute hydrochloric acid with sodium carbonate. Explain the chemical reactions occurring at each stage, including the initial reaction, the gas evolved, its properties, and how it is tested. Write balanced chemical equations for all the reactions. Discuss why sodium bicarbonate would produce a slower rate of gas evolution compared to sodium carbonate under similar conditions.
    *   **Reactions:**
        *   **Initial Reaction:** Dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl) reacts with solid sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃).
            **Equation 1:** Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
            *   This reaction produces sodium chloride (a salt), water, and carbon dioxide gas.
        *   **Gas Evolved:** The gas evolved is **carbon dioxide (CO₂)**.
        *   **Properties of CO₂:**
            *   Colourless gas.
            *   Odourless gas (in small quantities, though concentrated CO₂ can have a sharp smell).
            *   Does not support combustion (relights a burning candle).
            *   Turns lime water (calcium hydroxide solution) milky due to the formation of insoluble calcium carbonate.
        *   **Testing for CO₂:** The gas evolved is passed through lime water (Ca(OH)₂ solution).
            **Equation 2 (Testing):** Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l)
            *   The formation of a white precipitate (CaCO₃) indicates the presence of carbon dioxide.
        *   **Excess CO₂ Reaction:** If excess carbon dioxide is passed through the milky lime water, the precipitate dissolves.
            **Equation 3 (Excess CO₂):** CaCO₃(s) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) → Ca(HCO₃)₂(aq)
            *   Calcium bicarbonate is soluble in water.
    *   **Slower Rate with Sodium Bicarbonate:**
        *   Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) is sodium hydrogencarbonate. It is the acid salt of carbonic acid.
        *   When dilute HCl reacts with sodium bicarbonate:
            **Equation 4:** NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
        *   **Reason for slower rate:** In the case of sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃), the initial reaction releases CO₂. However, Na₂CO₃ is the salt of a stronger acid (carbonic acid, H₂CO₃) than NaHCO₃ is *as a reactant*. When HCl reacts with Na₂CO₃, it's a direct displacement of carbonic acid from its salt. With NaHCO₃, the reaction also displaces carbonic acid, but the concentration of available reactive species might be slightly different, or the intermediate steps might have slightly different activation energies.
        *   **More detailed explanation:** Na₂CO₃ contains two carbonate ions per formula unit, whereas NaHCO₃ contains one bicarbonate ion per formula unit. The reaction with Na₂CO₃ involves the conversion of carbonate (CO₃²⁻) to carbonic acid, which then decomposes. The reaction with NaHCO₃ involves the conversion of bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) to carbonic acid. While both produce CO₂, the rate-determining step might be influenced by the initial species. Often, the reaction with NaHCO₃ is observed to be slower because carbonic acid is formed directly in one step, whereas with Na₂CO₃, there might be an initial formation of bicarbonate intermediate in some cases before full decomposition, or the direct reaction of CO₃²⁻ might be faster. A key factor is the relative stability of the intermediate species and the activation energy of the primary reaction step. In practice, NaHCO₃ often leads to slower effervescence.

3.  **Question (5 marks):** Consider the reaction between a metal hydroxide and a non-metal oxide. If the metal hydroxide is a strong base like NaOH and the non-metal oxide is an acidic oxide like CO₂, what type of reaction occurs, and what are the products? Explain the acidic nature of non-metal oxides and the basic nature of metal hydroxides with reference to their chemical compositions and bonding.
    *   **Reaction Type and Products:** The reaction between a metal hydroxide (base) and a non-metal oxide (acidic oxide) is a **neutralization reaction**.
        *   **Equation:** 2NaOH(aq) + CO₂(g) → Na₂CO₃(aq) + H₂O(l)
        *   **Products:** Sodium carbonate (a salt) and water.
    *   **Nature of Non-metal Oxides:**
        *   **Chemical Composition & Bonding:** Non-metal oxides are typically formed by the reaction of non-metals (like C, S, N, P) with oxygen. The bonding involved is primarily **covalent**. For example, in CO₂, carbon and oxygen atoms are linked by covalent bonds.
        *   **Acidic Nature:** When dissolved in water, non-metal oxides react to form acids.
            *   CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (Carbonic acid)
            *   SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ (Sulfurous acid)
            *   SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄ (Sulfuric acid)
            *   This acidic nature arises because the bond between the non-metal and oxygen is polar covalent, and when reacting with water, the hydrogen atoms from water can be displaced, or the oxide itself effectively adds an acidic functional group. The presence of polar covalent bonds and the ability to donate H⁺ ions in aqueous solution characterize their acidic behavior.
    *   **Nature of Metal Hydroxides:**
        *   **Chemical Composition & Bonding:** Metal hydroxides are compounds formed between metals (like Na, K, Ca, Mg) and the hydroxide group (OH⁻). The bonding involves **ionic character** between the metal cation (e.g., Na⁺) and the hydroxide anion (OH⁻), or polar covalent bonds within the hydroxide group and ionic bonds to the metal. For example, in NaOH, there is an ionic bond between Na⁺ and OH⁻.
        *   **Basic Nature:** Metal hydroxides dissociate in water to release hydroxide ions (OH⁻), which are responsible for their basic properties.
            *   NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)
            *   Ca(OH)₂(aq) ⇌ Ca²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq)
            These free hydroxide ions can accept protons (H⁺) from acids, thus neutralizing them. The ability to produce OH⁻ ions in solution is the hallmark of bases.

4.  **Question (5 marks):** You observe that when a piece of zinc is added to dilute sulfuric acid, hydrogen gas is evolved. However, when the same amount of zinc is added to a solution of magnesium sulfate, no visible reaction occurs. Explain this difference in reactivity based on the concept of the reactivity series of metals. What would happen if you tried to react sodium metal with dilute sulfuric acid? Justify your answer.
    *   **Difference in Reactivity (Zn with H₂SO₄ vs. MgSO₄):**
        *   **Zn + Dilute H₂SO₄:** Zinc (Zn) is more reactive than hydrogen (H). In the reactivity series, Zn is placed above H. Therefore, Zn can displace hydrogen from dilute acids. The reaction is:
            Zn(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
            Hydrogen gas is evolved.
        *   **Zn + MgSO₄:** Magnesium (Mg) is significantly more reactive than zinc (Zn). In the reactivity series, Mg is placed much higher than Zn. Therefore, Zn is not reactive enough to displace Mg from its salt solution (magnesium sulfate). The reaction would require a metal higher in the reactivity series to displace a metal lower in the series. Since Zn is lower than Mg, no reaction occurs.
            Zn(s) + MgSO₄(aq) → No reaction
    *   **Reaction of Sodium Metal with Dilute Sulfuric Acid:**
        *   Sodium (Na) is a highly reactive metal, placed much higher in the reactivity series than both zinc and hydrogen.
        *   When sodium metal is added to dilute sulfuric acid, it will react **very vigorously and explosively**.
        *   **Reaction:** 2Na(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → Na₂SO₄(aq) + H₂(g)
        *   **Justification:** Sodium reacts with water present in the dilute acid exothermically, producing sodium hydroxide and hydrogen gas. The heat generated is often sufficient to ignite the evolved hydrogen gas, leading to an explosion. The reaction with the acid is also very rapid.
            *   Initial reaction with water: 2Na(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H₂(g) (Highly exothermic, ignites H₂)

5.  **Question (5 marks):** The formation of acid rain is a significant environmental problem. Identify the primary chemical species responsible for acid rain and explain the chemical reactions involved in their formation from atmospheric pollutants. Describe how acid rain affects aquatic life and the structural integrity of buildings. Propose two chemical methods to mitigate the effects of acid rain on a lake.
    *   **Primary Chemical Species Responsible:** The primary chemical species responsible for acid rain are **sulfur dioxide (SO₂)** and **nitrogen oxides (NOₓ)**, which react with water, oxygen, and other chemicals in the atmosphere to form sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃).
    *   **Chemical Reactions:**
        *   **Formation of Sulfuric Acid:**
            1.  Sulfur dioxide is released from the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil) containing sulfur impurities.
            2.  SO₂ reacts with oxygen in the air (often catalyzed by sunlight or metal particles):
                2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2SO₃(g)
            3.  Sulfur trioxide reacts with water in the atmosphere:
                SO₃(g) + H₂O(l) → H₂SO₄(aq) (Sulfuric acid)
        *   **Formation of Nitric Acid:**
            1.  Nitrogen oxides (primarily NO and NO₂) are formed during high-temperature combustion processes, such as in vehicle engines and power plants.
            2.  NO reacts with oxygen:
                2NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO₂(g)
            3.  Nitrogen dioxide reacts with water (and can be further oxidized):
                3NO₂(g) + H₂O(l) → 2HNO₃(aq) + NO(g) (Nitric acid)
    *   **Effects of Acid Rain:**
        *   **Aquatic Life:**
            *   Lowers the pH of lakes and rivers, making them acidic.
            *   Many fish species and other aquatic organisms cannot survive in acidic water (optimal pH for most is 6.5-8.0).
            *   Acidification can release toxic metals (like aluminum) from soil and rocks into the water, further harming aquatic life.
            *   Affects reproduction and development of aquatic organisms.
        *   **Structural Integrity of Buildings:**
            *   Reacts with building materials, especially those containing calcium carbonate (limestone, marble, statues).
            *   The acid dissolves the carbonate, causing erosion and damage.
            *   **Reaction:** CaCO₃(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CaSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
            *   CaSO₄ (calcium sulfate) is slightly soluble and can flake off.
            *   Acid rain can also corrode metals and damage paint.
    *   **Mitigation Methods for a Lake:**
        1.  **Liming (Adding alkaline substances):** The most common chemical method is to add alkaline materials like **calcium carbonate (CaCO₃)** or **calcium oxide (CaO)** or **calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂)** to the lake. These substances neutralize the acidity by reacting with the acids present.
            *   **Reaction:** H₂SO₄(aq) + CaCO₃(s) → CaSO₄(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
            *   This raises the pH of the lake water, making it more hospitable for aquatic life.
        2.  **Using Natural Buffering:** If the lakebed contains limestone or dolomite (calcium and magnesium carbonates), these rocks can naturally buffer the acidity to some extent. However, this buffering capacity can be overwhelmed by severe acid rain. Enhancing this buffering capacity might involve adding more calcium or magnesium compounds that can react with acids, similar to liming.
        *(Alternative mitigation often involves reducing emissions, but chemical mitigation for an existing affected lake is typically liming.)*

1.  **Question (5 marks):** Design an experiment to determine the concentration of an unknown solution of sulfuric acid using a standard solution of sodium hydroxide. Your design should include:
    *   The necessary apparatus and materials.
    *   A clear, step-by-step procedure.
    *   The balanced chemical equation for the reaction.
    *   The principle behind the titration.
    *   How you would calculate the concentration of the unknown acid solution, assuming you know the concentration of the standard base.

    *   **Apparatus and Materials:**
        *   Burette (50 mL)
        *   Pipette (20 mL or 25 mL)
        *   Conical flask (250 mL)
        *   Beaker
        *   Funnel
        *   Stand and clamp
        *   Wash bottle with distilled water
        *   Standard solution of NaOH (e.g., 0.1 M) of known concentration
        *   Unknown solution of H₂SO₄
        *   Methyl orange or phenolphthalein indicator (methyl orange is preferred for strong acid-strong base, but phenolphthalein is also commonly used for H₂SO₄/NaOH)
        *   Dropper

    *   **Step-by-Step Procedure:**
        1.  **Preparation:**
            *   Rinse the burette with distilled water and then with a small amount of the standard NaOH solution. Fill the burette with the standard NaOH solution. Ensure there are no air bubbles in the nozzle. Record the initial burette reading.
            *   Rinse the pipette with distilled water and then with a small amount of the unknown H₂SO₄ solution. Pipette exactly 20 mL (or 25 mL) of the unknown H₂SO₄ solution into a clean conical flask.
            *   Add 2-3 drops of methyl orange indicator to the H₂SO₄ in the conical flask. The solution will turn red.
        2.  **Titration:**
            *   Place the conical flask under the burette.
            *   Slowly add the NaOH solution from the burette to the H₂SO₄ solution in the flask, while continuously swirling the flask.
            *   As the NaOH is added, observe the colour change at the point where the NaOH stream enters the acid. Initially, a pink colour will appear and disappear as you swirl.
            *   When the pink colour starts to persist for longer, add the NaOH drop by drop.
            *   The **endpoint** is reached when the indicator just changes colour. For methyl orange with strong acid/strong base, this is the first permanent appearance of a yellow colour (neutralization point). For phenolphthalein, it's the first permanent faint pink colour.
            *   Stop adding NaOH and record the final burette reading.
        3.  **Concordant Readings:** Repeat the titration (steps 2-2.4) at least two more times to obtain at least three readings where the difference between the highest and lowest is within 0.1 mL. These are called concordant readings. Use the average of concordant readings for calculations.

    *   **Balanced Chemical Equation:**
        H₂SO₄(aq) + 2NaOH(aq) → Na₂SO₄(aq) + 2H₂O(l)

    *   **Principle Behind Titration:** This is an **acid-base titration**. It is based on the principle of **neutralization**, where an acid reacts completely with a base. At the **equivalence point** (or endpoint), the moles of acid have completely reacted with the moles of base according to the stoichiometry of the balanced chemical equation. By knowing the volume and concentration of one reactant (e.g., NaOH) and the volume of the other (H₂SO₄), we can calculate the concentration of the unknown reactant.

    *   **Calculation of Concentration:**
        Let:
        *   $V_a$ = Volume of H₂SO₄ solution used (e.g., 20 mL or 0.020 L)
        *   $C_a$ = Concentration of H₂SO₄ solution (unknown)
        *   $V_b$ = Volume of NaOH solution used (average of concordant readings from burette)
        *   $C_b$ = Concentration of standard NaOH solution (known, e.g., 0.1 M)
        *   From the balanced equation: 1 mole of H₂SO₄ reacts with 2 moles of NaOH.
        *   At the equivalence point:
            $\frac{\text{moles of acid}}{\text{moles of base}} = \frac{1}{2}$
        *   Moles of acid = $C_a \times V_a$
        *   Moles of base = $C_b \times V_b$
        *   So, $\frac{C_a \times V_a}{C_b \times V_b} = \frac{1}{2}$
        *   Rearranging to find $C_a$:
            $C_a = \frac{1 \times C_b \times V_b}{2 \times V_a}$
            (Ensure volumes are in the same units, preferably Litres, for $C_a$ to be in Molarity).

3.  **Question (5 marks):** Imagine a future scenario where we need to synthesize a novel pharmaceutical compound that requires a specific pH environment for its production, precisely maintained at pH 4.5. You have access to a dilute solution of acetic acid (CH₃COOH), a dilute solution of sodium hydroxide (NaOH), and pure water. Describe how you would prepare a buffer solution with a pH of 4.5 using these components. Explain the concept of a buffer solution and why it is suitable for maintaining a stable pH.

    *   **Concept of a Buffer Solution:** A buffer solution is an aqueous solution that resists changes in pH upon the addition of small amounts of acid or base, or upon dilution. It consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base, or a weak base and its conjugate acid.
    *   **Suitability for Maintaining Stable pH:** Buffers work by neutralizing added acids or bases.
        *   If an acid (H⁺) is added, the conjugate base in the buffer reacts with it: Base⁻ + H⁺ → H-Base.
        *   If a base (OH⁻) is added, the weak acid in the buffer reacts with it: H-Base + OH⁻ → Base⁻ + H₂O.
        This prevents significant changes in the concentration of H⁺ or OH⁻ ions, thus maintaining a stable pH.

    *   **Preparation of a Buffer Solution at pH 4.5:**
        *   We need a buffer system whose pKa is close to the desired pH (4.5). Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) is a weak acid with a pKa of approximately 4.76. This is close enough to 4.5 for effective buffering.
        *   The buffer system will be **acetic acid (CH₃COOH) and its conjugate base, acetate ion (CH₃COO⁻)**, typically provided by sodium acetate (CH₃COONa).
        *   **Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation:** The pH of a buffer solution can be calculated using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:
            $pH = pKa + \log \frac{[\text{conjugate base}]}{[\text{weak acid}]}$
        *   **Target:** We want pH = 4.5, and pKa (acetic acid) ≈ 4.76.
            $4.5 = 4.76 + \log \frac{[\text{CH₃COO⁻}]}{[\text{CH₃COOH}]}$
            $\log \frac{[\text{CH₃COO⁻}]}{[\text{CH₃COOH}]} = 4.5 - 4.76 = -0.26$
            $\frac{[\text{CH₃COO⁻}]}{[\text{CH₃COOH}]} = 10^{-0.26} \approx 0.55$
            This means the concentration of the conjugate base (acetate) should be about 0.55 times the concentration of the weak acid (acetic acid).
        *   **Method of Preparation:**
            1.  **Choose Concentrations:** Select appropriate concentrations for acetic acid and sodium acetate. For example, start with a dilute solution of acetic acid.
            2.  **Preparation:**
                *   Take a measured volume of dilute acetic acid solution.
                *   To this, add a calculated amount of sodium acetate (which can be prepared by reacting the dilute acetic acid with a dilute NaOH solution, ensuring not to add excess NaOH, or by using solid sodium acetate if available).
                *   *Alternatively, and more practically with the given materials:* React a *controlled* amount of dilute NaOH with the dilute acetic acid. The reaction is:
                    CH₃COOH(aq) + NaOH(aq) → CH₃COONa(aq) + H₂O(l)
                    By adding NaOH to acetic acid, we convert some of the acetic acid into sodium acetate (the conjugate base). We need to achieve the ratio $[\text{CH₃COO⁻}]/[\text{CH₃COOH}] \approx 0.55$.
                *   **Procedure:**
                    a. Prepare a solution of acetic acid.
                    b. Slowly add dilute NaOH solution to the acetic acid solution while monitoring the pH using a pH meter.
                    c. Continue adding NaOH until the pH meter reads approximately 4.5. At this point, you have a mixture of acetic acid and sodium acetate in the required ratio.
                    d. Add distilled water to bring the final volume to the desired amount if needed.
            *   **Alternative (if solid NaAc is available):** Dissolve a certain amount of acetic acid and a calculated amount of solid sodium acetate in distilled water to achieve the desired concentration and the required ratio. The ratio calculation above determines how much of the acid needs to be converted to its salt.
