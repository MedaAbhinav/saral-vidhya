### 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

1.  **Explain why carbon forms covalent bonds with other atoms.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Carbon has an atomic number of 6 and an electronic configuration of 2, 4. It has 4 valence electrons. To achieve a stable noble gas configuration, carbon needs to gain or lose 4 electrons. Gaining 4 electrons would make it difficult for the nucleus to hold 10 electrons. Losing 4 electrons would require a large amount of energy. Therefore, carbon overcomes this by sharing its 4 valence electrons with other atoms, forming covalent bonds.

2.  **What is meant by the term 'tetravalent'? Give an example of a tetravalent element.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Tetravalent means an element having a valency of four. This means the atom of the element can form four covalent bonds. Carbon is a tetravalent element because it has four valence electrons and needs to share all of them to achieve a stable electronic configuration.

3.  **Differentiate between saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   **Saturated Hydrocarbons:** These are hydrocarbons in which all the carbon-carbon bonds are single bonds. They contain only single bonds between carbon atoms. Their general formula is CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Example: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆).
        *   **Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:** These are hydrocarbons that contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond or triple bond. They have double or triple bonds between carbon atoms. Their general formulas are CₙH₂ₙ (alkenes) and CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (alkynes). Example: Ethene (C₂H₄), Ethyne (C₂H₂).

4.  **What is an allotrope? Give two allotropes of carbon and their uses.**
    *   **Model Answer:** An allotrope is one of two or more different physical forms in which an element can exist. Two allotropes of carbon are:
        *   **Diamond:** Used in jewellery and cutting tools due to its extreme hardness.
        *   **Graphite:** Used as a lubricant, in pencil leads, and in electrodes due to its softness and electrical conductivity.

5.  **Explain the formation of a double covalent bond in oxygen molecule (O₂).**
    *   **Model Answer:** An oxygen atom has 6 valence electrons and needs 2 more to achieve a stable octet. In an oxygen molecule (O₂), two oxygen atoms share two electrons each with each other. This results in two shared pairs of electrons between the two oxygen atoms, forming a double covalent bond (O=O).

6.  **Why are most carbon compounds poor conductors of electricity?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Most carbon compounds are formed by covalent bonding. In covalent bonding, electrons are shared between atoms, and no free ions are formed. Ionic compounds, which have charged ions, can conduct electricity when dissolved in water or in a molten state. Since covalent compounds do not have mobile ions, they are poor conductors of electricity.

7.  **Explain the formation of a triple covalent bond in nitrogen molecule (N₂).**
    *   **Model Answer:** A nitrogen atom has 5 valence electrons and needs 3 more electrons to achieve a stable octet. In a nitrogen molecule (N₂), two nitrogen atoms share three electrons each with each other. This results in three shared pairs of electrons between the two nitrogen atoms, forming a triple covalent bond (N≡N).

8.  **What is the significance of carbon's tetravalency and its ability to form long chains?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Carbon's tetravalency allows it to bond with up to four other atoms, including other carbon atoms. Its ability to form long chains, branched chains, and rings, along with its tetravalency, makes it incredibly versatile. This property, known as catenation, allows carbon to form a vast number of diverse compounds, forming the basis of organic chemistry and life itself.

9.  **Draw the electron dot structure of methane (CH₄).**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        ```
             H
             |
        H -- C -- H
             |
             H
        ```
        *(Dot structure would show 'x' for carbon's valence electrons and '.' for hydrogen's valence electrons, with each H sharing one electron with C, and C sharing one electron with each H.)*

10. **What are functional groups? Give an example and explain its effect on the properties of the compound.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Functional groups are specific atoms or groups of atoms within a molecule that are responsible for the characteristic chemical reactions of that molecule. For example, the hydroxyl group (-OH) is a functional group. When present in an organic molecule, it imparts properties of alcohols, such as making the compound soluble in water and reactive towards certain reagents.

### 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

1.  **Describe the bonding in carbon. Explain why carbon forms covalent bonds and not ionic bonds.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Introduction to carbon's atomic structure (atomic number 6, electronic configuration 2, 4).
        *   Explanation of the need to achieve noble gas configuration.
        *   Difficulties of gaining 4 electrons (forming C⁴⁻) due to nuclear attraction.
        *   Difficulties of losing 4 electrons (forming C⁴⁺) due to high energy requirement.
        *   Introduction to covalent bonding as the solution (sharing of electrons).
        *   Explanation of how sharing leads to stable electronic configurations for all involved atoms.
        *   Examples of covalent bond formation (e.g., H₂, Cl₂, O₂, N₂).

2.  **Explain the concept of catenation. How does it contribute to the vast number of carbon compounds? Give examples of different types of carbon chains formed by catenation.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Definition of catenation: the ability of an element to form bonds with atoms of the same element to form long chains.
        *   Explanation of why carbon exhibits catenation to a great extent (strong C-C single bonds, tetravalency).
        *   Contribution to the diversity of carbon compounds: formation of straight chains, branched chains, and cyclic structures.
        *   Examples:
            *   Straight chain alkanes (e.g., propane, butane).
            *   Branched chain alkanes (e.g., isobutane).
            *   Cyclic alkanes (e.g., cyclopropane, cyclohexane).
        *   Mention that this property is unique to carbon among common elements.

3.  **Describe the different types of covalent bonds (single, double, and triple) with examples and their electron dot structures.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Definition of covalent bond: sharing of electron pairs between atoms.
        *   **Single Covalent Bond:** Formed by sharing of one pair of electrons. Example: H₂ molecule (H-H), CH₄ molecule (C-H bonds). Draw electron dot structure.
        *   **Double Covalent Bond:** Formed by sharing of two pairs of electrons. Example: O₂ molecule (O=O), C₂H₄ molecule (C=C bond). Draw electron dot structure.
        *   **Triple Covalent Bond:** Formed by sharing of three pairs of electrons. Example: N₂ molecule (N≡N). Draw electron dot structure.
        *   Emphasize that sharing allows atoms to achieve stable electronic configurations.

4.  **What are hydrocarbons? Classify them into alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes. Explain the general formula and characteristics of each class.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Definition of hydrocarbons: organic compounds composed solely of hydrogen and carbon atoms.
        *   **Alkanes:**
            *   General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
            *   Contain only single bonds between carbon atoms.
            *   Saturated hydrocarbons.
            *   Example: Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆).
        *   **Alkenes:**
            *   General formula: CₙH₂ₙ
            *   Contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond.
            *   Unsaturated hydrocarbons.
            *   Example: Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆).
        *   **Alkynes:**
            *   General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
            *   Contain at least one carbon-carbon triple bond.
            *   Unsaturated hydrocarbons.
            *   Example: Ethyne (C₂H₂), Propyne (C₃H₄).

5.  **Explain why carbon compounds generally have low melting and boiling points and are poor conductors of electricity, referring to the type of bonding involved.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Reiterate that most carbon compounds are covalently bonded.
        *   Explain that covalent bonds are strong within the molecule but the forces of attraction between *molecules* (intermolecular forces) are weak.
        *   Low melting and boiling points: Explain that little energy is required to overcome these weak intermolecular forces, leading to low melting and boiling points compared to ionic compounds.
        *   Poor conductors of electricity: Explain that in covalent compounds, electrons are shared and there are no free-moving ions or electrons in the solid or molten state, hence they cannot conduct electricity. Contrast this with ionic compounds which conduct electricity due to the presence of mobile ions.
