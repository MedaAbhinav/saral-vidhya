**Passage 1:**

Carbon's unique ability to form covalent bonds with itself and other elements is the foundation of organic chemistry. The formation of single, double, and triple bonds, along with the concept of isomerism, leads to an incredible diversity of molecular structures. Consider the simple hydrocarbon methane (CHâ‚„). Combustion is a process involving rapid reaction between a substance with an oxidant, usually oxygen, to produce heat and light. For hydrocarbons, complete combustion yields carbon dioxide and water. **Passage 4:**

Carbon compounds exhibit isomerism, a phenomenon where molecules with the same molecular formula have different structural arrangements, leading to different physical and chemical properties. Chain isomerism involves different arrangements of the carbon skeleton. Positional isomerism occurs when a functional group is attached at different positions on the carbon chain. Functional isomerism arises when compounds with the same molecular formula belong to different classes of organic compounds due to the presence of different functional groups. For example, Câ‚„Hâ‚پâ‚€O can represent butan-1-ol, butan-2-ol, and diethyl ether.

**Passage 5:**

The reactivity of carbon compounds is often dictated by the presence of functional groups and the type of carbon-carbon bonds. Saturated hydrocarbons (alkanes) are generally less reactive and undergo substitution reactions under specific conditions (like UV light). Unsaturated hydrocarbons (alkenes and alkynes) are more reactive due to the presence of pi bonds, which are more easily broken than sigma bonds. They readily undergo addition reactions. Alcohols, characterized by the -OH group, can undergo oxidation, dehydration, and esterification reactions. Carboxylic acids, with the -COOH group, are acidic and undergo reactions like neutralization and esterification.

1.  **Electronegativity, Bonding, and Properties:**
    *   **Carbon's Electronegativity (2.55):** This intermediate value means carbon doesn't strongly attract or donate electrons. It's not electronegative enough to easily form ionic compounds by gaining electrons (like halogens) or lose electrons to form cations (like alkali metals).
    *   **Covalent Bonding:** Carbon's tetravalency (4 valence electrons) and intermediate electronegativity make it ideal for forming stable covalent bonds by sharing electrons.
    *   **Polar vs. Non-polar Covalent Bonds:**
        *   **Non-polar Covalent:** When carbon bonds with an atom of very similar or identical electronegativity (e.g., C-C bond in alkanes, C-H bond in methane where electronegativity difference is small, 2.55 vs 2.20). The electron pair is shared almost equally.
        *   **Polar Covalent:** When carbon bonds with an atom of significantly different electronegativity (e.g., C-O in alcohols/carboxylic acids, C-Cl in chloroform). The more electronegative atom pulls the electron pair closer, creating a partial negative charge (خ´âپ») on that atom and a partial positive charge (خ´âپ؛) on carbon.
    *   **Relation to Physical Properties:**
        *   **Melting/Boiling Points:** Compounds with predominantly non-polar covalent bonds (like methane) have weak van der Waals forces between molecules, leading to very low melting and boiling points. Compounds with polar covalent bonds experience stronger dipole-dipole interactions, increasing melting and boiling points (e.g., acetic acid). Ionic compounds formed by metals with carboxylate groups (like sodium acetate) have strong electrostatic forces, resulting in very high melting points and a crystalline solid structure.
        *   **Solubility:** "Like dissolves like." Non-polar compounds (e.g., methane) are soluble in non-polar solvents. Polar compounds (e.g., acetic acid, alcohols) are soluble in polar solvents like water, often due to hydrogen bonding. Ionic compounds (like sodium acetate) are generally soluble in polar solvents due to ion-dipole interactions.
        *   **Electrical Conductivity:** Compounds with covalent bonds (polar or non-polar) are generally poor conductors of electricity in any state because they do not form free ions. Ionic compounds conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water because the ions become mobile.

2.  **Allotropes of Carbon and Their Applications:**
    *   **Diamond:**
        *   **Structure:** Each carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement. This creates a rigid, three-dimensional giant network structure.
        *   **Hybridization:** `spآ³` hybridization.
        *   **Bonding:** Strong, extensive sigma covalent bonds throughout the crystal.
        *   **Properties:** Extremely hard, high melting point, transparent, electrical insulator.
        *   **Applications:** Due to its hardness, it's used in cutting tools, drills, and grinding wheels. Its high thermal conductivity also makes it useful in heat sinks.
    *   **Graphite:**
        *   **Structure:** Carbon atoms are arranged in hexagonal planar layers. Within each layer, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to three other carbon atoms. The layers are held together by weak van der Waals forces.
        *   **Hybridization:** `spآ²` hybridization.
        *   **Bonding:** Strong covalent bonds within layers, weak van der Waals forces between layers. The `spآ²` hybridization leaves one free electron per carbon atom, forming a delocalized pi electron system across each layer.
        *   **Properties:** Soft, flaky, good electrical conductor (along layers), high melting point.
        *   **Applications:** Due to its softness and layered structure, it's used as a lubricant. The delocalized electrons make it a good electrical conductor, used in electrodes for batteries and electrolysis. Its high thermal stability is also utilized in high-temperature applications.

    **(B) Determining Saturation:**

    3.  **Test 3: Reaction with Bromine Water**
        *   **Step:** Add a few drops of bromine water (brownish-yellow) to a sample of the liquid in a test tube. Shake well.
        *   **Expected Observation:**
            *   If the liquid is unsaturated (contains C=C or Câ‰،C bonds), the bromine water will decolorize as bromine adds across the double/triple bond.

        *   **Reasoning:** Alcohols react with carboxylic acids in the presence of an acid catalyst to form esters. This is a characteristic reaction of alcohols.

    **Conclusion based on observations:**
    *   If it burns, produces COâ‚‚, decolorizes bromine water, and reacts with sodium, it's likely an unsaturated alcohol.
    *   If it burns, produces COâ‚‚, does not decolorize bromine water, but reacts with sodium and shows fruity smell with acetic acid, it's likely a saturated alcohol.
    *   If it burns, produces COâ‚‚, does not decolorize bromine water, and does not react with sodium but shows fruity smell, it might be an ether reacting with acetic acid under very forcing conditions, or there might be other functional groups. (This test is more definitive for alcohols.)
