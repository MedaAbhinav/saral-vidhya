### 2. Short Answer Questions

**(2-3 Marks Each)**

1.  **Why does carbon form compounds mainly through covalent bonding?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Carbon has an atomic number of 6 and its electronic configuration is 2, 4. It has 4 valence electrons. To achieve a stable noble gas configuration, carbon would need to either gain 4 electrons or lose 4 electrons. Gaining 4 electrons would make the nucleus unable to hold onto the increased number of electrons. Losing 4 electrons would require a very large amount of energy. Therefore, carbon overcomes this by sharing its 4 valence electrons with other atoms, forming covalent bonds.

2.  **What is a covalent bond? Explain with an example.**
    *   **Model Answer:** A covalent bond is a chemical bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between two atoms. This sharing allows each atom to achieve a stable electron configuration, usually an octet. **Example:** In a hydrogen molecule (H₂), each hydrogen atom has one valence electron. They share their electrons to form a single covalent bond, resulting in each hydrogen atom having a stable configuration like Helium (2 electrons).
        (H • + • H → H : H or H-H)

3.  **Explain the formation of a double covalent bond with an example.**
    *   **Model Answer:** A double covalent bond is formed when two atoms share two pairs of electrons. This happens when each atom needs to gain two electrons to achieve a stable octet. **Example:** In an oxygen molecule (O₂), each oxygen atom has 6 valence electrons and needs 2 more. By sharing two pairs of electrons (one pair from each atom), both oxygen atoms achieve a stable octet, forming a double bond.
        (O :: O or O=O)

4.  **What are the properties of carbon compounds that suggest they are mainly covalent?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Most carbon compounds are poor conductors of electricity in molten or aqueous states, indicating the absence of free ions. They also have relatively low melting and boiling points compared to ionic compounds, suggesting weak intermolecular forces between the molecules. These properties are characteristic of covalently bonded substances.

5.  **Draw the electron dot structure of a water molecule (H₂O).**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        Oxygen has 6 valence electrons, Hydrogen has 1 valence electron.
        Oxygen needs 2 electrons, Hydrogen needs 1 electron.
        *   The oxygen atom shares one electron with each of the two hydrogen atoms.
        *   Each hydrogen atom shares its one electron with the oxygen atom.
        *   This results in two single covalent bonds and two lone pairs of electrons on the oxygen atom.
        ```
             . .
            : O :
            ' . '
             / \
            H   H
        ```
        *(Note: A simpler diagram with only valence electrons around the atoms would also be acceptable, showing 2 electrons shared between O and each H, and 4 non-bonding electrons on O.)*

6.  **What is the difference between a saturated and an unsaturated hydrocarbon? Give an example of each.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   **Saturated Hydrocarbons:** These are hydrocarbons in which the carbon atoms are linked only by single covalent bonds. They are called saturated because they contain the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms for a given number of carbon atoms. **Example:** Ethane (C₂H₆).
        *   **Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:** These are hydrocarbons that contain at least one double or triple bond between carbon atoms. They have fewer hydrogen atoms than the corresponding saturated hydrocarbons. **Example:** Ethene (C₂H₄) with a double bond, or Ethyne (C₂H₂) with a triple bond.

7.  **Explain the phenomenon of catenation in carbon.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Catenation is the ability of an element to form bonds with itself to create long chains, branched chains, or rings. Carbon exhibits a remarkable degree of catenation due to the strength of its C-C single and double bonds and its tetravalency. This property allows carbon to form a vast number of diverse compounds.

8.  **Why is it difficult for carbon to gain or lose four electrons?**
    *   **Model Answer:** If carbon were to gain four electrons to form a C⁴⁻ ion, the nucleus with 6 protons would have to hold onto 10 electrons, which would be very difficult due to strong electron-electron repulsion. If carbon were to lose four electrons to form a C⁴⁺ ion, it would require a tremendous amount of energy to remove those electrons, leaving behind a small ion with a net positive charge that would be highly unstable.

9.  **Define allotropes. Name two common allotropes of carbon and briefly describe one.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state. **Two common allotropes of carbon are Diamond and Graphite.**
        **Diamond:** In diamond, each carbon atom is bonded to four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement. This forms a very strong, rigid, three-dimensional network structure, making diamond extremely hard and giving it a very high melting point.

10. **What is the formula of a hydrocarbon with 3 carbon atoms and only single bonds between them? Draw its structure.**
    *   **Model Answer:** A hydrocarbon with 3 carbon atoms and only single bonds is called propane. Its formula is C₃H₈.
        Structure:
        ```
              H   H   H
              |   |   |
          H - C - C - C - H
              |   |   |
              H   H   H
        ```

### 3. Long Answer Questions

**(5 Marks Each)**

1.  **Describe the electronic configuration of carbon and explain how it leads to the formation of covalent bonds. Discuss the formation of methane (CH₄) and ammonia (NH₃) molecules, illustrating the bonding with electron dot structures.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Introduction:** Atomic number of Carbon (6), Electronic configuration (2,4). Valence electrons (4).
        *   **The Challenge:** Difficulty in forming C⁴⁺ or C⁴⁻ ions due to high energy requirements and instability.
        *   **Solution: Covalent Bonding:** Explain sharing of electrons to achieve noble gas configuration.
        *   **Formation of Methane (CH₄):**
            *   Carbon needs 4 electrons, each Hydrogen needs 1.
            *   Carbon shares one electron with each of the four hydrogen atoms.
            *   Each hydrogen atom shares its electron with carbon.
            *   Result: 4 single covalent bonds, carbon achieves octet, hydrogens achieve duplet.
            *   **Electron Dot Structure for CH₄.**
        *   **Formation of Ammonia (NH₃):**
            *   Nitrogen has atomic number 7, configuration (2,5). Needs 3 electrons.
            *   Hydrogen needs 1 electron.
            *   Nitrogen shares one electron with each of the three hydrogen atoms.
            *   Each hydrogen atom shares its electron with nitrogen.
            *   Result: 3 single covalent bonds, nitrogen achieves octet (with one lone pair), hydrogens achieve duplet.
            *   **Electron Dot Structure for NH₃.**
        *   **Conclusion:** Covalent bonding is the primary mode of bonding for carbon, enabling the formation of a vast array of organic compounds.

2.  **What are hydrocarbons? Classify them into saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons. Discuss the differences between single, double, and triple covalent bonds. Give examples of molecules that exhibit each type of bond.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Definition of Hydrocarbons:** Compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen.
        *   **Classification:**
            *   **Saturated Hydrocarbons:** Alkanes, single C-C bonds, general formula CnH2n+2.
            *   **Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:** Alkenes (double C=C bonds, CnH2n) and Alkynes (triple C≡C bonds, CnH2n-2).
        *   **Types of Covalent Bonds:**
            *   **Single Bond:** Sharing of one pair of electrons (e.g., H-H in H₂, C-C in Ethane).
            *   **Double Bond:** Sharing of two pairs of electrons (e.g., O=O in O₂, C=C in Ethene).
            *   **Triple Bond:** Sharing of three pairs of electrons (e.g., N≡N in N₂, C≡C in Ethyne).
        *   **Examples:** Provide specific molecules and their structures/formulas for each bond type.
        *   **Conclusion:** The type of covalent bond significantly influences the properties of hydrocarbons.

3.  **Discuss the properties of carbon that make it unique and essential for life. Explain the concept of catenation and its significance, and briefly mention the allotropes of carbon.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Uniqueness of Carbon:**
            *   **Tetravalency:** Ability to form 4 covalent bonds.
            *   **Catenation:** Ability to bond with itself.
        *   **Catenation:**
            *   Definition and explanation of strong C-C bonds.
            *   Formation of chains, branched chains, and rings.
            *   Significance: Enables formation of millions of diverse compounds, forming the basis of life.
        *   **Allotropes of Carbon:**
            *   Definition of allotropy.
            *   Mention Diamond (hard, covalent network).
            *   Mention Graphite (layered, soft, conducts electricity).
            *   Mention Fullerenes (e.g., Buckminsterfullerene).
        *   **Significance in Life:** Carbon's ability to form stable covalent bonds with itself and other elements (H, O, N, S, P) allows for the formation of complex organic molecules like carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and nucleic acids, which are fundamental to living organisms.

4.  **Explain the formation of a molecule of ethanoic acid (acetic acid), CH₃COOH, using electron dot structure. Show how all atoms achieve a stable electron configuration.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Identify Atoms and Valence Electrons:** Carbon (4), Hydrogen (1), Oxygen (6).
        *   **Structure of Ethanoic Acid:** CH₃ group attached to a carboxyl group (-COOH).
        *   **Bonding in CH₃ group:** Carbon shares one electron with each of the three hydrogen atoms, forming three single covalent bonds.
        *   **Bonding in -COOH group:**
            *   The carbon of the carboxyl group is bonded to the carbon of the methyl group.
            *   This carbon is also bonded to one oxygen with a double bond (sharing two pairs of electrons).
            *   It is bonded to another oxygen with a single bond.
            *   The oxygen bonded with a single bond is also bonded to a hydrogen atom.
        *   **Stable Configurations:** Show how carbon atoms achieve octets and hydrogen atoms achieve duplets.
        *   **Electron Dot Structure for CH₃COOH:** (Requires a detailed drawing showing all valence electrons and shared pairs).

5.  **Compare and contrast ionic and covalent compounds, focusing on their properties. Relate these properties to the nature of bonding.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Ionic Compounds:**
            *   **Bonding:** Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions (formed by transfer of electrons).
            *   **Properties:**
                *   High melting and boiling points (strong electrostatic forces).
                *   Generally solids at room temperature.
                *   Soluble in polar solvents (like water).
                *   Conduct electricity in molten or aqueous state (due to mobile ions).
                *   Often brittle.
        *   **Covalent Compounds:**
            *   **Bonding:** Sharing of electrons between atoms.
            *   **Properties:**
                *   Low melting and boiling points (weak intermolecular forces).
                *   Can be solids, liquids, or gases at room temperature.
                *   Generally insoluble in polar solvents, soluble in non-polar solvents.
                *   Do not conduct electricity in any state (no free ions or electrons).
                *   Often soft.
        *   **Table or Paragraph Comparison:** Summarize the differences clearly.
        *   **Relationship to Bonding:** Explain how the nature of electron transfer (ionic) versus sharing (covalent) dictates the strength of forces within the compound and consequently its physical properties.
