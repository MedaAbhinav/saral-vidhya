## 4.1 Bonding in Carbon – The Covalent Bond

### Properties of Ionic Compounds (Recap from Chapter 3)

*   High melting and boiling points.
*   Conduct electricity in solution or molten state.
*   These properties are explained by the nature of ionic bonding (electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions).

### Properties of Carbon Compounds

*   **Poor Conductors of Electricity:** Most carbon compounds do not conduct electricity.
*   **Low Melting and Boiling Points:**
    *   Refer to **Table 4.1: Melting points and boiling points of some compounds of carbon.**
        *   Acetic acid (CH₃COOH): MP 290 K, BP 391 K
        *   Chloroform (CHCl₃): MP 209 K, BP 334 K
        *   Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH): MP 156 K, BP 351 K
        *   Methane (CH₄): MP 90 K, BP 111 K
    *   **Conclusion:** These compounds have significantly lower melting and boiling points compared to ionic compounds. This indicates that the forces of attraction between their molecules are weak.
    *   **Further Conclusion:** Since they are poor conductors, their bonding does not produce ions.

### Electronic Configuration of Carbon and its Implications

*   **Atomic Number of Carbon:** 6
*   **Electronic Configuration:**
    *   K-shell: 2 electrons
    *   L-shell (outermost shell): 4 electrons
*   **Valence Electrons:** 4
*   **Goal of Elements:** To achieve a stable noble gas configuration (a full outermost shell).
*   **Carbon's Challenge:** To achieve a full outer shell, carbon needs to gain or lose 4 electrons.
    *   **Gaining 4 electrons (to form C⁴⁻):** This would require the nucleus (with 6 protons) to hold 10 electrons (6 original + 4 gained), which is energetically difficult due to strong repulsion.
    *   **Losing 4 electrons (to form C⁴⁺):** This would require a very large amount of energy to remove 4 electrons, leaving a cation with 6 protons holding only 2 electrons.
*   **Carbon's Solution: Sharing Electrons (Covalent Bonding)**
    *   Carbon overcomes this problem by *sharing* its valence electrons with other atoms (of carbon or other elements).
    *   The shared electron pair contributes to the outermost shell of *both* atoms involved, allowing each to achieve a stable noble gas configuration.
    *   This sharing of electrons forms **covalent bonds**.

### Examples of Covalent Bonding

1.  **Hydrogen (H₂):**
    *   Atomic number: 1. Electronic configuration: K-shell has 1 electron.
    *   Needs 1 more electron to fill its K-shell (like Helium, 2 electrons).
    *   Two hydrogen atoms share their single electrons.
    *   **Result:** Each H atom now effectively has 2 electrons in its K-shell.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure:** H : H (or H • H)
    *   **Single Covalent Bond:** The shared pair of electrons forms a single covalent bond, represented by a line (-) between the atoms.
    *   **Representation:** H-H

2.  **Chlorine (Cl₂):**
    *   Atomic number: 17. Electronic configuration: K=2, L=8, M=7.
    *   Valence electrons: 7. Needs 1 more electron to complete its octet.
    *   Two chlorine atoms share one electron each.
    *   **Result:** Each Cl atom effectively has 8 valence electrons.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure:**
        ```
            ..       ..
        : Cl : : Cl :
            ..       ..
        ```
        (Each dot represents a valence electron)
    *   **Representation:** Cl-Cl (a single covalent bond)

3.  **Oxygen (O₂):**
    *   Atomic number: 8. Electronic configuration: K=2, L=6.
    *   Valence electrons: 6. Needs 2 more electrons to complete its octet.
    *   Each oxygen atom shares *two* electrons with the other oxygen atom.
    *   **Result:** Two shared pairs of electrons are formed, meaning each oxygen atom effectively has 8 valence electrons.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure:**
        ```
           ..     ..
        : O :: O :
           ..     ..
        ```
    *   **Double Covalent Bond:** Two shared pairs constitute a double bond, represented by a double line (=).
    *   **Representation:** O=O

4.  **Water (H₂O):**
    *   Oxygen (valence 6) needs 2 electrons. Hydrogen (valence 1) needs 1 electron.
    *   One oxygen atom forms single covalent bonds with two hydrogen atoms.
    *   Oxygen shares one electron with each hydrogen, and each hydrogen shares its electron with oxygen.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure:**
        ```
            ..
        H : O : H
            ..
        ```
    *   **Bonding:** Single bonds (H-O-H). The oxygen atom achieves 8 valence electrons, and each hydrogen atom achieves 2.

5.  **Nitrogen (N₂):**
    *   Atomic number: 7. Electronic configuration: K=2, L=5.
    *   Valence electrons: 5. Needs 3 more electrons to complete its octet.
    *   Each nitrogen atom shares *three* electrons with the other nitrogen atom.
    *   **Result:** Three shared pairs of electrons are formed.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure:**
        ```
        : N ::: N :
        ```
    *   **Triple Covalent Bond:** Three shared pairs constitute a triple bond, represented by a triple line (≡).
    *   **Representation:** N≡N

6.  **Ammonia (NH₃):**
    *   Nitrogen (valence 5) needs 3 electrons. Hydrogen (valence 1) needs 1 electron.
    *   One nitrogen atom forms single covalent bonds with three hydrogen atoms.
    *   Nitrogen shares one electron with each hydrogen, and each hydrogen shares its electron with nitrogen.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure:**
        ```
           ..
        H : N : H
            |
            H
        ```
    *   **Bonding:** Single bonds (N-H). Nitrogen achieves 8 valence electrons, and each hydrogen achieves 2.

7.  **Methane (CH₄):**
    *   Carbon (valence 4) needs 4 electrons. Hydrogen (valence 1) needs 1 electron.
    *   Carbon shares one electron with each of the four hydrogen atoms. Each hydrogen atom shares its electron with carbon.
    *   **Result:** Carbon achieves 8 valence electrons, and each hydrogen achieves 2.
    *   **Electron Dot Structure (Fig. 4.5):**
        ```
              H
              |
        H --- C --- H
              |
              H
        ```
    *   **Bonding:** Single covalent bonds (C-H).
    *   **Significance:** Methane is a simple and widely used carbon compound (fuel, bio-gas, CNG).

### Characteristics of Covalently Bonded Compounds

*   **Strong Bonds Within Molecules:** The covalent bonds holding atoms together within a molecule are strong.
*   **Weak Intermolecular Forces:** The forces of attraction *between* different molecules are weak.
*   **Consequences:**
    *   **Low Melting and Boiling Points:** Little energy is required to overcome the weak intermolecular forces.
    *   **Poor Conductors of Electricity:** Electrons are localized in shared bonds and are not free to move, so they cannot conduct electricity.

## Allotropes of Carbon

*   **Definition:** Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state.
*   **Carbon's Allotropes:** Carbon exists in various forms with distinct physical properties due to different arrangements of carbon atoms.
*   **Key Allotropes:**

    1.  **Diamond:**
        *   **Structure:** Each carbon atom is bonded tetrahedrally to *four* other carbon atoms.
        *   **Result:** Forms a rigid, three-dimensional network structure.
        *   **Properties:**
            *   Extremely hard (hardest known natural substance).
            *   High melting point.
            *   Does not conduct electricity (electrons are localized in strong covalent bonds).
            *   Transparent.

    2.  **Graphite:**
        *   **Structure:** Each carbon atom is bonded to *three* other carbon atoms in the same plane, forming hexagonal rings. This creates flat layers. One bond in each hexagon is a double bond, satisfying carbon's valency.
        *   **Result:** Layers of hexagonal arrays stacked one above the other.
        *   **Properties:**
            *   Soft and slippery (layers can slide over each other).
            *   Good conductor of electricity (unlike most non-metals). The delocalized electrons in the double bonds allow for conductivity.
            *   Opaque and black.
            *   Used as a lubricant and in pencil leads.

    3.  **Fullerenes (e.g., C-60):**
        *   **Discovery:** A third class of carbon allotropes.
        *   **Structure:** Molecules where carbon atoms are arranged in a spherical, ellipsoidal, or hollow shape.
        *   **C-60 (Buckminsterfullerene):** Named after architect Buckminster Fuller due to its resemblance to geodesic domes. It has 60 carbon atoms arranged in a structure like a soccer ball (pentagons and hexagons).
        *   **Properties:** Varies, but generally softer than diamond and graphite.

*   **Key Distinction:** Despite being made only of carbon atoms, the different bonding arrangements lead to drastically different physical properties (hardness, conductivity, appearance) while their chemical properties remain largely the same (reactivity).

## Questions

**1. What would be the electron dot structure of carbon dioxide which has the formula CO₂?**

*   **Analysis:**
    *   Carbon (C): Atomic number 6, electronic configuration 2, 4. Valence electrons = 4. Needs 4 electrons.
    *   Oxygen (O): Atomic number 8, electronic configuration 2, 6. Valence electrons = 6. Needs 2 electrons.
    *   Formula: CO₂. One carbon atom, two oxygen atoms.
*   **Bonding Strategy:** Carbon needs 4 electrons, and each oxygen needs 2. To satisfy everyone, carbon will form double bonds with each oxygen atom. Carbon shares 2 electrons with each oxygen, and each oxygen shares 2 electrons with carbon.
*   **Electron Dot Structure:**
    ```
       ..     ..
    : O :: C :: O :
       ..     ..
    ```
*   **Explanation:**
    *   Carbon contributes 4 valence electrons (two to each oxygen).
    *   Each oxygen atom contributes 6 valence electrons (two to carbon, and four as lone pairs).
    *   The two double bonds (::) represent the shared electron pairs.
    *   Carbon achieves 8 valence electrons (4 shared pairs).
    *   Each oxygen atom achieves 8 valence electrons (2 shared pairs + 2 lone pairs).
