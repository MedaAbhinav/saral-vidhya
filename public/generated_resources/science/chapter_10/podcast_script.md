**Riya:** Absolutely! Mr. Sharma, the chapter excerpt mentions that the human eye is like a camera. That's a really interesting analogy. Can you elaborate on that for us?

**Mr. Sharma:** Of course, Riya. Think about how a camera works. It has a lens that focuses light onto a light-sensitive film or sensor. The human eye functions very similarly. Light enters our eye through the **cornea**, which is a transparent bulge at the front. This is like the outer glass of a camera lens.

**Riya:** So, the cornea is the first point of entry for light?

**Mr. Sharma:** Exactly. And then, the light passes through a small opening called the **pupil**. The size of this pupil is controlled by the **iris**, which is that coloured part of your eye. The iris acts like the diaphragm of a camera, adjusting the amount of light entering the eye. If it's bright, the iris constricts the pupil to let in less light, and in dim light, it dilates the pupil to let in more.

**Riya:** That makes sense! So, iris controls the light, and pupil is the opening. What about the lens itself?

**Mr. Sharma:** That's where the **eye lens** comes in. It's a flexible, jelly-like lens that works with the cornea to focus light. Unlike a camera lens that has a fixed focal length, our eye lens can actually change its curvature and thus its focal length. This incredible ability is called **accommodation**.

**Riya:** Accommodation! That sounds like our eye is adapting. How does it do that?

**Mr. Sharma:** It's all thanks to the **ciliary muscles**. When we look at distant objects, these muscles relax, making the eye lens thinner, and increasing its focal length. This allows us to focus on far-off things. But when we look at nearby objects, the ciliary muscles contract, making the lens thicker and decreasing its focal length. This helps us see things up close clearly.

**Riya:** Wow, so our eye is constantly adjusting! And where does the image finally form?

**Mr. Sharma:** The focused light rays converge on a light-sensitive screen at the back of the eye called the **retina**. The retina is packed with light-sensitive cells, like rods and cones. When light falls on them, they generate electrical signals. These signals are then sent to our brain through the **optic nerve**, and our brain interprets these signals as images, allowing us to see.

**Riya:** That's a truly remarkable process! So, the eye lens forms an inverted, real image on the retina.

**Mr. Sharma:** Precisely! And it's the brain's interpretation that flips it back for us to see the object upright.

**Riya:** Mr. Sharma, the chapter also touches upon some common defects of vision. Can you briefly explain what happens when these abilities of the eye diminish?

**Mr. Sharma:** Certainly, Riya. Sometimes, the eye's ability to accommodate, that is, to adjust its focal length, can be affected. This leads to blurred vision. The most common defects are **myopia** (near-sightedness), where a person can see nearby objects clearly but distant objects appear blurred, and **hypermetropia** (far-sightedness), where distant objects are clear, but nearby objects are blurred. Another age-related condition is **presbyopia**, where the eye's focusing ability diminishes, making it difficult to read or see close objects. And in older age, the lens can become cloudy, a condition known as **cataract**, leading to vision loss.

**Riya:** So, these defects can be corrected with the help of lenses in spectacles.

**Mr. Sharma:** Exactly! Different types of lenses are used to compensate for these refractive defects and restore clear vision. We'll delve deeper into those corrections in a future episode.
