### 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

1.  **What is the power of accommodation of the eye? Explain how it helps in focusing on objects at different distances.**
    *   **Model Answer:** The power of accommodation of the eye is its ability to adjust the focal length of the eye lens so as to focus images of objects at varying distances distinctly on the retina. This is achieved by the ciliary muscles which can change the curvature of the eye lens. When focusing on distant objects, ciliary muscles relax, making the lens thinner and increasing its focal length. When focusing on nearby objects, ciliary muscles contract, making the lens thicker and decreasing its focal length.

2.  **Differentiate between myopia and hypermetropia.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   **Myopia (Near-sightedness):** In myopia, a person can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects distinctly. The image of distant objects is formed in front of the retina. This is due to excessive curvature of the eye lens or elongation of the eyeball.
        *   **Hypermetropia (Far-sightedness):** In hypermetropia, a person can see distant objects clearly but cannot see nearby objects distinctly. The image of nearby objects is formed behind the retina. This is due to the focal length of the eye lens being too long or the eyeball being too small.

3.  **Why is it impossible to see colours in the dark?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The human eye has light-sensitive cells called rods and cones on the retina. Cones are responsible for colour vision and are sensitive to bright light. Rods are sensitive to dim light and help in seeing in the dark, but they do not distinguish between colours. In the absence of light, cones are not stimulated, hence we cannot see colours.

4.  **Explain the role of the iris and pupil in the human eye.**
    *   **Model Answer:** The iris is a muscular diaphragm that controls the size of the pupil. The pupil is the opening in the center of the iris. The iris regulates the amount of light entering the eye. In bright light, the iris constricts the pupil to reduce the amount of light entering, and in dim light, it dilates the pupil to allow more light to enter.

5.  **What is presbyopia? What are its causes and how can it be corrected?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Presbyopia is a defect of vision commonly occurring in old age, where the power of accommodation of the eye decreases. This makes it difficult to see nearby objects clearly. It is caused by the gradual weakening of the ciliary muscles and diminishing flexibility of the eye lens. It can be corrected using bifocal lenses, which have a convex lens in the lower part to facilitate near vision.

6.  **How does the human eye form an image of an object?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Light rays from an object enter the eye through the cornea and pupil. The eye lens, with the help of ciliary muscles, adjusts its focal length to focus these rays. The lens forms a real and inverted image of the object on the retina. The light-sensitive cells (rods and cones) in the retina get activated and generate electrical signals. These signals are transmitted to the brain via the optic nerve, which interprets them to form a perception of the object.

7.  **What is the function of the retina in the human eye?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The retina is the light-sensitive screen at the back of the eye. It contains a large number of light-sensitive cells called photoreceptors: rods and cones. When light falls on the retina, these cells get activated and convert light energy into electrical signals. These signals are then sent to the brain through the optic nerve for interpretation, allowing us to see.

8.  **Explain the formation of a rainbow.**
    *   **Model Answer:** A rainbow is formed due to the dispersion of sunlight by raindrops. When sunlight enters a raindrop, it undergoes refraction. Since different colours of light have different wavelengths, they bend at slightly different angles (dispersion). This separates white light into its constituent colours. The dispersed light then reflects internally from the back of the raindrop and undergoes refraction again as it emerges from the drop. This process results in the separation of colours and the formation of the visible spectrum of light, which we see as a rainbow.

9.  **Why does the sky appear blue?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The sky appears blue due to the scattering of sunlight by the molecules of gases and tiny particles in the Earth's atmosphere. This phenomenon is called Rayleigh scattering. Blue light has a shorter wavelength than red light, so it is scattered more effectively in all directions by the atmospheric particles. When we look at the sky away from the sun, we see this scattered blue light.

10. **What are the typical near point and far point of a normal human eye?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The least distance of distinct vision for a normal human eye, where objects can be seen most distinctly without strain, is called the near point. For a young adult with normal vision, this is approximately 25 cm. The farthest point up to which the eye can see objects clearly is called the far point. For a normal eye, the far point is at infinity.

### 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

1.  **Describe the structure of the human eye. Explain the function of the different parts in enabling us to see.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Introduction:** Briefly introduce the human eye as a vital sense organ.
        *   **Structure:** Describe the main parts:
            *   **Cornea:** Transparent outer layer, major refraction of light.
            *   **Iris:** Muscular diaphragm, controls pupil size.
            *   **Pupil:** Opening in the iris, regulates light entry.
            *   **Eye Lens (Crystalline Lens):** Fibrous, jelly-like, fine adjustment of focal length.
            *   **Ciliary Muscles:** Control the shape (curvature) of the eye lens for accommodation.
            *   **Retina:** Light-sensitive screen at the back, contains rods and cones, image formation.
            *   **Optic Nerve:** Transmits electrical signals to the brain.
        *   **Function:** Explain how light enters, is refracted, focused by the lens, and how the image is formed on the retina, leading to perception by the brain. Mention accommodation briefly.

2.  **Explain the defect of myopia (near-sightedness). State its causes and describe how it can be corrected using a suitable lens. Include a diagram.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Definition:** Myopia is the defect where near objects are seen clearly, but distant objects are blurred.
        *   **Causes:**
            *   Excessive curvature of the eye lens.
            *   Elongation of the eyeball.
        *   **Image Formation:** Image of distant objects is formed in front of the retina.
        *   **Correction:**
            *   Use of a concave lens of appropriate power.
            *   Explain how the concave lens diverges the incoming light rays, allowing the eye lens to focus the image on the retina.
        *   **Diagram:** Draw a diagram showing a normal eye, a myopic eye with rays focusing in front of the retina, and a myopic eye corrected with a concave lens.

3.  **Explain the defect of hypermetropia (far-sightedness). State its causes and describe how it can be corrected using a suitable lens. Include a diagram.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Definition:** Hypermetropia is the defect where distant objects are seen clearly, but near objects are blurred.
        *   **Causes:**
            *   Focal length of the eye lens is too long.
            *   Eyeball has become too small.
        *   **Image Formation:** Image of nearby objects is formed behind the retina.
        *   **Correction:**
            *   Use of a convex lens of appropriate power.
            *   Explain how the convex lens converges the incoming light rays, providing additional converging power to focus the image on the retina.
        *   **Diagram:** Draw a diagram showing a normal eye, a hypermetropic eye with rays focusing behind the retina, and a hypermetropic eye corrected with a convex lens.

4.  **What is meant by the dispersion of light? Explain the formation of a spectrum of colours when white light passes through a prism. How is this principle applied in the formation of a rainbow?**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Dispersion:** Define dispersion as the splitting of white light into its constituent colours when it passes through a medium like a prism.
        *   **Prism Experiment:** Describe how white light enters a prism, undergoes refraction, and splits into colours (VIBGYOR) due to different angles of deviation for each colour (based on wavelength). Mention that violet light deviates the most and red light the least.
        *   **Rainbow Formation:** Explain how raindrops act like tiny prisms. Sunlight undergoes refraction, dispersion, and internal reflection within the raindrops to form the colours of the rainbow. Emphasize that the order of colours is consistent.

5.  **Discuss the phenomenon of scattering of light in the atmosphere. Explain why the sun appears reddish during sunrise and sunset, and why the sky appears blue during the day.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Scattering of Light:** Define scattering of light by particles in the atmosphere. Introduce Rayleigh scattering and its dependence on wavelength.
        *   **Blue Sky:** Explain why blue light (shorter wavelength) is scattered more effectively than red light, making the sky appear blue during the day.
        *   **Reddish Sunrise/Sunset:** Explain that during sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels through a longer path in the atmosphere. Most of the blue light gets scattered away, leaving the longer wavelengths (red and orange) to reach our eyes directly, making the sun and surrounding sky appear reddish.
