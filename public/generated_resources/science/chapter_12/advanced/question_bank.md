### 2. Case Study / Passage-Based Questions

**2.1. Passage: Electromagnets**

Electromagnets are temporary magnets formed when an electric current flows through a coil of wire wrapped around a ferromagnetic core. The strength of an electromagnet depends on the number of turns in the coil, the magnitude of the current, and the nature of the core material. When the current is switched off, the magnetism largely disappears. This property makes them useful in various applications where controlled magnetism is required.

**(a)** A student wants to build a strong electromagnet. Which of the following combinations would result in the strongest electromagnet, assuming all other factors are kept as similar as possible?
    (i) A coil with 100 turns and a current of 1 A.
    (ii) A coil with 200 turns and a current of 0.5 A.
    (iii) A coil with 100 turns and a current of 2 A.
    (iv) A coil with 200 turns and a current of 1 A.

**(b)** Explain why an iron core significantly increases the strength of an electromagnet compared to an air core.

**(c)** Briefly describe two applications where the temporary nature of electromagnets is crucial.

**2.2. Passage: Magnetic Field Lines**

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines that represent the direction and relative strength of a magnetic field. By convention, they emerge from the north pole of a magnet and enter the south pole. They form closed loops, meaning they continue inside the magnet from south to north. The density of field lines indicates the strength of the magnetic field; where the lines are closer, the field is stronger. Crucially, magnetic field lines never intersect.

**(a)** If two magnetic field lines were observed to intersect at a point, what would it imply about the compass needle placed at that point?

**(b)** Imagine you have a bar magnet and you are mapping its magnetic field lines. If you find that the field lines are very dense near the ends of the magnet, what can you conclude about the magnetic field strength at those regions?

**(c)** While drawing the magnetic field lines around a current-carrying solenoid, why is it observed that the field lines inside are nearly straight and parallel, and spread out significantly outside?

**2.3. Passage: Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor**

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force. This force is perpendicular to both the direction of the current and the direction of the magnetic field. The magnitude of this force depends on the strength of the magnetic field, the magnitude of the current, the length of the conductor within the field, and the angle between the conductor and the magnetic field. This phenomenon is the basis of electric motors.

**(a)** A straight wire of length $L$ carries current $I$ and is placed in a uniform magnetic field $B$. The wire experiences a force $F$. If the wire is rotated by 90 degrees so it becomes perpendicular to the field, what will be the new force? If it is rotated by 180 degrees, what will be the force?

**(b)** Explain why the force on a current-carrying conductor is zero if it is placed parallel to the magnetic field.

**(c)** How does Fleming's Left-Hand Rule help in determining the direction of the force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field?

**2.4. Passage: Magnetic Field Due to a Straight Wire**

Hans Christian Oersted discovered that an electric current produces a magnetic field. The magnetic field lines around a straight current-carrying wire are concentric circles. The direction of these field lines can be determined using the right-hand rule: if you grasp the wire with your right hand such that your thumb points in the direction of the current, your fingers will curl in the direction of the magnetic field lines. The strength of the magnetic field is inversely proportional to the distance from the wire.

**(a)** If the current in a straight wire is doubled, how does the magnetic field strength at a point 2 cm away change?

**(b)** Consider two points, A and B, at distances $r_A$ and $r_B$ from a straight current-carrying wire, respectively. If $r_A = 2r_B$, how does the magnetic field strength at A compare to that at B?

**(c)** What would happen to the pattern of magnetic field lines if the current in the wire were reversed?

**2.5. Passage: Solenoids and Electromagnetism**

A solenoid is a coil of wire wound in a helical shape. When an electric current flows through the solenoid, it produces a magnetic field that is uniform inside the solenoid (away from the ends) and resembles that of a bar magnet outside. The strength of this magnetic field depends on the current, the number of turns per unit length, and the presence of a core material. Solenoids are fundamental components in many electrical devices.

**(a)** If a soft iron core is inserted into a current-carrying solenoid, explain the primary reason for the significant increase in its magnetic field strength.

**(b)** Imagine a solenoid with $N$ turns and length $L$, carrying current $I$. If the number of turns per unit length is increased by a factor of 2, while keeping the current and length constant, how does the magnetic field inside the solenoid change?

**(c)** Describe how the magnetic field produced by a solenoid can be used to attract or repel iron objects.

        **4.2. Real-World Application: Electric Motor Operation**
        **Concept:** A basic DC motor operates on the principle that a current-carrying conductor experiences a force when placed in a magnetic field. This force produces a torque, causing rotation.

        **Conceptual Diagram:**
        Imagine a rectangular coil of wire placed between the poles of a U-shaped magnet (providing a radial magnetic field). The ends of the coil are connected to a split-ring commutator, which is in contact with stationary brushes. Current flows from the power source through the brushes and commutator to the coil.

        **Operation:**
        1.  **Current Flow:** Current enters the coil through one brush, flows through the commutator, into one side of the coil, through the coil, out the other side, through the commutator segment, and out through the other brush to the power source.
        2.  **Force Production:** Let's say current flows upwards in one side of the coil (say, side AB) and downwards in the opposite side (say, side CD). These sides are placed in the magnetic field.
            *   In side AB, current is upwards, and the magnetic field is, say, from North to South pole. Using Fleming's Left-Hand Rule, a force acts on AB in one direction (e.g., outwards/upwards relative to the plane).
            *   In side CD, current is downwards, and the magnetic field is the same. Using Fleming's Left-Hand Rule, an equal and opposite force acts on CD (e.g., inwards/downwards relative to the plane).
        3.  **Torque and Rotation:** These two forces acting on opposite sides of the coil produce a torque, causing the coil to rotate.
        4.  **Commutator Action:** As the coil rotates, the commutator segments also rotate. When the coil reaches the vertical position, the commutator segments switch contact with the brushes. This reverses the direction of current flow in the coil.
        5.  **Continuous Rotation:** Due to the reversed current, the forces on sides AB and CD also reverse their directions *relative to the coil's new orientation*. This ensures that the torque continues to act in the same rotational direction, leading to continuous rotation.

        **Factor Limiting Efficiency:**
        One significant factor limiting the efficiency of a real-world DC motor is **armature resistance**. The coil windings and connecting wires have electrical resistance. This resistance causes a power loss in the form of heat ($P_{loss} = I^2R$) whenever current flows through the coil. This reduces the amount of electrical energy converted into mechanical energy. Other factors include friction at bearings and commutator, and eddy currents.

        **4.3. Conceptual Conundrum: Intersecting Magnetic Field Lines**
        If magnetic field lines could intersect, it would lead to several physical paradoxes and contradictions:

        1.  **Ambiguous Direction of Magnetic Field:** The direction of the magnetic field at any point is defined by the direction of the tangent to the magnetic field line at that point. If two field lines intersected at a point, there would be two different tangents at that point, implying two different directions for the magnetic field. A compass needle (which aligns with the local magnetic field) would be forced to point in two directions simultaneously, which is physically impossible. This violates the fundamental understanding that a magnetic field at any point has a unique direction.

        2.  **Violation of Monopole Absence (Magnetic Sources):** Magnetic field lines originate from north poles and terminate at south poles, forming closed loops. The fact that they form closed loops implies that there are no magnetic monopoles (isolated north or south poles). If field lines could intersect, it would suggest that lines are "created" or "destroyed" at intersection points, potentially implying the existence of sources or sinks of magnetic field, which are magnetic monopoles. This contradicts the observed behavior of magnets.

        3.  **Inconsistent Force Laws:** The force on a moving charge in a magnetic field is given by $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$. If the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ at a point has multiple directions due to intersecting field lines, the direction of the force on a charged particle moving through that point would become ambiguous. This would lead to unpredictable and non-unique trajectories for charged particles, violating the deterministic nature of electromagnetic interactions.

        4.  **Paradoxical Energy Transfer:** If field lines could intersect, it might imply that magnetic flux is not continuously distributed.
