### 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

1.  **What is a magnetic field? Explain how magnetic field lines represent it.**
    *   **Model Answer:** A magnetic field is the region around a magnet or a current-carrying conductor within which its magnetic influence can be detected. Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines drawn to represent the direction and strength of the magnetic field. The direction of the magnetic field at any point is taken to be the direction in which the north pole of a compass needle points when placed there. The closeness of these lines indicates the strength of the magnetic field; where they are crowded, the field is strong, and where they are far apart, the field is weak.

2.  **Describe an activity to demonstrate the magnetic field produced by a straight current-carrying conductor.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Take a straight thick copper wire and connect it in series with a battery and a plug key. Place this wire parallel to a compass needle. When the key is plugged in and current flows through the wire, the compass needle deflects, indicating the presence of a magnetic field around the wire. Reversing the direction of current reverses the deflection of the compass needle, showing that the direction of the magnetic field also reverses.

3.  **State any two important properties of magnetic field lines.**
    *   **Model Answer:**
        *   Magnetic field lines emerge from the North pole of a magnet and merge into the South pole. Inside the magnet, they run from the South pole to the North pole, forming closed loops.
        *   No two magnetic field lines intersect each other. If they did, it would imply that a compass needle would point in two different directions at the point of intersection, which is physically impossible.

4.  **What happens to the compass needle when it is brought near a current-carrying wire? Explain the observation.**
    *   **Model Answer:** When a compass needle is brought near a current-carrying wire, it deflects. This observation demonstrates that an electric current flowing through a conductor produces a magnetic effect, creating a magnetic field around it. The compass needle, being a small magnet, experiences a force in this magnetic field and aligns itself along the field lines.

5.  **How can you determine the direction of the magnetic field produced by a straight current-carrying conductor?**
    *   **Model Answer:** The direction of the magnetic field produced by a straight current-carrying conductor can be determined using the Right-Hand Rule. If you grasp the conductor with your right hand such that your thumb points in the direction of the electric current, then your fingers will curl around the conductor in the direction of the magnetic field lines. Alternatively, by observing the deflection of a compass needle placed near the wire and reversing the current direction, one can deduce the direction of the magnetic field.

6.  **What do you understand by the term 'magnetic field strength'? How is it represented using magnetic field lines?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Magnetic field strength refers to the intensity of the magnetic field at a particular point. It is a measure of how strong the magnetic force is in that region. Magnetic field strength is represented by the density or closeness of magnetic field lines. Where the field lines are crowded together, the magnetic field is strong, and where they are spread apart, the field is weak.

7.  **Explain why the deflection of the compass needle changes when the direction of current in the wire is reversed.**
    *   **Model Answer:** The deflection of the compass needle changes when the direction of current is reversed because the magnetic field produced by the current-carrying wire is directly dependent on the direction of the current. According to the Right-Hand Rule, reversing the current reverses the direction of the magnetic field lines. The compass needle aligns itself with these field lines, so its deflection reverses accordingly.

8.  **Are magnetic field lines real? Justify your answer.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Magnetic field lines are imaginary or conceptual lines. They are not physically visible. However, they are a very useful tool to visualize and understand the direction and strength of a magnetic field. Their existence is inferred from the behaviour of magnetic materials (like iron filings) and compass needles that align themselves along these lines, proving the presence and direction of the magnetic force.

9.  **What is meant by the "north-seeking pole" and "south-seeking pole" of a magnet?**
    *   **Model Answer:** When a bar magnet is freely suspended, one end points approximately towards the geographic North and the other towards the geographic South. The end pointing towards the geographic North is called the North-seeking pole (or North pole) and the end pointing towards the geographic South is called the South-seeking pole (or South pole). This naming convention helps in understanding the interactions between magnets.

10. **Why is it stated that magnetic field lines are closed curves?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Magnetic field lines are stated to be closed curves because they emerge from the North pole and merge into the South pole outside the magnet. Inside the magnet, they continue from the South pole back to the North pole. This means the lines form a continuous loop, without any beginning or end, making them closed curves.

### 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

1.  **Explain the concept of a magnetic field. Describe an activity to draw magnetic field lines around a bar magnet, and explain the properties of these field lines.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Magnetic Field:** Define magnetic field as the region around a magnet where its influence is detected.
        *   **Activity:** Detail Activity 12.3 from the textbook: placing a bar magnet on paper, using a compass, marking needle positions step-by-step, moving from one pole to the other, and joining points to draw a curve.
        *   **Properties:**
            *   Direction: Emerge from North, merge at South (outside); South to North (inside).
            *   Strength: Indicated by closeness of lines.
            *   No intersection: Explain why (single direction for compass).
            *   Closed curves: Explain their continuous nature.

2.  **Describe the magnetic field produced by a straight current-carrying conductor. How is the direction of this magnetic field related to the direction of the current? Explain using the Right-Hand Rule.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Magnetic Field:** State that a current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it (referencing Activity 12.1 and 12.4).
        *   **Direction of Current:** Explain that the direction of the magnetic field depends on the direction of the current.
        *   **Right-Hand Rule:** Clearly explain the Right-Hand Rule with a diagrammatic description (thumb = current, fingers = field direction).
        *   **Example:** Illustrate with a simple circuit diagram showing current direction and resulting field direction (e.g., current upwards, field counter-clockwise).
        *   **Effect of Reversing Current:** Mention that reversing current reverses the field direction.

3.  **Discuss the magnetic field lines around a bar magnet. Explain what they represent and list their key characteristics.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Representation:** Explain that magnetic field lines represent the magnetic field around a bar magnet, showing its direction and strength.
        *   **Diagram:** Refer to the diagram of field lines around a bar magnet (Fig. 12.4 in NCERT).
        *   **Characteristics:**
            *   Origin and Destination: Emerge from N, merge at S (outside); S to N (inside).
            *   Closed Loops: They form continuous closed curves.
            *   Density = Strength: Field is stronger where lines are crowded.
            *   Non-intersecting: Explain the reason for no intersection.
            *   Direction Convention: North pole of compass defines direction.

4.  **What is an electric current? How does it produce magnetic effects? Explain with reference to Hans Christian Oersted's discovery.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Electric Current:** Define electric current as the flow of electric charge.
        *   **Oersted's Discovery:** Describe Oersted's accidental observation in 1820 where he noticed a compass needle deflecting when placed near a wire carrying an electric current.
        *   **Magnetic Effect:** Explain that this demonstrated that electricity and magnetism are linked, and an electric current produces a magnetic field.
        *   **Mechanism (briefly):** Mention that moving charges (current) create magnetic fields.
        *   **Significance:** Highlight the importance of this discovery in the field of electromagnetism.

5.  **Compare and contrast the magnetic field produced by a straight current-carrying conductor and that produced by a bar magnet.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   **Source:** Straight conductor - electric current flow. Bar magnet - intrinsic magnetic property of material (poles).
        *   **Field Pattern:** Straight conductor - concentric circles around the wire. Bar magnet - closed loops originating from N and ending at S (outside).
        *   **Direction Control:** Straight conductor - direction of field depends on current direction (Right-Hand Rule). Bar magnet - fixed poles and field direction.
        *   **Strength Variation:** Both have varying strength. For a straight wire, strength depends on current and distance. For a bar magnet, strength is maximum near poles.
        *   **Continuity:** Both produce continuous field lines (closed loops).
