## 11.1 Electric Current and Circuit

### 11.1.1 Electric Current

*   **Analogy:** Similar to how flowing water constitutes a water current, the flow of electric charges through a conductor (like a metallic wire) constitutes an **electric current**.
*   **Example:** In a torch, cells (or a battery) provide the flow of charges, causing the bulb to glow.
*   **Circuit:** A **continuous and closed path** of an electric current is called an **electric circuit**.
*   **Switch Function:** A switch in a torch creates a conducting link between the cell and the bulb. When the switch is 'on', the circuit is closed, allowing current to flow and the bulb to glow. When 'off', the circuit is broken, and the current stops.

### 11.1.2 Quantifying Electric Current

*   **Definition:** Electric current is defined as the **amount of electric charge flowing through a particular cross-sectional area of a conductor in unit time**. It is the **rate of flow of electric charges**.
*   **Charge Carriers:** In metallic wires, **electrons** are the charge carriers.
*   **Conventional Current:** Historically, before the discovery of electrons, electric current was considered the flow of positive charges. By convention, the **direction of electric current is taken as opposite to the direction of flow of electrons** (which are negative charges).

### 11.1.3 Formula for Electric Current

*   If a net charge $Q$ flows across any cross-section of a conductor in time $t$, then the current $I$ is given by:
    $$I = \frac{Q}{t} \quad (11.1)$$

### 11.1.4 Units of Electric Current

*   **SI Unit of Charge:** Coulomb (C).
    *   1 Coulomb (C) is approximately the charge contained in $6 \times 10^{18}$ electrons.
    *   Charge of an electron ($e$) = $-1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C.
*   **SI Unit of Electric Current:** Ampere (A), named after André-Marie Ampère.
    *   **Definition:** One Ampere (A) is constituted by the flow of one Coulomb (C) of charge per second (1 s).
    *   $$1 \text{ A} = 1 \frac{\text{C}}{\text{s}}$$
*   **Sub-units:**
    *   Milliampere (mA): $1 \text{ mA} = 10^{-3} \text{ A}$
    *   Microampere ($\mu$A): $1 \mu\text{A} = 10^{-6} \text{ A}$

### 11.1.5 Measuring Electric Current

*   **Instrument:** An **ammeter** is used to measure electric current in a circuit.
*   **Connection:** An ammeter is always connected in **series** in the circuit through which the current is to be measured. This ensures that the entire current flows through the ammeter.
*   **Direction of Flow:** In a circuit, conventional current flows from the positive terminal of the cell/battery to the negative terminal.

### Solved Example 11.1

**Problem:** A current of 0.5 A is drawn by a filament of an electric bulb for 10 minutes. Find the amount of electric charge that flows through the circuit.

**Solution:**
*   Given: Current ($I$) = 0.5 A
*   Given: Time ($t$) = 10 minutes
*   Convert time to seconds: $t = 10 \text{ min} \times 60 \text{ s/min} = 600 \text{ s}$
*   Using the formula $Q = I \times t$:
    $Q = 0.5 \text{ A} \times 600 \text{ s}$
    $Q = 300 \text{ C}$

**Answer:** The amount of electric charge that flows through the circuit is 300 C.

## 11.2 Electric Potential and Potential Difference

### 11.2.1 What makes charges flow?

*   **Analogy:** Water flows in a tube only if there is a **pressure difference** between its ends (e.g., one end higher than the other). Similarly, electric charges do not flow by themselves in a conductor.
*   **Cause of Flow:** Charges (electrons) move only if there is an **electric pressure difference** called **potential difference** along the conductor.
*   **Source of Potential Difference:** This potential difference is produced by a **battery**, which consists of one or more electric cells.
*   **Cell's Role:** Chemical reactions inside a cell generate the potential difference across its terminals, even when no current is drawn. When connected to a circuit, this potential difference sets charges in motion.
*   **Energy Expenditure:** To maintain a current in a circuit, the cell must expend its stored chemical energy.

### 11.2.2 Potential Difference

*   **Definition:** The **potential difference (V)** between two points in an electric circuit carrying current is defined as the **work done (W) to move a unit charge (Q)** from one point to the other.
    $$V = \frac{W}{Q} \quad (11.2)$$

### 11.2.3 Units of Potential Difference

*   **SI Unit:** Volt (V), named after Alessandro Volta.
*   **Definition:** One Volt (V) is the potential difference between two points when 1 Joule (J) of work is done to move a charge of 1 Coulomb (C) from one point to the other.
    $$1 \text{ V} = \frac{1 \text{ J}}{1 \text{ C}} \quad (11.3)$$
    So, $1 \text{ V} = 1 \text{ J C}^{-1}$.

### 11.2.4 Measuring Potential Difference

*   **Instrument:** A **voltmeter** is used to measure potential difference.
*   **Connection:** A voltmeter is always connected in **parallel** across the two points between which the potential difference is to be measured. This allows it to measure the potential difference without significantly affecting the current in the main circuit.

---

### Solved Example 11.2

**Problem:** How much work is done in moving a charge of 2 C across two points having a potential difference 12 V?

**Solution:**
*   Given: Charge ($Q$) = 2 C
*   Given: Potential difference ($V$) = 12 V
*   Using the formula $W = V \times Q$:
    $W = 12 \text{ V} \times 2 \text{ C}$
    $W = 24 \text{ J}$

**Answer:** The work done is 24 J.

## 11.4 Ohm's Law

### 11.4.1 Investigating the Relationship between V and I

**Activity 11.1:** Studying Ohm's Law

*   **Objective:** To find the relationship between the potential difference across a conductor and the current through it.
*   **Setup:**
    *   A nichrome wire (XY) (e.g., 0.5 m long). Nichrome is an alloy suitable for heating elements.
    *   An ammeter (to measure current).
    *   A voltmeter (to measure potential difference across the nichrome wire).
    *   A battery (made of 1.5 V cells).
*   **Procedure:**
    1.  Start with one cell as the power source. Record the ammeter reading ($I$) and voltmeter reading ($V$) across the nichrome wire.
    2.  Increase the number of cells in the battery (e.g., two, three, four cells). For each step, record the new ammeter and voltmeter readings.
    3.  Calculate the ratio $V/I$ for each set of readings.
*   **Observations:**
    *   The $V-I$ graph for the nichrome wire is found to be a **straight line passing through the origin**.
    *   The ratio $V/I$ is approximately constant for all readings.

**Conclusion from Activity:** This demonstrates that the potential difference ($V$) across a conductor is directly proportional to the current ($I$) flowing through it, provided its temperature remains the same.

### 11.4.2 Statement of Ohm's Law

*   **Ohm's Law:** In 1827, Georg Simon Ohm found that for a given metallic conductor at a constant temperature, the potential difference ($V$) across its ends is **directly proportional** to the current ($I$) flowing through it.
    $$V \propto I \quad (11.4)$$
    This can be rewritten as:
    $$V = \text{constant} \times I$$
    $$V = R \times I$$
    or
    $$\frac{V}{I} = R$$

### 11.4.3 Resistance

*   **Definition:** The constant ratio $R$ is called the **resistance** of the conductor.
*   **Meaning:** Resistance is the property of a conductor that opposes the flow of electric current.
*   **Unit of Resistance:** The SI unit of resistance is the **Ohm** ($\Omega$), named after Georg Simon Ohm.
    *   **Definition:** One Ohm ($1 \Omega$) is the resistance of a conductor when a potential difference of 1 Volt (V) across its ends causes a current of 1 Ampere (A) to flow through it.
        $$1 \Omega = \frac{1 \text{ V}}{1 \text{ A}}$$
*   **Formula:** $R = \frac{V}{I}$

### 11.4.4 Factors Affecting Resistance

*   **Nature of Material:** Different materials have different resistances. Metals have low resistance, while insulators have very high resistance. Alloys like nichrome have intermediate resistance and are used in heating devices.
*   **Length of Conductor ($l$):** Resistance is **directly proportional** to the length of the conductor. A longer wire has more resistance.
    $$R \propto l$$
*   **Area of Cross-section ($A$):** Resistance is **inversely proportional** to the area of cross-section. A thicker wire (larger area) has less resistance.
    $$R \propto \frac{1}{A}$$
*   **Temperature:** Resistance of most metallic conductors increases with temperature.

### 11.4.5 Resistivity ($\rho$)

*   **Definition:** Combining the factors of length and area, we can write the resistance of a uniform metallic conductor as:
    $$R = \rho \frac{l}{A} \quad (11.5)$$
    where $\rho$ (Greek letter 'rho') is a proportionality constant called **resistivity**.
*   **Meaning:** Resistivity is an **intrinsic property of the material**. It indicates how strongly the material resists the flow of electricity.
*   **Unit of Resistivity:** Ohm-meter ($\Omega$m).
*   **Material Dependence:** Resistivity depends only on the nature of the material and its temperature. It does not depend on the length or area of the conductor.
*   **Range:**
    *   Conductors (metals) have low resistivity (e.g., silver: $1.60 \times 10^{-8} \Omega$m, copper: $1.72 \times 10^{-8} \Omega$m).
    *   Insulators have very high resistivity.
    *   Alloys have higher resistivity than pure metals.

### Solved Example 11.4

**Problem:** Why is nichrome usually employed for the heating element of an electric heater?

**Solution:**
Nichrome is an alloy of nickel, chromium, manganese, and iron. It is usually employed for the heating element of electric heaters due to the following properties:
1.  **High Resistivity:** Nichrome has a relatively high resistivity compared to other metals. This means it offers significant opposition to the flow of current, leading to the generation of a large amount of heat ($P = I^2 R$).
2.  **High Melting Point:** Nichrome has a high melting point, so it can get very hot without melting.
3.  **Oxidation Resistance:** At high temperatures, nichrome does not oxidize (burn) readily in air. This ensures the longevity of the heating element.

1.  **What determines the resistance of a conductor?**

2.  **In what way is the resistance of a wire related to its length and area of cross-section?**
    *   Resistance is directly proportional to its length ($R \propto l$).

3.  **Why are alloys like nichrome usually preferred over pure metals for making heating elements?**

4.  **What is resistivity? What is its SI unit?**
    *   Resistivity is an intrinsic property of a material that quantifies how strongly it resists electric current. Its SI unit is Ohm-meter ($\Omega$m).
5.  **Consider two wires A and B of equal length and equal area of cross-section. Wire A is made of copper and wire B is made of nichrome. Which wire has greater resistance? Why?**
    *   Wire B (nichrome) will have greater resistance because nichrome has a higher resistivity than copper.
6.  **A wire of length $L$ and resistance $R$ is stretched so that its length is doubled. What will be the new resistance of the wire?**
    *   Let the original length be $L_1 = L$ and original resistance be $R_1 = R$.
    *   Original cross-sectional area be $A_1$.
    *   $R_1 = \rho \frac{L_1}{A_1} \implies R = \rho \frac{L}{A_1}$
    *   The wire is stretched so its length is doubled. New length $L_2 = 2L$.
    *   When a wire is stretched, its volume remains constant.
    *   Volume $V = L_1 A_1 = L_2 A_2$
    *   $L \times A_1 = (2L) \times A_2 \implies A_2 = \frac{A_1}{2}$. The area is halved.
    *   New resistance $R_2 = \rho \frac{L_2}{A_2}$
    *   $R_2 = \rho \frac{2L}{A_1/2} = \rho \frac{2L \times 2}{A_1} = 4 \left(\rho \frac{L}{A_1}\right)$
    *   Since $R = \rho \frac{L}{A_1}$, we have $R_2 = 4R$.
    *   The new resistance will be four times the original resistance.

## 11.5 Electrical Resistivity

*   **Definition:** Electrical resistivity ($\rho$) is an intrinsic property of a material that quantifies its opposition to electric current flow. It is defined by the relation $R = \rho \frac{l}{A}$.
*   **Dependence:** Resistivity depends on the nature of the material and its temperature.
*   **Range of Resistivities:**
    *   **Conductors:** Very low resistivity (e.g., silver, copper, aluminum).
    *   **Semiconductors:** Intermediate resistivity (e.g., silicon, germanium).
    *   **Insulators:** Very high resistivity (e.g., rubber, glass, diamond).

### Solved Example 11.5

**Problem:** A resistor is made of a material with resistivity $\rho$. Its length is $L$ and its cross-sectional area is $A$. If the length of the resistor is increased by pushing it so that its new cross-sectional area is $A/2$, what is the new resistance?

**Solution:**
*   Original resistance $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$.
*   The material is pushed, meaning its volume remains constant.
*   Original Volume $V = L \times A$.
*   New cross-sectional area $A' = A/2$.
*   Let the new length be $L'$.
*   Volume conservation: $V' = L' \times A'$.
*   $L \times A = L' \times (A/2)$
*   $L' = 2L$.
*   The new resistance $R' = \rho \frac{L'}{A'}$.
*   $R' = \rho \frac{2L}{A/2} = \rho \frac{4L}{A} = 4 \left(\rho \frac{L}{A}\right)$
*   Since $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$, we have $R' = 4R$.

**Answer:** The new resistance is $4R$.

## 11.6 Combination of Resistors

Resistors can be connected in a circuit in two main ways:

### 11.6.1 Resistors in Series

*   **Connection:** When resistors are connected end-to-end, so that the same current flows through each resistor.
*   **Diagram:** (Represented using symbols from Table 11.1)
    `Cell --- Switch --- R1 --- R2 --- R3 ---` (Ammeter in series, Voltmeter across each resistor)
*   **Key Properties:**
    *   **Current:** The current is the **same** through all resistors in series. $I = I_1 = I_2 = I_3$.
    *   **Potential Difference:** The total potential difference across the combination is the sum of the potential differences across each individual resistor. $V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3$.
    *   **Equivalent Resistance ($R_{eq}$):** The total resistance of the series combination is the sum of the individual resistances.
        $$R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ... \quad (11.6)$$
*   **Implication:** The equivalent resistance in series is always **greater** than the largest individual resistance.

### 11.6.2 Resistors in Parallel

*   **Connection:** When the ends of resistors are connected to the same two points, so that the current divides among them.
*   **Diagram:** (Represented using symbols from Table 11.1)
    `Cell --- Switch ---` (Point A)
    `            |------- R1 -------|`
    `            |------- R2 -------|` (Point B)
    `            |------- R3 -------|`
    `         (Ammeter measuring total current, Voltmeter across AB)`
*   **Key Properties:**
    *   **Potential Difference:** The potential difference is the **same** across all resistors in parallel. $V = V_1 = V_2 = V_3$.
    *   **Current:** The total current is the sum of the currents flowing through each resistor. $I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3$.
    *   **Equivalent Resistance ($R_{eq}$):** The reciprocal of the equivalent resistance is the sum of the reciprocals of the individual resistances.
        $$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + ... \quad (11.7)$$
*   **Implication:** The equivalent resistance in parallel is always **smaller** than the smallest individual resistance.

### Solved Example 11.6

**Problem:** Determine the current drawn from the battery of a circuit consisting of two 10 $\Omega$ resistors, R1 and R2, joined in parallel to a 12 V battery.

**Solution:**
*   Given: $R_1 = 10 \Omega$, $R_2 = 10 \Omega$, $V = 12$ V.
*   The resistors are in parallel. First, find the equivalent resistance ($R_{eq}$).
*   Using the formula for parallel resistors:
    $$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}$$
    $$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{10 \Omega} + \frac{1}{10 \Omega} = \frac{2}{10 \Omega}$$
    $$R_{eq} = \frac{10 \Omega}{2} = 5 \Omega$$
*   Now, use Ohm's Law ($V = I R_{eq}$) to find the current ($I$) drawn from the battery.
*   $I = \frac{V}{R_{eq}}$
*   $I = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{5 \Omega} = 2.4 \text{ A}$

**Answer:** The current drawn from the battery is 2.4 A.

**Problem:** In the given circuit (Fig. 11.4 - assume this shows R1=10$\Omega$, R2=20$\Omega$, R3=30$\Omega$ in series, and a 12V battery), calculate:
(a) Total resistance of the circuit.
(b) Total current flowing in the circuit.
(c) Potential difference across R1, R2 and R3.

*(Note: The actual Figure 11.4 needs to be consulted from the textbook. For this example, I am assuming R1, R2, R3 are in series.)*

**Solution:**
**(a) Total resistance of the circuit:**
*   Since the resistors $R_1$, $R_2$, and $R_3$ are connected in series:
    $R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3$
    $R_{eq} = 10 \Omega + 20 \Omega + 30 \Omega = 60 \Omega$

**(b) Total current flowing in the circuit:**
*   Using Ohm's Law: $I = \frac{V}{R_{eq}}$
    $I = \frac{12 \text{ V}}{60 \Omega} = 0.2 \text{ A}$

**(c) Potential difference across $R_1$, $R_2$, and $R_3$:**
*   For $R_1$: $V_1 = I \times R_1 = 0.2 \text{ A} \times 10 \Omega = 2 \text{ V}$
*   For $R_2$: $V_2 = I \times R_2 = 0.2 \text{ A} \times 20 \Omega = 4 \text{ V}$
*   For $R_3$: $V_3 = I \times R_3 = 0.2 \text{ A} \times 30 \Omega = 6 \text{ V}$
*   Check: $V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = 2 \text{ V} + 4 \text{ V} + 6 \text{ V} = 12 \text{ V}$, which is equal to the battery voltage.

**Answer:**
(a) Total resistance = 60 $\Omega$.
(b) Total current = 0.2 A.
(c) Potential difference across $R_1$ is 2 V, across $R_2$ is 4 V, and across $R_3$ is 6 V.

## 11.7 Heating Effect of Electric Current

### 11.7.1 Joule Heating

*   **Concept:** When an electric current flows through a conductor, it encounters resistance. This resistance causes the conductor to heat up. This phenomenon is known as the **heating effect of electric current** or **Joule heating**.
*   **Explanation:** The electrons carrying the current collide with the atoms of the conductor. These collisions transfer kinetic energy from the electrons to the atoms, increasing their vibrational energy. This increased vibrational energy manifests as heat.
*   **Work Done and Heat:**
    *   Consider a resistor of resistance $R$ carrying a current $I$ for time $t$.
    *   The potential difference across the resistor is $V = IR$.
    *   Work done ($W$) in time $t$ is $W = V \times I \times t$.
    *   Substituting $V = IR$: $W = (IR) \times I \times t = I^2 R t$.
    *   This work done by the current is converted into heat ($H$).
    *   Therefore, the heat produced is:
        $$H = I^2 R t \quad (11.8)$$
        This is **Joule's Law of Heating**.

### 11.7.2 Applications of Heating Effect

The heating effect of electric current has several useful applications:

1.  **Electric Heater:** Used for space heating.
2.  **Electric Iron:** Used for pressing clothes.
3.  **Electric Kettle:** Used for boiling water.
4.  **Electric Toaster:** Used for toasting bread.
5.  **Electric Fuse:** A safety device that protects electrical appliances from damage due to overcurrent.
    *   **How it works:** A fuse wire is made of a material with a low melting point (e.g., an alloy of lead and tin). It is connected in series with the appliance. If a large current flows (due to a fault), the fuse wire heats up rapidly due to its resistance and melts, breaking the circuit and stopping the flow of current. This prevents damage to the appliance.

### 11.7.3 Choice of Material for Heating Elements

*   Heating elements (like in electric irons, heaters) are made of alloys such as **nichrome**.
*   **Reasons:**
    *   **High Resistivity:** To produce significant heat ($H = I^2Rt$).
    *   **High Melting Point:** To withstand high temperatures without melting.
    *   **Resistance to Oxidation:** Does not easily get oxidized (burn) at high temperatures.

### Solved Example 11.8

**Problem:** An electric iron consumes energy at a rate of 840 W when heating is at the main rate of 1680 W, both rates being steady. If the supply voltage is 220 V, calculate the current drawn in the two cases.

**Solution:**
*   Power ($P$) is related to voltage ($V$) and current ($I$) by the formula $P = VI$.
*   **Case 1: Heating at the main rate (840 W)**
    *   $P_1 = 840$ W
    *   $V = 220$ V
    *   $I_1 = \frac{P_1}{V} = \frac{840 \text{ W}}{220 \text{ V}} = \frac{84}{22} \text{ A} = \frac{42}{11} \text{ A} \approx 3.82 \text{ A}$
*   **Case 2: Heating at the higher rate (1680 W)**
    *   $P_2 = 1680$ W
    *   $V = 220$ V
    *   $I_2 = \frac{P_2}{V} = \frac{1680 \text{ W}}{220 \text{ V}} = \frac{168}{22} \text{ A} = \frac{84}{11} \text{ A} \approx 7.64 \text{ A}$

**Answer:** The current drawn is approximately 3.82 A at the main rate and approximately 7.64 A at the higher rate.

### Solved Example 11.9

**Problem:** A piece of wire of resistance 20 $\Omega$ is bent into the shape of a square. The opposite corners of the square are connected to a battery of 6 V. Calculate the current flowing in the circuit.

**Solution:**
*   Let the resistance of the wire be $R = 20 \Omega$.
*   When bent into a square, the wire is divided into four equal parts. Each part has a resistance of $20 \Omega / 4 = 5 \Omega$.
*   The battery is connected across opposite corners. This means the current splits and flows through two paths, each consisting of two series-connected segments of the wire.
*   Path 1: Two segments in series $\implies$ Resistance $R_{path1} = 5 \Omega + 5 \Omega = 10 \Omega$.
*   Path 2: The other two segments in series $\implies$ Resistance $R_{path2} = 5 \Omega + 5 \Omega = 10 \Omega$.
*   These two paths are connected in parallel across the battery.
*   The equivalent resistance of the circuit ($R_{eq}$) is the parallel combination of $R_{path1}$ and $R_{path2}$.
    $$\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_{path1}} + \frac{1}{R_{path2}} = \frac{1}{10 \Omega} + \frac{1}{10 \Omega} = \frac{2}{10 \Omega}$$
    $$R_{eq} = \frac{10 \Omega}{2} = 5 \Omega$$
*   The battery voltage is $V = 6$ V.
*   Using Ohm's Law to find the total current flowing from the battery:
    $I = \frac{V}{R_{eq}} = \frac{6 \text{ V}}{5 \Omega} = 1.2 \text{ A}$.

**Answer:** The current flowing in the circuit is 1.2 A.

    *   Given: $P = 100$ W, $V = 220$ V.
    *   We know $P = \frac{V^2}{R}$.
    *   $R = \frac{V^2}{P} = \frac{(220 \text{ V})^2}{100 \text{ W}} = \frac{48400 \text{ V}^2}{100 \text{ W}} = 484 \Omega$.

        $E = P \times t = 100 \text{ W} \times (5 \times 3600 \text{ s})$
        $E = 100 \times 18000 \text{ J} = 1,800,000 \text{ J} = 1.8 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$.

## Chapter Summary Points

*   **Electric Current:** Rate of flow of electric charge ($I = Q/t$). Unit: Ampere (A).
*   **Conventional Current:** Direction opposite to electron flow.
*   **Electric Potential Difference:** Work done per unit charge ($V = W/Q$). Unit: Volt (V).
*   **Battery/Cell:** Device that maintains potential difference.
*   **Circuit Diagram:** Uses standard symbols to represent components.
*   **Ohm's Law:** $V = IR$ (at constant temperature).
*   **Resistance ($R$):** Opposition to current flow. Unit: Ohm ($\Omega$).
*   **Factors Affecting Resistance:** Length ($l$), Area ($A$), Material (resistivity $\rho$), Temperature. $R = \rho \frac{l}{A}$.
*   **Resistivity ($\rho$):** Intrinsic property of material. Unit: Ohm-meter ($\Omega$m).
*   **Resistors in Series:** $R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + ...$ (Current same, PD adds).
*   **Resistors in Parallel:** $\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + ...$ (PD same, current adds).
*   **Heating Effect of Current (Joule's Law):** $H = I^2Rt$.
*   **Applications:** Fuses, heating elements, electric irons, etc.
