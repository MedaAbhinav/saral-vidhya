### 2. Short Answer Questions (2-3 Marks)

1.  **What is the difference between asexual and sexual reproduction with respect to the creation of variations?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Asexual reproduction produces offspring that are genetically identical or very similar to the parent, with variations arising mainly from minor errors in DNA copying. Sexual reproduction involves the combination of genetic material from two parents, leading to a much greater diversity of offspring and the generation of significant variations.

2.  **Explain the terms 'dominant trait' and 'recessive trait' with an example.**
    *   **Model Answer:** A dominant trait is one that expresses itself in the F1 generation when it is crossed with a contrasting recessive trait. A recessive trait is one that does not express itself in the F1 generation but appears in the F2 generation. For example, in pea plants, tallness (T) is dominant over shortness (t). When a pure tall plant (TT) is crossed with a pure short plant (tt), all F1 offspring are tall (Tt). The shortness trait is recessive.

3.  **What is the role of DNA in heredity?**
    *   **Model Answer:** DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) carries the genetic information in the form of genes. These genes are passed from parents to offspring during reproduction. DNA dictates the traits and characteristics of an individual, ensuring that these are inherited across generations. Changes in DNA can lead to variations.

4.  **How does the environment influence the survival of variations within a species?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Environmental factors act as selective pressures. Variations that provide an advantage to an organism in a particular environment increase its chances of survival and reproduction (e.g., bacteria resistant to heat surviving a heatwave). Conversely, unfavorable variations can lead to decreased survival rates, thus promoting the selection of advantageous traits over time.

5.  **What was Gregor Mendel's significant contribution to the study of heredity?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Gregor Mendel's significant contribution was his systematic approach using mathematical analysis to study the inheritance of traits in pea plants. He formulated the fundamental laws of inheritance, proposing the existence of discrete "factors" (genes) that are passed down from parents, and explaining the concepts of dominant and recessive traits.

6.  **Define genotype and phenotype. Give an example.**
    *   **Model Answer:** Genotype refers to the genetic makeup of an organism, i.e., the combination of alleles it possesses for a particular trait (e.g., TT, Tt, tt for height). Phenotype refers to the observable physical characteristics of an organism, which are the result of its genotype and environmental influences (e.g., tallness or shortness of a plant). For example, both TT and Tt genotypes result in the tall phenotype.

7.  **Explain why the F1 generation in Mendel's tall and short pea plant cross showed only one parental trait.**
    *   **Model Answer:** In Mendel's cross between a pure tall plant (TT) and a pure short plant (tt), the F1 generation inherits one allele for tallness (T) from one parent and one allele for shortness (t) from the other, resulting in the genotype Tt. Since tallness (T) is a dominant trait, it masks the expression of the recessive shortness trait (t), leading to all plants in the F1 generation appearing tall.

8.  **What is the significance of DNA copying accuracy in reproduction?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Accurate DNA copying ensures that genetic information is faithfully transmitted from one generation to the next, maintaining the basic body design and characteristics of a species. Minor inaccuracies, however, can lead to small variations, which are crucial for adaptation and evolution.

9.  **How are traits inherited from parents to offspring?**
    *   **Model Answer:** Traits are inherited through genes, which are segments of DNA. In sexually reproducing organisms, offspring receive a set of genes from each parent. These genes carry specific instructions for the development of various traits. The combination of these inherited genes determines the characteristics of the offspring.

10. **Explain the concept of "selection of variants" by the environment.**
    *   **Model Answer:** The environment acts as a filter, favoring the survival and reproduction of individuals with traits that are advantageous in that specific environment. For instance, in a hot climate, individuals with variations that allow them to tolerate higher temperatures are more likely to survive and pass on those traits. This process is known as selection of variants.

### 3. Long Answer Questions (5 Marks)

1.  **Describe Mendel's experiment on the inheritance of a single trait (monohybrid cross) in pea plants. Explain the concepts of dominant and recessive traits and the genotypic and phenotypic ratios observed in the F1 and F2 generations.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Introduction: Mendel's work, choice of pea plants.
        *   Experiment Description: Crossing pure tall (TT) and pure short (tt) plants.
        *   F1 Generation: All plants are tall (Tt). Explain dominance of tallness (T).
        *   F2 Generation: Self-pollination of F1 plants. Observe tall and short plants.
        *   Ratios: Phenotypic ratio (3 Tall: 1 Short) and Genotypic ratio (1 TT: 2 Tt: 1 tt).
        *   Explanation of Dominant and Recessive: Define and link to alleles.

2.  **Explain the mechanism of inheritance of traits. How do parents pass on genetic material to their offspring? Discuss the role of genes and alleles.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Introduction: Inheritance as the passing of traits.
        *   Contribution from Parents: Equal contribution of genetic material from father and mother.
        *   Chromosomes and Genes: Genes are segments of DNA located on chromosomes.
        *   Alleles: Different versions of a gene (e.g., T for tallness, t for shortness).
        *   Inheritance Pattern: Offspring receive one allele for each gene from each parent.
        *   Genotype and Phenotype: How the combination of alleles (genotype) determines the observable trait (phenotype).

3.  **What are variations? Explain how variations are created during reproduction and why they are important for the survival of species.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Definition of Variations: Subtle differences among individuals of the same species.
        *   Creation of Variations:
            *   Asexual Reproduction: Minor DNA copying errors.
            *   Sexual Reproduction: Combination of parental genes, crossing over.
        *   Importance for Survival:
            *   Adaptation to changing environments.
            *   Survival advantage (e.g., disease resistance, tolerance to environmental stress).
            *   Basis for evolution.

4.  **Explain Mendel's Law of Segregation and Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment. (While the text directly explains segregation and hints at independent assortment with dihybrid crosses, you can frame the question assuming a broader understanding of Mendel's laws based on the chapter's context).**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Introduction to Mendel's Laws.
        *   Law of Segregation:
            *   Explanation: Each individual possesses two alleles for a trait, and these alleles separate (segregate) during gamete formation, with only one allele passed to each gamete.
            *   Experimental evidence (Monohybrid cross F1 to F2).
        *   Law of Independent Assortment:
            *   Explanation: When two or more traits are inherited, the alleles for one trait segregate independently of the alleles for other traits.
            *   (Mention that this is observed in dihybrid crosses, even if not fully detailed in the provided text).

5.  **Describe the process of inheritance of traits using an example like earlobe attachment. Discuss how this trait can be linked to parental inheritance and suggest a possible rule for its inheritance.**
    *   **Outline Answer:**
        *   Observation: Free vs. attached earlobes in the class (Activity 8.1).
        *   Inheritance Pattern: Child's earlobe type often resembles one or both parents.
        *   Genetic Basis: Earlobes are controlled by genes.
        *   Possible Rule Suggestion:
            *   Hypothesize that one type (e.g., free) is dominant and the other (e.g., attached) is recessive.
            *   Explain how parents' genotypes could lead to different offspring phenotypes (e.g., if both parents have free earlobes but carry the allele for attached earlobes, some offspring might have attached earlobes).

    *   **Hint:** Think about how a genetic variation could lead to such differential severity and how the environment (presence of the virus) acts as a selective force.
