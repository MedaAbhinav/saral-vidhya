This chapter explores **Heredity**, focusing on how traits are passed from parents to offspring and how variations arise. While reproduction ensures similar body design, subtle differences accumulate over generations, especially with sexual reproduction. These variations, though minor, are crucial for a species' survival.

The chapter introduces **inherited traits**, which are passed down through genetic material from both parents. Each child receives genetic information from both mother and father, leading to two versions of information for each trait. Gregor Mendel's foundational work in the mid-19th century established the fundamental **rules for the inheritance of traits**. These rules explain how dominant and recessive traits manifest in offspring, influencing observable characteristics like earlobe type. The accumulation and selection of variations are key to evolutionary processes.
